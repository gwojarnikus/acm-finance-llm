# Criterion C1: Integrated Counter-Argumentation

## Definition

Does the report independently identify weaknesses in its own thesis and address them before they arise? Look for "steel-manning" of opposing positions — presenting the strongest version of arguments against the recommendation, then engaging substantively.

This criterion captures the "Critic effect": even in single-shot systems (B1, R1), the final text may bear traces of dialectical reasoning. In AKM, the Critic agent is supposed to force this. Hypothesis: AKM > B1 on C1 if the multi-agent architecture works.

## Functional assessment approach

This criterion is part of dimension C (Multi-Agent Dynamics), but is assessed **functionally** from the final product, not from the dialogue logs. This allows comparable scoring across AKM, B1, and R1 (none of which have dialogue logs except AKM).

For AKM, a separate process-mode prompt exists (`judge-rubric-process-template.md`) that scores C1 from the dialogue trace directly. The two scores can be triangulated for "faithfulness" — does the product reflect the process?

## Behavioral anchors (from BARS pilot v1)

- **Level 2 (poor):** One-sided narrative; no counter-arguments; no Risks/Limitations section.
- **Level 5 (average):** Single "limitations" mentioned at end without analysis. Counter-arguments listed but not engaged.
- **Level 8 (good):** Active "However", "Counter to", "Critics may argue" built into narrative + Risks/Limitations section that actively rebuts charges. Observed in R1 (e.g., S3-R1-C line 35: `The crucial counterargument is that UBI might still be worth it if it dramatically reduced poverty, simplified administration and restored trust in the welfare state. Those benefits are not imaginary` — explicit steel-manning).
- **Level 10 (excellent):** Steel-manning of opposing positions + rebuttal after acknowledging their strength. Observed in R1 (e.g., S3-R1-C's entire framing around UBI — best argument for UBI named and then weakened by fiscal arithmetic).

## Scoring method

**Type:** marker search + structural assessment.

**Procedure:**

1. **Marker scan.** Look for indicators of counter-argumentation:
   - "However", "Yet", "On the other hand", "But"
   - "Critics may argue", "One might object", "A common counter-argument is"
   - "Risk", "Limitation", "Drawback", "Caveat"
   - "Steel-man", "the best argument for", "the strongest case for the opposite"
2. **Density check.** Are these markers concentrated in one section (acceptable) or distributed throughout (better)?
3. **Substantive engagement.** When counter-arguments are raised, is there substantive engagement (extended discussion, evidence-based rebuttal) or token acknowledgment ("this concern is valid but...")?
4. **Risks section structural check.** Does the report have an explicit Risks / Limitations / Caveats section as a separate rhetorical block?
5. **Steel-man test.** Does the report present opposing positions in their strongest form, or in a strawman version that's easy to defeat?

Score guidance:
- Steel-manning + distributed counter-arguments + Risks section → 9–10
- Distributed counter-arguments + Risks section → 7–8
- Risks section present, counter-arguments mostly there → 5–6
- Risks listed but no real engagement → 3–4
- One-sided narrative → 0–2

## Research hypothesis context

A core hypothesis of the AKM project: the Critic agent forces the Analyst to address counter-arguments. The product-level test of this hypothesis is comparing C1 scores across AKM vs B1 (where B1 has no critic). If AKM > B1 systematically on C1, the multi-agent architecture is doing its job.

## Asymmetry per response type

- **AKM:** the Critic agent is explicitly tasked with adversarial pressure. Expect higher C1.
- **B1:** single-shot model, no critic. Expect lower C1 unless the underlying model has strong "consider counter-arguments" tendencies.
- **R1:** Deep Research training likely includes counter-argumentation. Expect high C1 baseline.
