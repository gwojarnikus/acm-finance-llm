# Criterion B5: Scenario Modeling

## Definition

Does the report present a range of scenarios (base / pessimistic / optimistic, or equivalent) with probabilities, triggers, or differentiating conditions? Risk management in financial analysis requires probabilistic rather than deterministic thinking.

## Behavioral anchors (from BARS pilot v1)

- **Level 2 (poor):** Only 1 scenario (deterministic). The recommendation assumes one future and ignores alternatives.
- **Level 5 (average):** 2 scenarios (e.g., baseline + one alternative), but without probabilities or condition specification. Observed in R1 (e.g., S1-R1-G presents Scenario A "State Budget Only" vs Scenario B "Consolidated General Government").
- **Level 8 (good):** 3 scenarios with explicit conditions/triggers. Hypothetical for most R1; rarely observed in practice.
- **Level 10 (excellent):** 3+ scenarios with probabilities AND transition triggers between scenarios. Aspirational.

## Scoring method

**Type:** structural count.

**Procedure:**

1. **Scenario inventory.** Identify named or clearly delimited scenarios in the report. A "scenario" is a distinct future path (or analytical case) with its own assumptions and conclusions.
2. **Differentiation check.** Are the scenarios genuinely different (different assumptions → different conclusions), or are they just rhetorical framing?
3. **Probability/condition check.** Does the report assign probabilities to scenarios, or specify conditions under which each holds?
4. **Trigger identification.** Are transitions between scenarios specified (e.g., "if inflation exceeds 6%, we move from base to pessimistic")?

Score guidance:
- 3+ scenarios with probabilities and triggers → 9–10
- 3 scenarios with conditions but no probabilities → 7–8
- 2 distinct scenarios with conditions → 5–6
- 2 scenarios mentioned but not differentiated → 3–4
- 1 scenario only → 0–2

## Important caveat

The case prompts do NOT explicitly require scenario modeling. A report that gives a sharp, well-reasoned single-path analysis is not methodologically flawed — but it scores lower on B5. Use this signal in the context of overall report quality, not as a disqualifier. A low B5 with high B2 (causal chain) and B4 (second-order) indicates a deterministic but thorough analyst.

## Asymmetry per response type

- Apply uniformly. None of the prompt types mandate scenarios.
