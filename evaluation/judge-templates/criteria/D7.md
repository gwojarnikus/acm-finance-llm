# Criterion D7: Information Density (Linguistic)

## Definition

Ratio of substantive content to word count — conciseness, absence of filler. Professional finance reports must be tight: every paragraph earns its place.

D7 differs from C4 (Insight Density) in granularity:
- **C4** measures unique insights per 1000 words (analytical content).
- **D7** measures linguistic conciseness — sentence-level economy, absence of filler words and redundancy.

A report can score high on C4 (many insights) but low on D7 (each insight padded with verbose academic prose), or vice versa.

## Behavioral anchors (from BARS pilot v1)

- **Level 2 (poor):** "Word salad"; academic verbose style with 30% filler words; "It is important to note that...".
- **Level 5 (average):** Mix; some paragraphs tight, others verbose. Observed in R1 (e.g., S2-R1-G line 23: `This stagflation-adjacent environment systematically invalidates many of the core assumptions underpinning modern portfolio theory, particularly the historically reliable negative correlation...` — verbose academic style example).
- **Level 8 (good):** Every paragraph delivers content; minimal filler. Observed in R1 (e.g., S1-R1-C line 49: short, very dense paragraph with 4 datapoints + conclusion + citation).
- **Level 10 (excellent):** Condensed, executive-grade. Aspirational; rare in any pilot R1.

## Scoring method

**Type:** subjective judgment with concrete checks.

**Procedure:**

1. **Filler word scan.** Look for filler patterns that add no information:
   - "It is important to note that"
   - "In conclusion", "To summarize", "It should be mentioned that"
   - "This analysis demonstrates", "This research reveals"
   - "Furthermore, in addition to the above"
   - Repetitive transitional phrases at every paragraph start
2. **Redundancy check.** Are any paragraphs essentially restating the previous? Could they be deleted without loss?
3. **Sentence length distribution.** Excessive long sentences with subordinate clauses often signal padding. Short, declarative sentences usually carry more information per word.
4. **Word/insight ratio.** A rough heuristic: under 200 words per unique substantive insight is dense; over 400 is padded.
5. **Comparison with R1-C baseline.** R1-C reports tend to be dense (5600w / ~50 insights ≈ 112 w/insight). R1-G tend to be more verbose (3600w / ~30 insights ≈ 120 w/insight, but more academic padding per insight). Use as rough calibration.

Score guidance:
- Minimal filler + tight paragraphs + executive-grade structure → 8–10
- Mostly tight, some filler → 6–7
- Mixed density → 4–6
- Visible filler throughout → 2–4
- Word salad → 0–2

## Note on BERTScore/ROUGE rejection

Earlier proposals to use BERTScore or ROUGE for D7 were rejected (decision 2026-05-16; Stage 5 was removed from the workflow). Reason: those metrics measure similarity, not density. D7 is evaluated by LLM-judge subjective assessment with the explicit anti-verbosity warning from grounding spec §5.1.

## Verbosity bias self-check (from grounding spec §5.1)

Reminder: you have a measurable tendency to favor longer responses. For D7 specifically: a verbose report should NOT score higher on D7 just because it has more words. A tighter, shorter report on the same topic should score higher. Penalize length without substance.

## Asymmetry per response type

- **AKM:** Moderator role redacts the final report — if functioning, expect higher D7.
- **B1:** single-shot; D7 depends on base model style.
- **R1:** observed pilot — R1-G more verbose than R1-C systematically. Reflect in scoring (do not give R1-G credit for length).
