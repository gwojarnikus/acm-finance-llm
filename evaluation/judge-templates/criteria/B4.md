# Criterion B4: Second-Order Thinking

## Definition

Does the report consider indirect, second-order consequences of recommended actions? Examples: competitor reactions, feedback loops, behavioral responses by economic agents, regulatory countermeasures, market repricing, vertical integration incentives.

This distinguishes expert analysis from amateur: amateurs see "X causes Y"; experts also see "Y triggers Z which feeds back into X". In financial-economic policy, ignoring second-order effects often leads to recommendations that fail in practice even when arithmetically sound at first order.

## Behavioral anchors (from BARS pilot v1)

- **Level 2 (poor):** Only direct first-order consequences considered. E.g., "tax X reduces revenue Y" with no consideration of behavioral response.
- **Level 5 (average):** 1–2 second-order effects mentioned but superficially ("competitors may respond").
- **Level 8 (good):** ≥3 second-order effects with explicit mechanism analysis. Observed in R1 (e.g., S1-R1-G lines 102–104 on cumulative turnover tax: `vertical integration incentive + import bias + monopolization` — 3 second-order effects with explicit mechanism).
- **Level 10 (excellent):** Second- AND third-order effects with explicit "What could go wrong?" reasoning + scenario chains. Observed in R1 (e.g., S3-R1-C line 53: `an ambitious UBI would first be launched as a symbol of social renewal and then later be cut, taxed back, or hollowed out under debt pressure, producing precisely the insecurity and policy volatility it was supposed to reduce` — full third-order chain: intervention → backlash → reversal → worse outcome than status quo).

## Scoring method

**Type:** marker search + chain enumeration.

**Procedure:**

1. **First-order claim inventory.** Identify the main causal claims in the report ("X causes Y").
2. **Second-order extension search.** For each first-order claim, does the report extend the analysis with "and this in turn would..." or equivalent?
3. **Marker search.** Look for indicator phrases:
   - "would trigger", "in response", "second-order", "cascade", "feedback loop"
   - "this creates an incentive for", "agents would respond by"
   - "would push toward", "would shift the equilibrium"
   - "ripple effect", "downstream consequence"
4. **Count distinct second-order chains.** A "chain" is a complete causal sequence going beyond the direct effect of the policy.
5. **Third-order check.** Are there chains going beyond two steps? E.g., "policy → behavior change → market shift → political backlash → policy reversal."

Score guidance:
- ≥3 distinct second-order chains + at least one third-order chain → 9–10
- ≥3 distinct second-order chains, no third-order → 7–8
- 1–2 second-order chains → 5–6
- Only second-order mentioned superficially → 3–4
- No second-order analysis → 0–2

## Asymmetry per response type

- Apply uniformly. The case prompts all involve policy / strategy where second-order effects are core to good analysis.
