# Universal Turnover Tax as a Replacement for PIT and CIT in Poland

## Problem Diagnosis

The core challenge is that replacing Poland’s PIT and CIT with one universal tax on gross turnover can be made arithmetically budget-neutral only at a rate that is likely to overburden low-margin sectors, distort supply chains, and run into serious EU-law design constraints. citeturn29view0turn24view0turn25view0turn34search4turn37search0

## In-Depth Analysis

### Revenue-Neutral Rate

Any serious estimate has to begin by rejecting the most obvious but misleading denominator: the **central state budget’s** PIT and CIT entries. Since 2025 was the first year of Poland’s new local-government financing rules, the Ministry of Finance itself warns that state-budget PIT collapsed mainly because PIT-based revenue was redirected to local governments. The state budget recorded only **PLN 28.8 billion** of PIT in 2025, but the **whole public-finance sector** still collected **PLN 202.8 billion** of PIT and **PLN 91.8 billion** of CIT. A reform that truly replaces PIT and CIT in the Polish tax system must therefore replace about **PLN 294.6 billion**, not the post-reform central-budget subtotal of **PLN 92.5 billion**. Put differently, roughly **68.6%** of 2025 PIT+CIT receipts sat outside the state budget proper, mostly in local governments. Any lower benchmark would understate the fiscal target by design. (Ministry of Finance, 2026a). citeturn29view0

The second step is to choose a workable turnover base. Because a turnover tax is most coherently understood as a levy on **gross business receipts from sales**, the cleanest official proxy is enterprise net sales rather than taxable income. Statistics Poland reports that enterprises keeping accounting books and employing **10 or more workers** generated **PLN 5,995.6 billion** of net sales in 2025. That is broad, but not universal: it omits micro-enterprises and excludes some institutional sectors, especially banks and insurers, for which turnover concepts are legally and economically awkward. To broaden the base, one can combine the 2025 sales figure for the 10+ segment with Statistics Poland’s latest micro-enterprise revenue data and with the very stable sales-to-total-revenue ratio observed in the 10+ enterprise data, which was **96.6%** in 2024 and **96.5%** in 2025. Using those official aggregates produces a 2025 all-enterprise turnover proxy in the neighbourhood of **PLN 8.1–8.4 trillion**. On that base, replacing **PLN 294.6 billion** of PIT and CIT revenue implies a flat turnover-tax rate of roughly **3.5% to 3.65%**. A practical point estimate is therefore **about 3.6%**. This estimate assumes no exemptions, no behavioural change, and ordinary compliance rather than a collapse in the base. (Ministry of Finance, 2026a; Statistics Poland, 2026a; Statistics Poland, 2025a; Statistics Poland, 2025b; Statistics Poland, 2025c). citeturn29view0turn24view0turn28view0turn25view0turn10view1

That **3.6%** figure is fiscally plausible but economically revealing. It is not a “small nuisance levy.” It is several times larger than the **0.8%** and **1.4%** bands of Poland’s retail-sector tax litigated before the EU courts, and unlike that sectoral levy it would apply across the whole production and distribution chain. If policymakers narrowed the base through exemptions for micro firms, agriculture, finance, exports, or public bodies, the revenue-neutral rate would rise quickly. For example, if the tax were imposed only on the 2025 net sales of the currently observed **10+ worker** enterprise population, the neutral rate would jump to roughly **4.9%**. So the scheme has a brutal arithmetic: either the base is truly universal and the rate is around **3.6%**, or the rate climbs into a range that becomes even harder to absorb commercially. (Statistics Poland, 2026a; Statistics Poland, 2025a; Ministry of Finance, 2026a; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2021a). citeturn24view0turn28view0turn29view0turn6search2

### Sectoral, Distributional, and Macroeconomic Effects

The fundamental economic difference between an income tax and a turnover tax is not administrative but conceptual. PIT and CIT tax **income** after recognized deductions and therefore track, however imperfectly, a taxpayer’s economic surplus. A turnover tax taxes **gross receipts**, whether the transaction is barely profitable, very profitable, or outright loss-making. That reshuffles liabilities across sectors in a highly asymmetric way. At a **3.6%** turnover rate, the implicit tax burden relative to profits equals about **72% of profit** when the pre-tax margin is **5%**, **120% of profit** when the margin is **3%**, and **180% of profit** when the margin is **2%**. The IMF’s work on turnover-based presumptive taxes makes exactly this general point: low-margin trade is where effective tax rates rise fastest and where turnover taxation most readily exceeds the burden under a profit-based regime. (IMF, 2023). citeturn36search5

That is why retail trade is the reform’s stress point. Statistics Poland reports that among non-financial enterprises with **50+ workers**, the **net turnover profitability** of **trade and motor-vehicle repair** in 2025 was only **2.5%**. A universal turnover tax at **3.6%** would therefore exceed the average sectoral net margin **before** any attempt to pass the burden forward or backward. In plain English, the tax would be larger than the average net profit currently earned on the same turnover. Listed-company data tell the same story from a different angle. Dino Polska’s 2025 net margin was about **3.5%**, so a 3.6% turnover tax would slightly exceed that benchmark. Biedronka’s **7.9%** EBITDA margin looks more comfortable, but even there the tax would absorb roughly **46% of EBITDA** before taking account of rents, finance costs, reinvestment needs, and competitive price pressure. (Statistics Poland, 2026b; Dino Polska S.A., 2026; Jerónimo Martins SGPS, S.A., 2026). citeturn21view3turn19search14turn19search1

The micro-enterprise evidence points in the same direction, though through a different accounting lens. In 2024, trade and motor-vehicle repair firms with up to 9 workers generated **PLN 734.1 billion** of revenue and posted a **91.3%** cost ratio. That left only **8.7%** of revenue as the residual available for profit, owner remuneration, tax, and other claims. A 3.6% turnover levy would consume roughly **41% of that residual**. For a small shop, wholesaler, convenience store, or fuel outlet, that is not a marginal hit; it is a business-model shock. It would favour those able to squeeze suppliers, integrate logistics, or raise prices, while punishing stand-alone retailers and intermediaries who survive on volume rather than fat margins. (Statistics Poland, 2025b). citeturn25view0

The supply-chain effect is even more important than the firm-level effect. A turnover tax is levied on each sale, not on value added created at each stage. Unless it is transformed into something very close to VAT—with invoices, deduction rights, and crediting—tax “pyramids” through the chain. The IMF notes that taxes on turnover are levied on sales to businesses as well as final consumers and are now rarely used as major revenue instruments, precisely because they are generally viewed as a poor substitute for VAT. A simple illustration shows why. If the estimated Polish rate were **3.6%**, and a product moved through four taxed sales valued at **100**, **120**, **135**, and **150**, cumulative tax payments would total **18.18**, equal to **12.1%** of the final pretax selling price, even though the statutory rate is only 3.6%. That hidden multiplication is what makes turnover taxes especially hostile to long value chains, outsourcing, and specialized intermediation. (IMF, 2025; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2024b). citeturn38search6turn33search2

Once PIT is added to the picture, the reform also changes distributional logic. Poland’s PIT currently retains a progressive scale of **12%** and **32%**, plus a **PLN 30,000** tax-free amount. Replacing that with a flat turnover levy does not leave labour and household income untaxed in any economically meaningful sense; it simply taxes them **indirectly** through prices, wages, and supplier margins rather than **directly** through a progressive income schedule. That weakens the link between tax liability and ability to pay. High-margin professional or digital businesses, and households with relatively low consumption out of current income, would likely fare better than mass-market, low-margin sectors where almost every złoty of sales is already spoken for by inventory, logistics, payroll, and rent. (Ministry of Finance, n.d./2025–2026; IMF, 2023). citeturn39search0turn39search2turn39search7turn36search5

There is also a public-finance architecture problem that is easy to miss in abstract tax debates. In 2025, local governments received **PLN 174.1 billion** of PIT-related revenue and **PLN 28.1 billion** from CIT. So a “universal turnover tax” is not merely a central-government tax reform. It would require a new permanent revenue-sharing formula for municipalities, counties, and regions on a scale larger than many individual state-budget chapters. The 2025 financing reform makes this especially acute, because local government is now heavily anchored in PIT-related flows. A turnover tax might therefore preserve aggregate public revenue while simultaneously destabilising subnational finance unless distribution rules were redesigned from scratch. (Ministry of Finance, 2026a). citeturn29view0

### Compatibility with EU Law

From the standpoint of EU primary law, Poland retains broad discretion over the structure of direct taxation. The European Parliament’s current fact sheet notes that the Treaties contain **no explicit general legislative competence** for EU direct taxation, while the Commission emphasises that EU tax policy is aimed at safeguarding the single market and eliminating discriminatory tax barriers. In that sense, abolishing or redesigning PIT and CIT is **not** impossible simply because the EU prefers another model. The legal problem is subtler: whatever Poland does must still be compatible with VAT harmonisation, State-aid disciplines, and the internal-market freedoms. (European Parliament, 2025; European Commission, 2025). citeturn40search1turn40search8

The existing Polish retail tax is the best starting point for that discussion because it disproves the simplistic claim that “turnover taxes are unlawful in the EU.” The CJEU held in 2021 that the Polish retail-sector tax and the Hungarian advertising tax did **not** infringe EU State-aid rules merely because they used progressive turnover bands. The Court accepted that a progressive tax can reflect the legislature’s choice to raise revenue with a greater burden on undertakings with higher turnover. Likewise, in related free-movement case law concerning Hungary’s retail-turnover tax, turnover was treated as a neutral proxy linked to ability to pay, rather than as a disguised nationality criterion. So EU law does not categorically ban turnover-based taxation or progression in turnover-based taxation. (Court of Justice of the European Union, 2021a; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2020). citeturn42search3turn41search4

However, a **universal** turnover tax is legally more delicate than a **sectoral** retail levy. Article 401 of the VAT Directive allows Member States to maintain or introduce taxes and charges that **cannot be characterised as turnover taxes**, provided that collection does not entail frontier formalities. The Court’s case law repeatedly explains that the essential characteristics of VAT are fourfold: general application to transactions concerning goods and services, proportionality to price, collection at each stage of production and distribution, and a deduction mechanism that removes tax paid at previous stages. The Court has also made clear that Article 401 does **not** preclude a national levy if it lacks at least one of those essential characteristics. That means a crude gross-receipts tax with **no deduction right** is not automatically unlawful in the way a second VAT would be. (Council of the European Union, 2006; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2024a; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2018; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2006). citeturn34search4turn34search5turn41search0turn41search2turn33search1turn35search9

But that is not the end of the matter. The more “universal” and neutral policymakers try to make such a tax, the closer they move toward VAT territory. If they want to avoid severe export penalisation, import asymmetry, and cascading through business-to-business chains, they will be pushed toward crediting, invoice chains, or border adjustments. Yet those same features are exactly what make a levy look like a turnover tax in the VAT sense, while Article 401 separately forbids collection methods that create frontier formalities in intra-EU trade. So the legal design space is narrow: a national gross-receipts tax may survive if it remains clearly different from VAT, but the more it is engineered to behave like a modern neutral consumption tax, the more Article 401 risk reappears. That makes a universal turnover tax legally **possible in theory** but **fragile in design**, especially if sold as a clean system-wide replacement for income taxation rather than as a narrow supplementary levy. (Council of the European Union, 2006; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2024a; IMF, 2025). citeturn34search4turn34search5turn41search0turn38search6

The most concrete EU-law obstacle, however, sits elsewhere: **Pillar Two**. Directive (EU) **2022/2523** establishes common rules for the **minimum effective taxation of multinational enterprise groups and large-scale domestic groups** in the Union. OECD Pillar Two guidance makes equally clear that “covered taxes” are fundamentally **income taxes**, not non-income-based levies such as indirect taxes or other gross-flow taxes. A turnover tax therefore cannot, by itself, perform the role of the required minimum effective taxation for in-scope groups. Poland could in principle abolish ordinary CIT for many taxpayers and overlay a domestic minimum top-up tax for Pillar Two groups, but that would mean the new turnover tax does **not** fully replace corporate income taxation in practice. Under current EU law, at least one income-based corporate tax instrument would have to remain or be recreated for the in-scope population. That is a decisive limit on the “full replacement” version of the proposal. (Council of the European Union, 2022; OECD, 2024). citeturn37search0turn37search3turn37search21

The bottom-line legal assessment is therefore differentiated rather than binary. A sectoral or supplementary turnover levy can be compatible with EU law if designed carefully. A **general** turnover tax that coexists with VAT and remains visibly different from it may also be arguable under Article 401. But a **universal** turnover tax that is meant to **fully replace both PIT and CIT** is much harder to reconcile with the current EU framework, not because the EU mandates Poland’s present PIT/CIT design, but because the combination of VAT-harmonisation logic, state-aid/internal-market scrutiny, and especially the Union’s Pillar Two minimum-tax directive makes a clean one-tax substitution legally incomplete. (European Parliament, 2025; Council of the European Union, 2006; Council of the European Union, 2022; Court of Justice of the European Union, 2021a). citeturn40search1turn34search4turn37search0turn42search3

## Recommended Strategy or Key Conclusions

The arithmetic answer is straightforward: under current official Polish revenue data, a universal turnover tax broad enough to replace **all** PIT and CIT revenue would need to be set at roughly **3.6% of gross turnover**, with a plausible sensitivity band of about **3.5% to 3.7%** depending on the exact breadth of the base and treatment of smaller firms. That estimate is materially higher than many policy discussions assume because it must replace **whole public-sector** PIT and CIT receipts, not merely what remains in the post-2025 central state budget. (Ministry of Finance, 2026a; Statistics Poland, 2026a; Statistics Poland, 2025a; Statistics Poland, 2025b; Statistics Poland, 2025c). citeturn29view0turn24view0turn28view0turn25view0turn10view1

The economic verdict is much less favourable than the arithmetic one. At around **3.6%**, the tax would hit low-margin, high-volume sectors hardest. In Polish trade, the official **2.5%** net margin for larger firms sits **below** the estimated neutral tax rate, which means the reform would force large price increases, aggressive supplier squeeze, wage restraint, consolidation, or exit. Because the tax would apply to each stage of the chain, it would also introduce exactly the pyramiding and vertical-integration bias that modern VAT was designed to remove. The result would not be a simpler equivalent of PIT and CIT; it would be a different and more distortionary tax system. (Statistics Poland, 2026b; IMF, 2025; Dino Polska S.A., 2026; Jerónimo Martins SGPS, S.A., 2026). citeturn21view3turn38search6turn19search14turn19search1

The legal verdict is that a universal turnover tax is **not automatically impossible**, but it is **not cleanly available as a full substitute** for Poland’s income taxes under current EU conditions. A carefully framed national turnover levy can coexist with EU law, as the Polish retail-tax litigation demonstrates. Yet once the reform is pushed into a universal, system-replacing form, the legal design becomes unstable: it must avoid becoming a second VAT in Article 401 terms, avoid internal-market discrimination or selectivity, and still preserve an income-based instrument for groups covered by Directive (EU) 2022/2523. In that sense, the proposal can perhaps exist as a **supplement**, a **sectoral levy**, or a **presumptive regime for smaller firms**, but not as a legally neat one-for-one replacement of PIT and CIT across the whole Polish system. (Court of Justice of the European Union, 2021a; Council of the European Union, 2006; Council of the European Union, 2022; OECD, 2024). citeturn42search3turn34search4turn37search0turn37search3

### Bottom Line

The most defensible policy conclusion is therefore conservative rather than revolutionary.

- **Do not pursue a universal turnover tax as a full replacement for PIT and CIT.** The rate required for fiscal neutrality is too high for low-margin sectors and too distortionary for supply chains. citeturn29view0turn21view3turn38search6

- **If simplification is the objective, confine turnover taxation to narrow uses** such as presumptive regimes for micro and small firms, or as a minimum tax backstop, while retaining ordinary income taxation for medium and large businesses. That is much closer to the way turnover taxes are treated in IMF policy design work. citeturn36search5turn38search6

- **Retain an income-based corporate layer for EU minimum-tax compliance and local-government finance stability.** Even a broad turnover levy would need a separate mechanism for Pillar Two groups and a redesigned transfer formula for local governments. citeturn29view0turn37search0turn37search3
