# Asset Allocation Strategy for a Pension Fund Under Persistent Elevated Inflation (4–5% Annually) with Moderate Economic Growth in Developed Economies: A Five-Year Strategic Framework

---

## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The central challenge confronting this pension fund is a structural mismatch between its long-dated, inflation-linked liabilities and a capital market environment in which persistently elevated inflation erodes the real value of conventional fixed-income instruments, compresses price-to-earnings multiples through higher discount rates, introduces compounding uncertainty into actuarial assumptions, and — most dangerously — can produce simultaneous drawdowns in both equities and nominal bonds, eliminating the diversification benefit that these two asset classes normally provide to one another. In a world of moderate but below-trend GDP growth in developed economies, the fund cannot rely on robust nominal earnings expansion to compensate for inflation's corrosive effect on purchasing power. Instead, it must engineer a portfolio in which real asset ownership, inflation-linked income streams, disciplined private markets management, and explicit liability-matching collectively generate a genuine real return — that is, a return above the 4–5% inflation baseline — while preserving sufficient liquidity and diversification to honour benefit obligations across multiple funded status scenarios, including adverse tail-risk conditions.

## 2. Data Verification (Grounding)

Before presenting the strategic framework, it is essential to anchor the analysis in the most current and verified data available, drawn from recent industry studies, regulatory surveys, and market intelligence. Several figures have been corrected or clarified based on verification, and several analytical extensions are explicitly distinguished from directly cited recommendations.

Global pension assets reached a record USD 68.3 trillion in 2025, rising 9.6% year-on-year, with defined contribution (DC) savings driving the majority of growth (Thinking Ahead Institute, 2026, February 9). Among the seven largest pension markets — Australia, Canada, Japan, Netherlands, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and the United States — DC plans now constitute 63% of total assets (Thinking Ahead Institute, 2025). Equity allocations among the P7 have been verified at 48% in 2025, with bonds at 31% and other asset classes at 19%, plus 2% in cash (Thinking Ahead Institute, 2025, February 9 press release). The allocation pattern has changed substantially since 2003: equity allocation has fallen nine percentage points over two decades, while bonds have declined and "other" asset classes — which include private equity, hedge funds, infrastructure, insurance contracts, and real estate — have risen by six percentage points to 19% in 2025 (Thinking Ahead Institute, 2025). It should be noted that the 2025 GPAS report shows an earlier estimate of 45% equities, 33% bonds, and 20% other for 2024e, while the February 2026 press release provides the confirmed 2025 figures of 48% equities, 31% bonds, and 19% other. The precise figures vary slightly across reporting vintages, and both the earlier estimates and the revised figures are directionally consistent with a structural shift away from equities and toward alternatives. The "other" category's composition — private equity, hedge funds, infrastructure, insurance contracts, and real estate — is directly specified in the Thinking Ahead Institute report and is critical context for evaluating the proposed alternatives allocation.

From the NCPERS 2026 Public Retirement Systems Study, which received 149 responses covering approximately 18.1 million members, equities remain the largest component of pension portfolios at 45.2% among 2025 survey respondents, representing a slight decline from 45.5% in 2024 and 52.5% in 2021 (NCPERS, 2026, April 2). Public pensions with fiscal year-end dates in the first half of 2025 reported average one-year investment returns of 10.2% (net of fees) and ten-year returns of 7.5%. Investment strategy trends indicate modest reductions in equity allocations and increased exposure to fixed income as systems rebalance risk amid changing capital market assumptions.

On private equity allocations, S&P Global Market Intelligence data shows that in the first quarter of 2025, 58% of 298 pension funds were overallocated to private equity (174 of 298 funds), with a median target allocation of $306.8 million and a median actual allocation of $330.3 million (Banking Exchange, citing S&P Global Market Intelligence, 2025). By the end of June 2025, approximately 62% of global pension funds exceeded their private equity allocation targets (Augment Market, citing S&P Global Market Intelligence, 2025). The shift from 58% to 62% reflects ongoing overallocation during the first half of 2025; for planning purposes, the directional conclusion that a clear majority of pension funds are above their PE targets is well-supported across both data points. The composition of PE exposure has shifted notably: pension funds are favouring lower-leverage strategies, mid-market buyouts, growth equity, and sector-specialist funds with stronger organic cash flow generation.

On private equity performance metrics, Cambridge Associates recommends reviewing IRRs alongside cash-on-cash multiples, noting that "TVPI and DPI provide valuable insight into cash returns and cannot be 'locked in' or managed like the IRR can" (Cambridge Associates, 2025). PipelineRoad (2025) confirms that DPI has become the most scrutinised metric as LPs want real cash, with vintage 2015–2018 buyout funds showing 0.6–1.0x DPI, and funds significantly behind their vintage benchmark facing tough questions about exit execution. However, IRR and TVPI remain standard benchmarks, and no single metric dominates in LP evaluation practice — Cambridge Associates explicitly recommends presenting multiple metrics together to give a complete picture of fund performance. The shift toward heightened DPI scrutiny, while real, should not be characterised as a displacement of IRR or TVPI as the primary evaluation criterion; rather, it represents an intensification of LP focus on capital return discipline alongside the traditional return metrics.

Regulatory constraints on illiquid alternatives vary significantly across jurisdictions. The OECD 2025 Annual Survey of Investment Regulation of Pension Providers documents that limits on alternative assets — defined to include real estate (non-residential), private equity, private debt, and co-investments — range from approximately 15% in some jurisdictions to 30% or more in others, with a gradual trend toward increasing these limits (OECD, 2025). A specific jurisdiction detailed in the OECD survey is phasing its alternatives cap incrementally: from 15% (August 2024 to July 2025), to 17% (August 2025 to July 2026), and ultimately to 20% from August 2027 onward (OECD, 2025). The wide variance across jurisdictions — from roughly 15% to 30% or more — means that implementation feasibility of the recommended alternatives allocation is highly jurisdiction-specific and constitutes a material constraint risk that must be explicitly addressed in the fund's investment policy. Pension funds operating in more restrictive jurisdictions must calibrate their targets to the applicable regulatory ceiling.

On liability-driven investing and glide paths, the Bank of America (2025) defines a de-risking glidepath as a dynamic pension investment strategy centred on shifting from growth assets to liability-hedging assets as the plan's funded status improves. Triggers are typically reviewed quarterly, monthly, or even daily, and the strategy is typically fully phased in at a sufficiently high funded status considered "fully funded," typically between 100% and 110%. Critically, Milliman (2025) notes that many plans still maintain exposure to a small return-seeking allocation even at full funding to hedge against "unknowns" such as longevity risk or potential costs to annuitize or fully terminate the plan — a consideration that applies equally to the current allocation framework. Russell Investments (2025) confirms that a well-designed glidepath details strategic asset allocations appropriate at various funded status levels and under various economic conditions, and that for most pension plans, it is appropriate to reduce risk as funded status improves.

On liquidity requirements for mature liabilities, the IMF (2025) notes that for more mature pension systems in aging societies, careful liquidity risk management is paramount. Closed pension funds with single-employer or industry-wide structures in shrinking economic sectors, and pension funds in aging countries with high old-age dependency ratios, face particular liquidity challenges. Some large Canadian pension funds have already reached their decumulation stage, wherein benefits payouts are and will foreseeably remain higher than contributions (IMF, 2025). EIOPA's 2025 stress test report emphasises that while IORPs are long-term investors with generally low liquidity risks given that contributions precede cash outflows, acute liquidity risks can be triggered by concentration in illiquid investments, investment strategies, and collateral that could result in large margin calls — as demonstrated by UK gilt market dislocations in 2022 (EIOPA, 2025). The IMF document discusses liquidity risk management principles for mature pension systems in aging societies but does not provide a specific quantitative recommendation for a 36–48 month liquidity buffer. The 36–48 month figure represents an analytical extension based on the fund's liability maturity profile — drawing on the IMF's general finding that more mature pension systems require heightened liquidity management — rather than a directly cited IMF recommendation, and should be understood accordingly.

On scenario stress testing, the Virginia Retirement System (VRS) 2022 stress test and sensitivity analysis includes a "Lost Decade" scenario with stagflationary characteristics. The inflation modelled under this scenario fluctuates between 3.7% and 12.0% during the first six years, averaging 8.2%, which is well above the 2.5% assumed rate of inflation (Virginia Retirement System, 2022, pp. 26–30). Although nominal equity returns are relatively high under stagflation, real returns reflect much slower growth and are negative for years 1–5 in this scenario, implying that the period of stagflation is front-loaded and then tapers off. Fixed income is hit hardest initially in years 1–4, but then stagnates slightly below the baseline for the remainder of the scenario. Critically, the Lost Decade scenario persists throughout the entire 20-year projection period — unlike the other scenarios, which are front-loaded — making it the most severe stress test for long-dated liabilities (Virginia Retirement System, 2022, p. 8). Bain Capital Real Estate research (2022) found that between 1972 and 1982, real estate generated total returns 62% and 85% higher than equities and bonds, respectively, though early in the cycle real estate values declined in tandem with rising interest rates before outperforming as leases matured. Kiplinger (2024) confirms that both stocks and bonds tend to underperform when stagflation occurs: stocks are hampered by slow economic growth while high inflation erodes bond returns.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### 3.1 The Inflation Environment and Its Portfolio Implications

Persistently elevated inflation at 4–5% annually does not merely reduce the real return on fixed-income holdings; it fundamentally reshapes the risk-return trade-offs across virtually every asset class, and — critically — it can produce simultaneous drawdowns in both equities and bonds, eliminating the diversification benefit that these asset classes normally provide to one another. Understanding these transmission mechanisms is prerequisite to constructing a coherent allocation framework.

For nominal bonds, the primary impact is the erosion of purchasing power in coupon and principal payments. While rising nominal yields that accompany inflation provide some compensation, the mark-to-market losses on existing holdings can be substantial, as evidenced by the 2022 bond market dislocations when simultaneous inflation and rate hikes produced double-digit losses in conventional fixed-income indices. The VRS stress test confirms that in a stagflationary scenario, fixed income is hit hardest initially in years 1–4. Inflation-linked bonds, particularly TIPS, partially address this vulnerability, as their principal value adjusts semi-annually with changes in the Consumer Price Index, providing a direct hedge for the inflation component of the liability. However, TIPS also carry duration risk and their real yields can be volatile; in periods of deflation or falling inflation expectations, they underperform nominal Treasuries.

Equities, in theory, should provide an inflation hedge over sufficiently long horizons, as corporate revenues and earnings nominally grow with prices. However, elevated inflation creates a more nuanced and frequently adverse picture. Companies with strong pricing power — particularly in consumer staples, energy, and selected technology subsectors — can pass on rising costs to customers and protect profit margins. Companies lacking pricing power, or those with high operating leverage and fixed costs, suffer margin compression. Moreover, the equity risk premium tends to compress when inflation is elevated, because the discount rate applied to future cash flows rises with nominal interest rates, placing downward pressure on price-to-earnings multiples even if earnings growth remains positive in nominal terms. The VRS Lost Decade scenario modelling confirms that while equity nominal returns are relatively high under stagflation, real returns are negative for the first five years — meaning that in real terms, equities fail to preserve purchasing power despite positive nominal performance. Historical data from the 1970s suggests that equity markets can deliver positive nominal returns during high-inflation periods, but with significantly higher volatility and a wide dispersion of outcomes across sectors.

The most dangerous aspect of a stagflationary scenario — one where inflation exceeds 5% and growth turns negative simultaneously — is the correlation breakdown between asset classes. Kiplinger (2024) confirms that both stocks and bonds tend to underperform when stagflation occurs, with stocks hampered by slow growth and high inflation eroding bond returns. This is precisely the tail risk that the proposed allocation must explicitly address, going beyond the baseline moderate growth scenario to model the fund's resilience under adverse conditions. The VRS Lost Decade scenario compounds this risk because it is not front-loaded but persists throughout the entire 20-year projection period — meaning that a fund relying on return-seeking assets to outpace inflation over a five-year horizon could face sustained real return underperformance.

Real assets — encompassing real estate, infrastructure, commodities, and natural resource equities — have the most direct and intuitive relationship with inflation. Real estate values and rental income tend to rise with replacement costs and consumer prices over the medium term, and Bain Capital's research confirms that over the full 1972–1982 stagflationary period, real estate outperformed both equities and bonds by substantial margins — though with an important caveat: early in the rate-hike cycle, real estate values declined in tandem with broader financial market stress before ultimately recovering and outperforming. Infrastructure assets with concession agreements often incorporate explicit inflation escalation clauses. Commodities are perhaps the most immediate and pure inflation hedge, as their spot prices respond quickly to changes in the general price level. Gold, while often touted as an inflation hedge, has a more complex relationship with inflation — it performed well during the 1970s but delivered mixed results relative to CPI during the 2010s, suggesting that its role is better characterised as a tail-risk hedge and portfolio diversifier rather than a reliable inflation-specific exposure.

The moderate growth backdrop is equally consequential. In a high-inflation, low-growth stagflationary scenario, the allocation problem becomes considerably more acute because neither equities nor conventional fixed income can be relied upon to deliver positive real returns with any confidence. The moderate growth assumption — developed economy GDP growth in the range of 1.5–2.5% annually — provides a critical anchor: corporate earnings can grow in nominal terms at 5–7% (assuming a modest 2–3% inflation assumption embedded in nominal GDP), providing fundamental support for equity valuations even as the discount rate remains elevated.

### 3.2 Liability-Driven Considerations and Funded Status Glide Path

Pension funds operate under a unique constraint: the actuarial liability. For a typical defined-benefit pension fund, the present value of future benefit obligations is computed using a discount rate — often linked to high-quality corporate bond yields — that must be compared against portfolio returns. In an elevated inflation environment, the fund faces two simultaneous pressures. First, if its liabilities are indexed to inflation (as many public sector and final-salary pension obligations are), the absolute size of the liability grows with CPI, requiring higher nominal returns to maintain funded status. Second, the discount rate used to present-value the liability may rise with nominal yields, partially offsetting the liability growth, but the net effect is typically negative for funded status when inflation is high and rate adjustments lag behind price increases.

This liability structure argues strongly for an asset allocation that is explicitly liability-aware — a principle broadly known as liability-driven investing (LDI). In practice, this means maintaining a core of high-quality, duration-matched fixed-income assets (including inflation-linked bonds) that move in concert with the liability, and supplementing this core with real-return assets that are expected to outperform CPI over the medium to long run, thereby reducing the funded status deficit.

Critically, the fund's investment policy must incorporate an explicit funded status glide path — a dynamic strategy centred on de-risking the portfolio's asset allocation as the plan's funded status improves. The glide path should specify funded status trigger points at which the investment committee will review and potentially reduce return-seeking allocations in favour of liability-hedging assets. Common trigger thresholds, as documented by Bank of America, Milliman, and Russell Investments, include staged reductions in equity exposure as the fund moves from severely underfunded (below 70%) to near fully funded (100–110%). The operative principle is that as the plan approaches and achieves full funding, the investment committee should systematically reduce the risk of funded status deterioration by shifting from growth assets to liability-matching assets. The glide path should be reviewed quarterly against the plan's updated funded status, and the investment policy statement should pre-authorise specific reallocation steps at defined funded status levels to avoid decision paralysis during periods of market stress.

The Critic raises a legitimate concern that the proposed Pillar 3 allocation of 37% to return-seeking assets may be aggressive in a stagflationary scenario where the VRS stress test shows equities delivering negative real returns for years 1–5. This criticism is well-founded, and the glide path framework must address it explicitly. The fund's appropriate return-seeking allocation is not a static number but a function of funded status, risk tolerance, and the probability-weighted return outlook. At lower funded status levels (below 75%), the fund may accept higher return-seeking exposure in an effort to close the funding gap — but this exposure should be managed with a more defensive tilt (value, quality, low duration in equity terms) than would be appropriate at higher funded status levels. As funded status improves, the glide path should systematically reduce return-seeking exposure to preserve gains. This is not merely a risk management consideration; it is the core logic of the glide path itself, as articulated by Bank of America and Russell Investments: the appropriate investment policy for a pension plan is heavily dependent on funded status, and de-risking as funded status improves is the mechanism through which the fund locks in improvements while retaining potential upside during the period when upside is most needed.

### 3.3 The Role of Alternatives in an Inflationary Environment: Navigating Regulatory Concentration Limits

The structural shift toward alternatives documented in industry data — with equity allocation having declined from 57% twenty years ago to 48% in 2025, while "other" asset classes (which include private equity, hedge funds, infrastructure, insurance contracts, and real estate) have grown to 19% — reflects a recognition that liquid, mark-to-market asset classes alone cannot deliver the combination of inflation protection, stable cash flows, and illiquidity premium that pension fund liabilities demand. The proposed alternatives allocation of 25–30% total is at or above the current P7 average of 19% for the "other" category, acknowledging that the fund is recommending a deliberate further rotation toward alternatives relative to current industry practice.

However, the proposed allocation must explicitly account for regulatory concentration limits that many pension funds face. The OECD 2025 Annual Survey of Investment Regulation of Pension Providers documents that limits on alternative assets range from approximately 15% in some jurisdictions to 30% or more in others. This wide variance — spanning a factor of two or more across jurisdictions — means that the feasibility of the recommended 25–30% alternatives allocation is highly jurisdiction-specific and constitutes a material constraint risk that must be flagged prominently. Pension funds operating in more restrictive jurisdictions — particularly those with statutory caps at or below 17% — will need to prioritise their alternative allocations, potentially favouring the most inflation-sensitive sub-categories (private infrastructure, private credit) over private equity or hedge funds, or alternatively seeking regulatory relief or restructuring through fund-of-funds vehicles that may be classified differently under applicable regulations. The directional trend toward increasing alternatives limits, confirmed by the OECD data, provides some medium-term relief for funds in currently restrictive jurisdictions, but implementation planning must be anchored to the current regulatory ceiling rather than the projected ceiling.

Within the alternatives sleeve, the composition is as important as the total allocation. Private infrastructure is particularly well-suited to the current environment. Assets such as regulated utilities, toll roads, airports, water treatment facilities, and renewable energy projects typically operate under long-term concession agreements with revenues tied to regulated tariffs that escalate with inflation. The cash flow predictability — often resembling senior debt in its stability — makes infrastructure an ideal bridge asset between fixed income and equities, providing a real return with lower volatility than listed equities. The illiquidity premium, historically in the range of 150–300 basis points over comparable public market equivalents, compensates the fund for locking up capital.

Private credit, particularly direct lending to mid-market companies, offers floating-rate exposure that is structurally advantaged in a rising-rate environment. As base rates remain elevated at 4–5%, the all-in yield on direct lending strategies — typically SOFR plus 500–700 basis points — provides attractive real returns, and the floating-rate structure means that the income stream rises with inflation or at least with monetary policy rates. For pension funds with moderate liquidity needs and a 5–7 year investment horizon, direct lending can be a core alternative allocation.

Private equity, while more challenged in an inflationary environment — particularly for leveraged buyouts where debt service becomes more burdensome and exit multiples may compress — still offers potential value in growth equity and venture capital strategies focused on productivity-enhancing and AI-adjacent sectors. The key is selectivity and LP discipline: favouring businesses with durable competitive moats, strong pricing power, and manageable leverage rather than highly leveraged financial engineering strategies. Cambridge Associates (2025) recommends evaluating PE performance using multiple metrics in combination — IRR, TVPI, and DPI — rather than any single metric in isolation. The heightened focus on DPI — distributions to paid-in capital — is real and documented, but it supplements rather than replaces IRR and TVPI as evaluation criteria. The shift toward evaluating PE managers on capital return discipline should be formalised in the fund's manager evaluation framework, with vintage-year-adjusted DPI benchmarks used alongside IRR benchmarks as performance screening criteria. Consistent with S&P Global Market Intelligence data showing that 58% of pension funds were overallocated to PE in Q1 2025, and that by mid-2025 this figure had risen to approximately 62%, the fund should target managers whose vintage-year DPI benchmarks are on track and should actively evaluate co-investment opportunities that offer lower fee drag and improved cash velocity.

Real assets through open-end or interval fund structures provide daily or quarterly liquidity while maintaining the underlying inflation linkage of direct real estate, infrastructure, and natural resources. These vehicles have grown substantially in sophistication and scale over the past decade, making them increasingly accessible for pension fund allocations, and their semi-liquid nature provides an important middle ground between fully liquid public securities and locked-up private fund interests.

### 3.4 Geopolitical and Structural Considerations

The current investment landscape is shaped by several structural forces beyond inflation and growth. Geopolitical fragmentation is prompting pension funds to reassess geographic allocations, with some US state plans actively divesting from Chinese securities in response to political mandates (Chronograph, 2025). While this creates country-specific reallocation dynamics, the broader implication is that pension funds must increasingly price geopolitical risk into their asset allocation decisions, potentially requiring broader diversification across non-correlated markets and careful consideration of supply chain resilience in their private asset portfolios.

Technological disruption, particularly the rapid adoption of generative AI, is reshaping corporate earnings trajectories and sector-level inflation dynamics. AI-driven productivity gains could partially offset inflationary pressures in labour-intensive sectors, but they also introduce concentrated technology exposure and valuation risk for pension funds that increase their technology allocations in search of growth.

Climate change, acknowledged as a material investment risk by approximately 75% of institutional investors who assess climate risks as financially significant (Morgan Stanley Institute for Sustainable Investing, 2025), creates both physical risk (damage to real asset portfolios) and transition risk (stranded assets in carbon-intensive industries). For a pension fund with a multi-decade horizon, integrating climate scenario analysis into asset allocation is not merely an ethical imperative but a risk management necessity, particularly for the real asset and infrastructure sleeves where physical climate risks can directly impair asset values.

### 3.5 Liquidity Management and the Mature Liability Dimension

A frequently underweighted dimension of asset allocation in inflationary environments is liquidity management, and this concern is especially acute for funds with mature liability profiles. The IMF (2025) notes that for more mature pension systems in aging societies, careful liquidity risk management is paramount. Some large Canadian pension funds have already reached their decumulation stage, wherein benefits payouts exceed contributions and will foreseeably continue to do so. This structural shift from net positive cash flow to net negative cash flow fundamentally changes the liquidity calculus: the fund must generate sufficient liquid assets not merely to meet near-term benefit payments, but to sustain operations through potential market dislocations without being forced to liquidate illiquid assets at distressed prices.

The appropriate liquidity buffer depends critically on the fund's liability maturity profile. The 36–48 month liquidity buffer figure referenced in this analysis represents an analytical extension based on the IMF's (2025) general finding that closed funds in aging societies face particular liquidity challenges, rather than a specific quantitative recommendation drawn directly from the IMF document. The calibration should be determined by the fund's actuary based on its specific liability maturity profile, the ratio of current retirees to active members, the expected pattern of benefit payments over the planning horizon, and the fund's tolerance for forced liquidation risk. EIOPA's 2025 stress test exercise specifically targets liquidity risk assessment under both a Yield Curve Up and a Yield Curve Down scenario, reflecting the regulator's recognition that liquidity strains materialise primarily under adverse conditions (EIOPA, 2025). The UK's 2022 gilt market crisis demonstrated that pension funds using LDI strategies with synthetic leverage through interest rate derivatives, repos, and LDI funds faced daily margin calls that proved difficult to meet through standard liquidity sources, ultimately triggering forced liquidation of long-dated gilts at distressed prices during a period of extreme market stress.

These considerations argue for a tiered liquidity framework: a primary buffer of highly liquid instruments (government securities, money market funds, short-duration investment-grade bonds) covering at minimum 24 months of projected benefit payments for funds with typical liability profiles, and potentially 36–48 months for mature funds in decumulation; a secondary tier of semi-liquid instruments (listed REITs, open-end real estate funds, exchange-traded commodities) that can be liquidated within days to weeks if the primary buffer is insufficient; and a tertiary tier of illiquid alternatives (private equity, private credit, private infrastructure) that are not relied upon for liquidity under normal or stressed conditions, but whose redemption terms and distribution schedules are explicitly modelled in the fund's cash flow projections.

## 4. Recommended Asset Allocation Strategy

Drawing together the macroeconomic analysis, the empirical data on current pension fund practice, the regulatory concentration constraints, the liability-driven glide path framework, and the scenario stress testing evidence, the following five-year asset allocation strategy is proposed. The framework is structured around four strategic pillars: liability matching, real-asset inflation protection, return-seeking diversification, and risk management. The allocation is calibrated to provide genuine real return generation while building in sufficient downside protection, particularly for lower funded status scenarios, through the funded status glide path. All figures are indicative target allocations with permitted tactical deviations of ±3–5 percentage points under the fund's investment policy framework, subject to applicable regulatory concentration limits.

### 4.1 Pillar 1 — Liability Matching and Inflation-Linked Fixed Income (Target: 28–32% of Total Assets)

The foundation of the portfolio must be a high-quality fixed-income sleeve that explicitly addresses the inflation-linked nature of the fund's actuarial liabilities. Within this pillar, two sub-allocations are recommended.

Direct Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) and inflation-linked government bonds from sovereign issuers with credible fiscal positions — principally the United States, Germany, and the United Kingdom — should constitute approximately 14–16% of total assets. These instruments provide a direct, liquid, and duration-matched hedge against the inflation component of the liability. Their real yield, rather than nominal yield, is the relevant return metric, and in the current environment of positive real yields on TIPS, they offer genuine real return generation rather than mere nominal preservation. The duration of this sub-allocation should be managed to approximate the duration of the liability's inflation-linked component, with the specific duration target reviewed against the fund's actuarial assumptions at each quarterly measurement date.

Investment-grade corporate bonds with inflation-linked features — including floating-rate notes and collateralised loan obligations (CLOs) that reset with short-term rates — should constitute approximately 12–16% of total assets. The floating-rate component ensures that income keeps pace with monetary policy rates in an inflationary environment, while the investment-grade credit rating maintains portfolio quality. In the current base rate environment, these instruments can deliver all-in yields in the range of 5.5–7.0%, providing meaningful real returns above the inflation baseline.

The combined fixed-income sleeve, at 28–32% of assets, should be duration-managed to approximate the duration of the actuarial liability, with periodic rebalancing as the liability profile evolves and funded status improves along the glide path. The key insight is that nominal government bonds alone are insufficient in an elevated inflation scenario; the inflation linkage must be explicit and direct. The slightly higher target range for this pillar (compared to the prior analysis) reflects the Critic's valid point that more robust liability matching is warranted, particularly for a fund facing the stagflationary tail risk documented in the VRS Lost Decade scenario.

### 4.2 Pillar 2 — Real Assets with Inflation Escalation (Target: 18–23% of Total Assets)

The real-asset sleeve is the portfolio's primary engine of real return generation and inflation outperformance. Three sub-categories are recommended, subject to applicable regulatory concentration limits on alternative assets. Given the OECD data on the wide variance in alternatives caps (15% to 30%+ depending on jurisdiction), the fund must monitor its aggregate alternatives exposure across all pillars against the applicable regulatory ceiling, prioritising the most inflation-sensitive sub-categories where constraints bind.

Core listed real estate investment trusts (REITs) and unlisted real estate funds targeting commercial, industrial, and residential properties in supply-constrained markets should constitute approximately 7–9% of total assets. REITs offer liquidity and transparency through public market listings while providing the inflation-linkage benefits of direct property ownership, including rental income that tends to escalate with market rents and replacement costs. The 1970s experience, as documented by Bain Capital, demonstrates both the resilience of real estate over the full stagflationary cycle and its near-term vulnerability to rising interest rates — underscoring the importance of selecting sub-sectors with structural demand tailwinds (logistics, residential, life sciences) and limited rate sensitivity, while managing duration exposure carefully. The interval fund and non-traded REIT structures provide an important semi-liquid middle ground for pension funds seeking real estate inflation exposure without full lock-up.

Private infrastructure — encompassing regulated utilities, transportation assets, digital infrastructure (data centres, fibre networks), and renewable energy projects — should constitute approximately 7–9% of total assets. Infrastructure assets with explicit inflation escalation clauses in their concession agreements represent perhaps the most direct inflation hedge available to institutional investors. The predictable, long-duration cash flows are particularly valuable for matching long-dated pension liabilities. Infrastructure investments can be accessed through open-end infrastructure funds, co-investments alongside established general partners, and selectively through listed infrastructure equities for liquidity management purposes. Given the regulatory concentration limits documented by the OECD (2025), the fund should monitor its aggregate alternatives allocation across all sub-categories (real estate, infrastructure, private equity, private credit) against the applicable regulatory ceiling, prioritising infrastructure within the alternatives sleeve due to its superior inflation linkage characteristics.

Commodities and natural resource equities should constitute approximately 4–5% of total assets. A diversified commodity exposure — whether through total return swaps, commodity futures indices, or commodity-focused private equity — provides the most immediate and direct hedge against CPI surprises and commodity price spikes. Natural resource equities, including energy and mining companies with strong balance sheets and disciplined capital allocation, offer a hybrid exposure: the inflation linkage of commodities combined with the equity risk premium and potential dividend yields of equity ownership. The weighting is kept modest (4–5%) because commodities do not generate income, exhibit high volatility, and can underperform during economic slowdowns — risks that are partially mitigated but not eliminated by the moderate growth assumption.

### 4.3 Pillar 3 — Return-Seeking Equities and Private Alternatives (Target: 32–37% of Total Assets)

The return-seeking sleeve is where the fund captures the equity risk premium and illiquidity premium necessary to outpace its 4–5% inflation benchmark on a portfolio-wide basis. The recommended allocation represents a deliberate shift from the conventional 50–55% equity allocation typical of pension funds in the pre-2020 era, reflecting the changed capital market assumptions and the need to reduce dependence on any single asset class. The Critic's observation that 37% may be aggressive for a stagflationary scenario is well-founded, and the calibrated range of 32–37% — combined with an explicit funded status glide path that reduces this exposure as the fund approaches full funding — addresses this concern directly.

Developed market equities should constitute approximately 17–21% of total assets, with a deliberate tilt toward sectors that have historically demonstrated pricing power during inflationary periods: consumer staples, energy, healthcare, and select technology companies with dominant market positions and high free cash flow conversion. Within the equity sleeve, the geographic allocation should be broadly diversified across the United States, European Union, United Kingdom, and Japan. The equity sleeve's geographic composition should broadly reflect global market capitalisation weights, with a value tilt within the equity allocation, as value stocks (with lower duration characteristics in earnings terms) tend to outperform growth stocks during periods of rising real yields and elevated inflation. Kiplinger (2024) corroborates this tilt, noting that value stocks and companies in defensive areas like consumer staples and healthcare outperformed other sectors during the 1970s stagflation. Furthermore, the equity allocation should incorporate explicit tail-risk management through the use of protective put spreads on major index exposures — a cost-effective mechanism for preserving upside participation while capping downside exposure in a scenario where equities deliver negative real returns for multiple consecutive years, as modelled in the VRS Lost Decade scenario.

Emerging market equities, at 5–7% of total assets, should be approached with greater selectivity given geopolitical risks and the currency volatility that often accompanies inflationary pressures in developing economies. The allocation should focus on commodity-exporting emerging markets (which benefit from global inflation) and Asia ex-China for structural growth exposure, while reducing or eliminating single-country exposures that carry significant geopolitical tail risk. Currency hedging should be actively managed for this sleeve.

Private equity — encompassing buyouts, growth equity, and venture capital — should constitute approximately 5–8% of total assets, calibrated to the fund's position relative to applicable regulatory concentration limits on alternatives. Consistent with the industry trend toward higher PE allocations and the S&P Global Market Intelligence data indicating that approximately 58% of pension funds were above their PE targets in Q1 2025 — a figure that rose to roughly 62% by mid-2025 — this sleeve is justified by the historical illiquidity premium and the potential for outperformance relative to public markets over a five-year horizon. However, the composition of the PE sleeve should differ materially from the pre-2020 consensus. Leveraged buyouts with high financial leverage are disadvantaged in an elevated inflation environment, as rising interest expenses compress returns and uncertain exit multiples create valuation risk. Instead, the allocation should favour mid-market buyouts with moderate leverage, growth equity strategies in sectors benefiting from structural tailwinds (healthcare technology, climate solutions, AI infrastructure), and co-investments alongside established general partners to reduce fee drag and improve cash velocity. The emphasis on DPI — distributions to paid-in capital — alongside IRR and TVPI as evaluation criteria, consistent with Cambridge Associates' (2025) recommendation to present multiple metrics in combination, should govern manager selection. Funds should set minimum DPI thresholds by vintage year and actively monitor whether PE relationships are on track to meet these benchmarks.

Private credit, at 5–7% of total assets, provides floating-rate income with a premium over public credit markets. In the current base rate environment, direct lending to middle-market companies can generate all-in yields of 8–12%, representing a compelling real return above the inflation baseline with default risk managed through careful credit selection and diversification. This allocation benefits from the elevated rate environment while avoiding the duration risk of fixed-rate bonds. The allocation should be diversified across borrowers, geographies, and industries to manage credit concentration risk.

### 4.4 Pillar 4 — Risk Management and Tactical Reserves (Target: 12–17% of Total Assets)

The final pillar addresses portfolio resilience, opportunistic deployment, and liquidity management. The slightly elevated range for this pillar — relative to the prior analysis — reflects the need for greater downside protection in a stagflationary environment where both equities and nominal bonds may simultaneously produce negative real returns.

Investment-grade short-duration bonds and money market instruments (including government securities, commercial paper, and short-maturity corporate bonds) should constitute approximately 6–8% of total assets. The duration of this sleeve should be kept short (under two years) to minimise mark-to-market sensitivity to rate changes while maintaining the option to extend duration when real yields are attractive. Critically, this sleeve also constitutes the fund's primary liquidity buffer — for funds with typical liability profiles, the target should be to maintain at least 24 months of projected benefit payments in this tier, while mature funds with net negative cash flow should target 36–48 months of coverage. The exact calibration should be determined by the fund's actuary based on the liability maturity profile, consistent with the IMF's (2025) analytical direction regarding the liquidity challenges of decumulation-stage pension funds.

Gold and other precious metals, at 2–4% of total assets, serve as a tail-risk hedge and portfolio diversifier. While gold's inflation-hedging credentials are nuanced, it retains its role as a store of value during currency debasement episodes, geopolitical crises, and systemic financial stress — risks that are non-trivial in the current environment of elevated geopolitical tension and fiscal imbalance in developed economies.

Tactical and opportunistic allocations — including secondaries, rescue financing, and structured credit — should constitute approximately 3–5% of total assets. These strategies offer superior risk-adjusted returns in dislocated market conditions, and the current environment of elevated uncertainty creates periodic opportunities for well-capitalised investors to earn premium returns. Secondaries, in particular, have emerged as a favoured strategy for pension funds seeking to access private market returns with reduced blind-pool risk and shorter effective duration (Chronograph, 2025). The secondary market allows the fund to acquire PE and PE-adjacent exposures at potentially discounted valuations during periods of market stress, which can be particularly valuable in the stagflationary tail risk scenario.

## 4.5 Implementation and Governance Considerations

**Rebalancing Discipline and Funded Status Glide Path.** With a five-year horizon and elevated market volatility, the rebalancing framework must be rules-based but flexible enough to accommodate tactical opportunities. The fund's investment policy should specify an explicit funded status glide path with the following illustrative trigger framework, calibrated to the fund's specific liability maturity profile. When funded status is below 75%, the portfolio may carry a higher allocation to return-seeking assets (up to 40% in Pillar 3) with a defensive tilt toward value, quality, and low equity duration — reflecting the VRS Lost Decade finding that equities can deliver negative real returns for the first five years of a stagflationary scenario. When funded status is between 75% and 90%, the allocation should move toward the strategic targets specified above. When funded status is between 90% and 100%, the allocation to liability-matching assets (Pillar 1) should increase by 3–5 percentage points, funded by reductions in return-seeking assets. When funded status is between 100% and 110% ("near fully funded"), the allocation to return-seeking assets should be reduced further, with the liability-matching sleeve expanded toward 35–40% of total assets — consistent with Milliman's (2025) finding that many plans maintain a small return-seeking allocation even at full funding to hedge against longevity risk and annuitization costs. When funded status exceeds 110% ("fully funded"), the investment committee should consider implementing a full liability-matching strategy with minimal return-seeking exposure, consistent with the plan sponsor's risk tolerance and contribution policy. The glide path should be reviewed quarterly against updated actuarial valuations, and the investment committee should pre-authorise specific reallocation steps at each trigger level to avoid decision paralysis during market stress.

**Regulatory Concentration Limit Monitoring.** Given the OECD 2025 data on alternatives concentration limits — which range from approximately 15% to 30% or more depending on jurisdiction, with at least one jurisdiction phasing upward from 15% to 17% to 20% during 2024–2027 — the fund's investment team must maintain a real-time register of its aggregate alternatives exposure across all sub-categories. Where the aggregate alternatives allocation approaches the applicable regulatory ceiling, the fund should prioritise its most inflation-sensitive alternatives sub-categories (private infrastructure, private credit) and may need to reduce or delay new commitments to private equity or hedge funds in favour of liquid instruments that do not count against the alternatives cap. This jurisdiction-specific constraint risk is material and must be explicitly disclosed in the investment policy statement.

**Manager Selection in Alternatives.** Given that the recommended framework allocates approximately 24–27% of assets to alternative investments (subject to regulatory limits), manager selection is the primary determinant of whether the theoretical return premiums of these asset classes are actually realised. The fund should concentrate commitments among established general partners with proven track records, diversified across vintage years to avoid concentration risk, and supplemented by co-investments to reduce fee drag and improve cash velocity (DPI). The heightened focus on DPI — consistent with current LP evaluation criteria documented by Cambridge Associates, Altvia, and Carta — should be formalised alongside IRR and TVPI as part of a multi-metric manager evaluation framework, with vintage-year-adjusted DPI benchmarks used as one of several performance screening criteria. The evidence from S&P Global Market Intelligence — showing that 58% of pension funds were already overallocated to PE in Q1 2025, rising to approximately 62% by mid-2025 — underscores both the scale of existing PE exposure across the industry and the importance of disciplined management going forward. The fund should be disciplined about not chasing PE exposure simply because others are above target, but should instead evaluate each commitment on its standalone merits and alignment with the inflation-resilient portfolio design.

**ESG and Climate Integration.** Consistent with the data indicating that approximately 75% of institutional investors assess climate risks as financially material (Morgan Stanley Institute for Sustainable Investing, 2025), the fund should integrate climate scenario analysis into its asset allocation process. For real assets, this means conducting physical risk assessments on property and infrastructure portfolios. For private equity, it means incorporating transition risk into credit analysis. For public equities, it means applying systematic ESG screens alongside financial analysis. The goal is not to sacrifice returns for ethical considerations, but to ensure that structural risks are priced into the portfolio rather than ignored.

**Currency Risk Management.** Approximately 45–50% of the fund's equity and alternative exposure will be denominated in currencies outside the base currency of the liability. For major currency pairs (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY), a partial hedging programme — covering 50–70% of foreign currency exposure — should be implemented using rolling forward contracts. The remaining unhedged exposure is intentional, as it provides diversification benefit and captures the equity risk premium associated with foreign currency exposure. For emerging market currencies, hedging costs and liquidity constraints argue for a more selective approach, focusing hedging activity on the highest-conviction positions.

## 4.6 Scenario Analysis and Stress Testing

The proposed allocation should be evaluated against four representative scenarios over the five-year horizon to ensure robustness, including the critical stagflationary tail risk identified by the Critic.

Under a **baseline scenario** — moderate growth of 1.5–2.5%, inflation of 4–5%, and central banks maintaining restrictive but stable policy rates — the portfolio is expected to generate a nominal return in the range of 6.0–7.5% annually, representing a real return of approximately 1.5–3.0% above inflation. The TIPS sleeve provides the inflation hedge, infrastructure and private credit generate stable real income, and equities contribute growth-oriented returns with the value/quality tilt providing resilience. Funded status is expected to improve modestly if actuarial assumptions are met.

Under a **stagflationary scenario** — growth below 1%, inflation above 5% — the simultaneous drawdown risk in both equities and nominal bonds is the primary threat. The VRS Lost Decade stress test modelling, drawn from Virginia Retirement System (2022, pp. 26–30), confirms that in this scenario, inflation fluctuates between 3.7% and 12.0% during the first six years, averaging 8.2%, which is well above the 2.5% assumed rate of inflation. Fixed income is hit hardest initially in years 1–4, while equities generate positive nominal returns but negative real returns for years 1–5 due to inflation erosion. Critically, the Lost Decade scenario is not front-loaded like the other VRS scenarios; it persists throughout the entire 20-year projection period (Virginia Retirement System, 2022, p. 8), making it the most severe long-term stress test for the fund's liability profile. The real-return assets (infrastructure, commodities, TIPS) become the portfolio's most important contributors, providing genuine purchasing power protection. The short-duration fixed-income positioning prevents the mark-to-market losses that devastated long-duration bond portfolios in 2022. Bain Capital's research confirms that over the full stagflationary cycle (1972–1982), real estate outperformed both equities and bonds by 62–85%, though with near-term vulnerability at the onset of rate-hike cycles. The protective put spreads on the equity sleeve provide a mechanism for capping equity downside without fully eliminating upside participation. The tiered liquidity buffer (Pillar 4) ensures that benefit payments can be met without forced liquidations of illiquid assets during the period of maximum market stress. Portfolio real returns may compress to near zero or marginally negative under this scenario, but the funded status deterioration should be materially limited relative to a conventional high-equity, long-duration portfolio — and the funded status glide path ensures that as funded status deteriorates below 75%, the investment committee has pre-authorised triggers to further reduce return-seeking exposure, thereby automatically protecting the fund from escalating equity market stress.

Under an **inflation resolution scenario** — in which central banks succeed in bringing inflation back to the 2–3% target over two to three years, and developed economy growth accelerates to 2.5–3.5% — the equity and private equity sleeves should benefit from the simultaneous expansion of earnings multiples and earnings growth. Infrastructure and TIPS, while their inflation outperformance moderates, will have locked in real returns during the high-inflation period. The portfolio is well-positioned to benefit from any such scenario by maintaining the diversified structure rather than making dramatic shifts based on near-term inflation forecasts.

Under a **liquidity stress scenario** — in which the fund faces elevated benefit payments due to demographic pressures, large margin calls on derivative positions, or a redemption wave from participant-directed accounts — the tiered liquidity framework provides resilience. The primary buffer (6–8% in short-duration instruments, calibrated to 24–48 months of benefit payments depending on liability maturity) covers the near-term payment requirements; the secondary tier (semi-liquid real estate and listed equities) can be accessed within days; the tertiary tier (illiquid alternatives) is not relied upon under stress but its distribution schedule is modelled in the fund's cash flow projections. This framework is designed to avoid the forced liquidation dynamics that afflicted UK pension funds in 2022, as documented by EIOPA's 2025 stress test exercise.

## 5. Key Conclusions and Decision-Relevant Takeaways

The proposed allocation strategy rests on five foundational conclusions that the fund's investment committee and trustees should internalise as guiding principles over the five-year horizon.

First, the fund must explicitly abandon the pre-2020 consensus that a conventional 60/40 equity-bond portfolio is the default optimal allocation in a persistently inflationary environment. The structural shift toward elevated and persistent inflation, combined with the re-pricing of the equity risk premium in a higher-rate environment, means that the 60/40 portfolio's expected real return is materially lower than historical averages, while its inflation risk is inadequately addressed. The VRS stress test and Bain Capital research confirm that in a stagflationary scenario, both equities and nominal bonds can simultaneously deliver negative real returns — eliminating the diversification benefit that the 60/40 structure traditionally provides. The industry data from NCPERS (2026) confirms that leading pension funds have already migrated toward lower equity allocations (45.2% for 2025 respondents), and the Thinking Ahead Institute (2026) data verifies that P7 equity allocations stand at 48% in 2025, down nine percentage points from 57% twenty years prior. The pace of this structural shift varies across funds, but the direction is clear and the rationale is compelling in the current environment.

Second, the funded status glide path is not an optional governance enhancement but the primary risk management mechanism for the entire allocation framework. The Critic's valid observation that a 37% return-seeking allocation may be aggressive under stagflationary conditions is addressed not by reducing the strategic target per se — which must be set to enable the fund to close any funding gap — but by embedding the return-seeking allocation within a dynamic glide path that automatically reduces exposure as funded status improves or deteriorates below critical thresholds. The pre-authorised trigger framework — with specific reallocation steps at 75%, 90%, 100%, and 110% funded status — ensures that the fund does not maintain excessive return-seeking exposure precisely when equity markets are most stressed, as modelled in the VRS Lost Decade scenario.

Third, real assets — specifically private infrastructure, inflation-linked real estate, and selective commodities — are not an optional enhancement but a structural necessity. Their unique combination of inflation linkage, long-duration cash flows, and illiquidity premium makes them the primary vehicle through which the fund can close the gap between its required real return and the return available from conventional asset classes. Bain Capital's historical data confirms that over the full stagflationary cycle of 1972–1982, real estate generated total returns 62% and 85% higher than equities and bonds, respectively. An allocation of 18–23% to real assets, while higher than historical norms for many funds, is fully justified by the current and projected capital market environment, subject to applicable regulatory concentration limits.

Fourth, private alternatives — private equity and private credit — deserve a substantial but reformed allocation. The key is not whether to invest in private markets, but how. The heightened focus on DPI — distributions to paid-in capital — is real and documented, but it operates alongside IRR and TVPI rather than replacing them. As Cambridge Associates (2025) explicitly states, "TVPI and DPI provide valuable insight into cash returns and cannot be 'locked in' or managed like the IRR can," and IRR should always be reviewed alongside cash-on-cash multiples to give a complete picture of fund performance. The evidence from S&P Global Market Intelligence — showing that 58% of pension funds were overallocated to PE in Q1 2025, rising to approximately 62% by mid-2025 — underscores both the scale of existing PE exposure across the industry and the importance of disciplined management going forward. Leveraged, financial-engineering-dependent buyout strategies face structural headwinds in an inflationary environment, while cash-generative growth strategies and floating-rate credit are structurally advantaged. The fund should apply these criteria rigorously in manager selection, vintage-year pacing, and performance monitoring, and should actively manage its alternatives allocation against applicable regulatory concentration limits documented by the OECD (2025).

Fifth, the fund's liquidity framework must be explicitly calibrated to its liability maturity profile. The 36–48 month liquidity buffer referenced in this analysis is an analytical extension based on the IMF's (2025) finding that closed funds in aging societies face particular liquidity challenges, rather than a specific quantitative recommendation drawn directly from the IMF document. The appropriate calibration depends on the fund's specific liability maturity profile, demographic composition, and actuarial projections, and should be determined in consultation with the fund's actuary. For mature pension funds with net negative cash flows — a situation that the IMF (2025) identifies as already affecting some large Canadian pension funds and becoming increasingly common in aging societies — a larger liquidity buffer may be warranted. The tiered liquidity framework (primary, secondary, and tertiary) provides resilience against both normal benefit payment requirements and the acute liquidity strains documented in the UK's 2022 gilt market crisis and EIOPA's 2025 stress test exercise.

In sum, this five-year asset allocation strategy is designed not merely to survive the elevated inflation environment but to generate genuine real returns above the 4–5% inflation baseline, while maintaining the liquidity, diversification, risk discipline, regulatory compliance, and funded status governance that a pension fund's fiduciary obligations demand. The strategy is deliberately positioned away from the conventional 60/40 model toward a more resilient, multi-asset structure in which real assets, inflation-linked securities, return-seeking private alternatives, and explicit liability-driven glide path management collectively deliver the risk-adjusted performance that the fund's beneficiaries are entitled to expect over the long run.
