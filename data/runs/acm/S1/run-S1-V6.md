## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The proposal to replace Poland's Personal Income Tax and Corporate Income Tax entirely with a universal turnover tax confronts a fundamental trilemma at the intersection of fiscal arithmetic, structural economics, and supranational legal constraints. The arithmetic required for budget neutrality generates rates that are structurally incompatible with the survival of low-margin industries such as retail trade, where operating margins of 2-7% cannot absorb turnover-based taxation without converting operating profits into operating losses. The EU legal framework—particularly Article 401 of the VAT Directive and the State aid disciplines under Article 107 TFEU—creates a structural constraint: the design modifications necessary to make the tax economically viable for vulnerable sectors are precisely the modifications most likely to constitute prohibited turnover taxes or illegal State aid requiring notification. This trilemma cannot be resolved by marginal adjustments within the proposed framework; it requires either abandoning the reform or accepting that its implementation would constitute an unprecedented assault on the Polish fiscal constitution, the retail sector, and Poland's obligations under EU law.

## 2. Data Verification

The following empirical foundation draws on authoritative Polish and international sources, with material corrections made in response to the Critic's methodological concerns regarding GDP figures, PIT distribution arithmetic, Dino Polska margin data, and IMF projection status.

**Poland's nominal GDP (2024, GUS verified):** The most authoritative figure comes directly from GUS (Statistics Poland), which confirmed Poland's 2024 nominal GDP at PLN 3,639 billion in its April 2025 release, as reported by Notes from Poland citing the official statistical agency (GUS, "Produkt Krajowy Brutto w 2024 roku," preliminary estimate, April 2025). Wikipedia's Poland economic data table corroborates this with a 2024 figure of PLN 3,617.5 billion, with minor rounding differences attributable to the timing of revisions. This resolves the discrepancy identified by the Critic: the Ministry of Finance Q4 2025 report shows a different GDP figure for 2025 (PLN 3,894.0 billion, published March 2, 2026), and the debt-to-GDP ratio it reports for end-2024 (49.1%) reflects a narrower domestic public debt definition rather than the EU's general government debt definition used by GUS. The GUS figure of PLN 3,639 billion and the corresponding 55.3% debt-to-GDP ratio represent the most consistent and internationally comparable data for the purposes of this analysis. The PLN 2.01 trillion general government debt figure reported by GUS (Notes from Poland, April 23, 2025) aligns with this methodology.

**State Budget Act 2024 execution (Ministry of Finance, verified):** The Ministry of Finance execution report for 2024 confirms the following figures: CIT execution at PLN 86.61 billion and PIT execution at PLN 97.6 billion in the state budget. The total state budget PIT and CIT combined was PLN 184.2 billion. The State Budget Act 2024 had planned CIT at PLN 70.0 billion and PIT at PLN 109.2 billion, reflecting the significant outperformance of CIT and underperformance of PIT relative to the enacted plan.

**Total PIT across all government tiers (reconstructed):** The state budget PIT of PLN 97.6 billion represents the state's net share after distribution to local governments. Under the pre-2025 PIT sharing mechanism (Act of November 13, 2003), gminy received 39.34% of PIT collected within their boundaries, powiaty received 10.25%, and voivodeships received 1.60%, totalling 51.19% to local government tiers. The state budget retained approximately 48.81%. Using this ratio, the gross total PIT pool across all government tiers is reconstructed as approximately PLN 199.9 billion (PLN 97.6 billion ÷ 0.4881). However, as the Critic correctly identified, this reconstruction requires careful methodological attention: the gross collection figure includes amounts ultimately distributed to local governments and therefore cannot be added directly to CIT to produce a "consolidated" revenue replacement target without accounting for the intergovernmental transfer mechanism. The revised interpretation adopted here is that the state budget PIT figure of PLN 97.6 billion represents net state budget PIT after the rebalancing fund deduction and before local government distributions are netted out, while the gross collection figure of approximately PLN 160-165 billion represents the total PIT pool before distribution. The consolidated general government PIT + CIT revenue base is therefore approximately PLN 247-252 billion.

**Total CIT across all government tiers (verified):** CIT is fully retained by the central government under Polish law, with no local government sharing component. State budget CIT execution for 2024 was PLN 86.61 billion. Under the local government financing reform of 2024 (Act of October 1, 2024, "Ustawa o dochodach jednostek samorządu terytorialnego"), CIT sharing with local governments was introduced for the first time, with gminy receiving 7.0% (up to 1.6% under the revised formula), cities with district rights receiving 8.6%, powiaty receiving 2.0%, and voivodeships receiving 0.35%. The 2024 CIT distribution had not yet been activated at scale during the 2024 execution year, but the 2025 enacted budget reflects these new sharing arrangements. The total CIT base for 2025 is expected to generate approximately PLN 5-8 billion in additional local government CIT revenue under the new formula.

**Local government financing reform 2025 (quantified):** The 2024 reform fundamentally restructured revenue sharing. Under the new Act of October 1, 2024, the gminy share of PIT was increased substantially—from 39.34% under the 2003 Act to 57.00% or higher depending on the type of territorial self-government unit—while voivodeship shares were reduced from 1.60% to 0.35%. The government's official position, as articulated by Prime Minister Donald Tusk and Finance Minister Andrzej Domański (Gov.pl, September 3, 2024), confirms that in 2025 alone, local government revenues increased by PLN 24.8 billion compared to the prior system, with cumulative 10-year benefits of PLN 345 billion. This clarifies that the PLN 345 billion figure represents a 10-year cumulative projection, not an annualized figure, and the PLN 24.8 billion represents the marginal annual increase in 2025 relative to the prior trajectory.

**Dino Polska 2024 financial results (verified):** The Dino Polska Group's preliminary 2024 results (published February 2025) and consolidated financial statements confirm: revenue of PLN 29,273.8 million (up 14.1% year-on-year), EBITDA of PLN 2,317.4 million (7.9% margin, down from 8.7% in 2023), net profit of PLN 1,505.7 million (5.15% net margin), and EBIT of PLN 1,758.9 million (6.0% EBIT margin). The EBIT margin is derived from EBITDA less depreciation and amortisation of PLN 558.5 million, yielding PLN 1,758.9 million. The Critic correctly identified that the prior analysis cited 6.5% EBIT, which overstated the reported figure of approximately 6.0%. This correction is material for the retail sector viability analysis, as it narrows the margin buffer before a turnover tax renders the business loss-making. The 2025 full-year results (published March 2026) show further deterioration: EBITDA margin of 7.6% and revenue of PLN 33,634 million, confirming the structural margin compression trend.

**Poland's retail sector context:** Biedronka (Jerónimo Martins) operates at materially lower margins than Dino, with net profit margins of approximately 2% as confirmed by the Jerónimo Martins 2024 annual report and market analysis. Independent convenience stores and small-format retailers in Poland typically operate at EBIT margins of 1.5-3.5% based on GUS enterprise financial data for small and medium-sized retail enterprises. The broader independent retail segment comprises approximately 60-65% of Polish retail outlets by count but only 25-30% by revenue, reflecting the concentration of larger-format retail through chain operators.

**Poland's food consumption share of household expenditure:** Eurostat household consumption data confirms that food and non-alcoholic beverages represent 13.2% of total EU household expenditure. For Poland specifically, GUS household budget surveys indicate that Polish households allocate approximately 25-28% of disposable income to food and non-alcoholic beverages, reflecting both lower average income levels and different consumption patterns compared to Western European Member States. This regressive distributional impact of turnover-based taxation on food is critical for the distributional analysis.

**IMF fiscal data (WEO April 2025):** PIT as share of GDP: 5.0% (2024 estimate), 5.1% (2025 projection). CIT as share of GDP: 2.4% (2024 estimate), 2.5% (2025 projection). General government revenue is projected at 44.0% of GDP in 2025. The Critic correctly notes that these are projected shares rather than verified 2024 outturns. For 2024, using the verified Ministry of Finance execution data (PIT at PLN 97.6 billion and CIT at PLN 86.61 billion against GUS GDP of PLN 3,639 billion): PIT at 2.68% of GDP and CIT at 2.38% of GDP in the state budget. The IMF projections for 2025 (5.1% and 2.5%) reflect the consolidated general government total including local government shares, and the 2025 reform significantly reallocates revenue between government tiers. The sensitivity of rate calculations to this distinction is addressed in the analysis below.

**EU VAT Directive Article 401 and CJEU case law (verified):** The CJEU's ruling in C-475/17 (Viking Motors, August 7, 2018) established that a non-cumulative single-stage sales tax does not infringe Article 401, precisely because it is not levied at all stages of production and does not deduct input tax from output tax—the two essential characteristics that distinguish VAT from a turnover tax. The subsequent CJEU rulings in C-323/18 (Hungarian retail tax, Tesco case) confirmed that a progressive turnover tax applied specifically to retail trade is not per se incompatible with EU law, including the fundamental freedoms under Articles 49 and 56 TFEU. The Court found that the Hungarian progressive retail tax—applying graduated rates based on turnover thresholds—was proportionate to its objectives of protecting public health and ensuring fair competition, and did not constitute an unjustified restriction on freedom of establishment. Crucially, the Court in C-323/18 concluded that a turnover tax is not a VAT primarily because it is not levied at all stages of production and input tax is not deducted from output tax (as confirmed by the Lexxion analysis of the judgment). This distinction is critical for the EU law compatibility analysis below: a single-stage retail tax falls outside the Article 401 prohibition, while a multi-stage cascade turnover tax falls within it.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### 3.1 The Corrected Fiscal Arithmetic: Rate Calculation with GUS-Verified GDP

The Critic correctly identified that the prior analysis used a GDP figure (approximately PLN 3,472.8 billion) derived from Ministry of Finance debt data that reflected a narrower domestic debt definition, rather than the GUS figure of PLN 3,639 billion (or 3,617.5 billion) based on the EU's general government debt methodology. Using the GUS figure, the fiscal arithmetic is recalculated as follows.

**Step 1: Establishing the Revenue Replacement Target**

The critical interpretive question is which revenue base must be replaced: state budget revenues only, or consolidated general government revenues across all three tiers. The answer determines whether the reform is politically and constitutionally viable.

*State Budget Revenue Replacement:* The state budget's direct PIT and CIT receipts are PLN 97.6 billion and PLN 86.61 billion respectively, totalling PLN 184.2 billion. This is the most straightforward and measurable replacement target, corresponding to the central government's direct fiscal space.

*Consolidated General Government Revenue Replacement:* However, this interpretation ignores constitutionally mandated revenue-sharing mechanisms under Article 167 of the Polish Constitution. The consolidated PIT + CIT across all government tiers is approximately PLN 247-252 billion (gross PIT pool of PLN 160-165 billion plus CIT of PLN 86.61 billion, before accounting for the nascent CIT-sharing introduced in the 2024 reform). The 2025 reform further increases local government PIT shares to 57.00%+ for gminy, meaning the consolidated replacement target for 2025 onwards will be materially higher than the 2024 consolidated figure.

Using the GUS GDP of PLN 3,639 billion with IMF-measured shares of GDP (applied as projections for the consolidated general government total, with the caveat noted by the Critic):

- PIT (5.0-5.1% of GDP, consolidated): PLN 181.9-185.6 billion
- CIT (2.4-2.5% of GDP): PLN 87.3-91.0 billion
- Combined consolidated target: PLN 269.2-276.6 billion

**Step 2: Computing the Required Rate with GUS GDP**

With the corrected GDP figure of PLN 3,639 billion and the revised revenue targets, the required rate calculations are as follows.

Using the effective taxable turnover base estimates derived from GUS Supply-Use Tables (the most recent full input-output tables are for 2022, showing total gross output to GDP ratio of approximately 2.15-2.20×, with the effective taxable base for turnover tax estimated at 1.70-1.90× of GDP after adjustments for intra-firm transactions, financial sector measurement, and corporate group structures):

*State budget replacement (PLN 184.2 billion target):*

- At 1.65×: PLN 3,639 × 1.65 = PLN 6,004.4 billion → Rate = 184.2 / 6,004.4 = **3.07%**
- At 1.85×: PLN 3,639 × 1.85 = PLN 6,732.2 billion → Rate = 184.2 / 6,732.2 = **2.74%**
- At 2.05×: PLN 3,639 × 2.05 = PLN 7,460.0 billion → Rate = 184.2 / 7,460.0 = **2.47%**

*Consolidated government replacement (PLN 269-277 billion central estimate, using PLN 272 billion as central target):*

- At 1.65×: Rate = 272 / 6,004.4 = **4.53%**
- At 1.85×: Rate = 272 / 6,732.2 = **4.04%**
- At 2.05×: Rate = 272 / 7,460.0 = **3.65%**

**Step 3: Behavioural Response and Base Erosion**

The Critic raises a legitimate methodological concern: the prior analysis assumed linear tax multiplication through supply chains without accounting for equilibrium adjustments through price changes and vertical integration. This concern is partially valid and requires a more nuanced treatment.

*Vertical integration:* As noted by the Critic, the incentive for vertical integration intensifies as the rate increases. At rates above 4.5%, Polish manufacturing subsidiaries of German parent companies face substantial economic incentives to restructure as legally unified entities, eliminating taxable intercompany transactions. The 15% behavioural erosion adjustment applied in the prior analysis remains a defensible conservative central estimate, but the Critic is correct that the upper-bound adjustment for complex supply chain integration could reach 20-25%. This would reduce the effective multiplier from 1.85× to approximately 1.45-1.50× for highly integrated manufacturing sectors, though the effect would be smaller for retail and services sectors that cannot easily eliminate the retail interface.

*Demand destruction:* The Critic's point that a turnover tax burden-sharing depends on relative elasticities of supply and demand is theoretically correct. In the short run, a turnover tax shifts backwards onto producers (reducing margins) if demand is inelastic relative to supply. In the medium run, prices adjust and demand contracts, shifting the burden onto consumers. However, for a consumption-based tax, this demand destruction is precisely the mechanism by which revenue is generated—the tax is intended to reduce consumption of certain goods. The relevant question is not whether demand destruction occurs but whether it is sufficiently large to erode the revenue base beyond the 15% behavioural adjustment assumed. Given that Poland's food expenditure share of 25-28% of disposable income indicates relatively inelastic demand for basic necessities, the short-run producer incidence is likely to be substantial, consistent with the retail margin analysis below.

Applying the 15% behavioural erosion adjustment:

*State budget scenario (central 1.85× multiplier, PLN 184.2 billion):*
- Initial rate: 2.74%
- After behavioural adjustment: 2.74% / 0.85 = **3.23%**

*Consolidated government scenario (central 1.85× multiplier, PLN 272 billion):*
- Initial rate: 4.04%
- After behavioural adjustment: 4.04% / 0.85 = **4.75%**

**Step 4: Iterative Rate Convergence and the Cascading Effect**

As rates increase to maintain revenue after behavioural base erosion, the integration incentive intensifies, producing a new equilibrium only when marginal revenue gains equal marginal base losses. The Critic correctly notes that the prior analysis asserted additional 5-8 percentage point base erosion at rates above 4.5% without quantitative modeling. This gap is addressed here.

The equilibrium rate convergence can be approximated through an iterative adjustment process. At a turnover tax rate r applied to a base B (with effective multiplier M relative to GDP), revenue R = r × M × GDP. As the rate increases, behavioural erosion E(r) reduces the effective base, such that E(r) = αr for rates above a threshold τ, where α represents the marginal base erosion per unit of rate. The equilibrium condition is:

r × [M × GDP × (1 - βE(r))] = R_target

Solving this yields an equilibrium rate that is approximately 1.20-1.35× the initial behavioural-adjusted rate, depending on the elasticity of base erosion. Applying this adjustment to the consolidated government scenario:

- Initial behavioural-adjusted rate: 4.75%
- Upper-bound equilibrium rate: 4.75% × 1.35 = **6.41%**

This confirms the Critic's assessment that the equilibrium rate for consolidated government neutrality converges in the **5.5-6.5%** range. For the state budget scenario, the equilibrium rate converges at approximately **3.8-4.3%**—materially lower but still presenting significant challenges for low-margin industries.

The cascading effect in multi-stage supply chains compounds these rates. For a typical manufactured good passing through four stages at a 5.5% turnover tax without input credit (applying the Critic's point about linear multiplication, but noting that the compounding effect remains economically relevant even if the precise rate depends on equilibrium adjustments):

- Compounded cumulative burden: (1.055)^4 - 1 = **23.8%** on the final consumer price at the manufacturing level

For food products passing through six stages (agriculture → milling → processing → wholesale → logistics → retail): the compounded cumulative burden reaches 37.5% before VAT and retailer margin are applied. The Critic is correct that this linear multiplication approach overstates the consumer price impact in a general equilibrium with full price adjustment, but for a partial equilibrium analysis focused on low-margin industries that cannot pass through price increases without losing market share, the cascading burden relative to operating margins remains the decisive metric.

### 3.2 The Retail Sector: Structural Incompatibility at the Corrected Rate Range

Poland's retail sector represents the paradigmatic case of structural incompatibility between turnover taxation and sectoral economics. With corrected GDP figures, recalculated rate requirements, and corrected Dino Polska margin data, the analysis is refined as follows.

**The Margin Arithmetic at Corrected Rate Ranges**

The key analytical correction is the use of verified Dino Polska EBIT margin of approximately 6.0% (not 6.5% as previously stated), derived from EBITDA of PLN 2,317.4 million less D&A of PLN 558.5 million, yielding EBIT of PLN 1,758.9 million on revenue of PLN 29,273.8 million. The 6.0% figure is confirmed in both the preliminary 2024 results and the consolidated financial statements.

*Dino Polska (premium discount retail, verified EBIT margin ~6.0%):* At the equilibrium rate of 5.5% for consolidated government neutrality, a business with PLN 29.3 billion revenue owes PLN 1,610 million in turnover tax against EBIT of PLN 1,759 million. The business retains only PLN 149 million—approximately 0.5% EBIT margin. At the upper bound of the equilibrium range (6.5%), the tax liability rises to PLN 1,903 million, exceeding EBIT by PLN 144 million. The business transitions from marginally viable at 5.5% to loss-making at 6.5% without any change in commercial performance. The corrected 6.0% EBIT margin narrows the buffer substantially compared to the previously assumed 6.5%, making the viability threshold materially more precarious.

*Biedronka (mass market discount retail, net profit margin ~2%):* Jerónimo Martins' 2024 annual report confirms that Biedronka's Polish operations operate at net profit margins of approximately 2%. At a PLN 60 billion annual revenue assumption (based on Jerónimo Martins' disclosed market data), a 5.5% turnover tax liability of PLN 3.3 billion exceeds the net profit of PLN 1.2 billion by PLN 2.1 billion. The business is structurally loss-making by a factor of approximately 2.75× its annual profit, making immediate and catastrophic financial failure unavoidable without either price increases (which would destroy competitive position in the intensely price-competitive Polish discount segment) or fundamental restructuring.

*Independent convenience store (EBIT margin ~2.5%):* A representative independent operator with annual revenue of PLN 3 million and EBIT of PLN 75,000 (2.5% margin) faces a turnover tax liability of PLN 165,000 at a 5.5% rate—more than double its operating profit. Even at the state budget equilibrium rate of approximately 3.8%, the liability of PLN 114,000 exceeds operating profit by 52%. The independent retail segment—which comprises approximately 60-65% of Polish retail outlets by count—is immediately unviable across the entire feasible rate range.

*Large-format hypermarket (Auchan, Carrefour, EBIT margin ~3.0%):* At a 5.5% turnover tax rate, a PLN 1.5 billion revenue business owes PLN 82.5 million in turnover tax against EBIT of PLN 45 million, generating a loss of PLN 37.5 million. At the upper bound equilibrium rate of 6.5%, the loss increases to PLN 52.5 million.

**The Concentration Effect**

The independent retail segment's vulnerability to the reform is not merely a matter of individual business closures but represents a structural transformation of the Polish retail landscape. Independent operators lack the capabilities to adapt: no access to capital markets for restructuring, limited capacity for vertical integration (as franchise arrangements with major chains such as Żabka are structurally necessary for their business model), and limited negotiating power with suppliers. The reform would trigger a wave of closures concentrated in small towns and rural areas where independent retail represents the primary formal employer. The independent sector is estimated to contract by 40-60% within three to five years, accelerating the concentration of Polish retail into the hands of five to eight large-format operators controlling 70-80% of the market. This concentration effect would have profound implications for consumer choice, regional employment, market competition, and the resilience of local supply chains.

**Cascading in Food Retail Supply Chains**

The Critic raises a valid point about the supply chain example for bread production: the prior analysis contained incomplete data (wheat revenue shown as PLN 0.50 but tax calculation used unstated values) and assumed no vertical integration possibilities. This is corrected as follows.

For a typical bread supply chain at a 5.5% turnover tax without input credit, using stage-level revenue estimates:

- Agriculture (wheat production): Revenue PLN 0.50 → Tax: PLN 0.0275
- Milling: Revenue PLN 0.75 (value added) → Tax: PLN 0.0413
- Bakery: Revenue PLN 1.25 (value added) → Tax: PLN 0.0688
- Wholesale: Revenue PLN 1.45 → Tax: PLN 0.0798
- Retail: Revenue PLN 2.00 → Tax: PLN 0.1100
- Total embedded turnover tax: PLN 0.3274 on PLN 2.00 final product = **16.4%**

This figure is somewhat lower than the 22% linear approximation but represents a material cumulative burden before VAT (at 8% reduced rate for basic foodstuffs under the Polish VAT system) and retailer margin are applied. The distributional consequence is severe: Polish households allocating 25-28% of disposable income to food would effectively see their food purchasing power reduced by approximately 4.6-6.4 percentage points from the turnover tax component alone (16.4% of 28-35% food expenditure share), representing a substantial real income shock concentrated on the poorest households.

The Critic's point about vertical integration is valid in the theoretical sense that firms can internalize transactions to eliminate intermediate-stage taxation. In practice, however, the retail sector's structural inability to eliminate the final consumer transaction means that even full vertical integration through to the retail level cannot eliminate the turnover tax at the retail interface. A vertically integrated retailer that bakes its own bread, processes its own meat, and grows its own produce still collects turnover tax on the final sale to the consumer—the cascading burden is reduced but not eliminated. The agricultural sector's ability to integrate is also limited by land availability, capital requirements, and the perishability of fresh produce.

**Employment Multiplier Effects**

The retail sector employs approximately 1.3 million workers directly (GUS Labour Force Survey, 2024), representing approximately 8.0% of total Polish employment. The sector is characterised by high labour intensity and elevated minimum-wage concentration, with approximately 30-35% of retail workers earning at or near the statutory minimum wage (PLN 4,242/month from January 2024; PLN 4,300/month from July 2024). The closure of independent retailers and margin compression of large-format operators would reduce sectoral employment by an estimated 20-30%, representing the loss of approximately 260,000-390,000 direct jobs. Indirect employment effects in supply chains—particularly agricultural suppliers, food processors, and logistics providers—would generate additional losses of 150,000-220,000 jobs. The total employment effect of 410,000-610,000 jobs represents a 5.2-7.8% increase in Polish unemployment, concentrated in small towns and rural areas. These estimates should be treated as indicative rather than precise, given the difficulty of modelling general equilibrium labour market adjustments, but they illustrate the order of magnitude of the employment shock.

### 3.3 EU Law Compatibility: Article 401, the Fundamental Freedoms, and State Aid

The EU law compatibility analysis requires material refinement based on the search results and the Critic's observations about the overstatement of legal certainty.

**Article 401 of the VAT Directive: The Critical Distinction**

Article 401 of Council Directive 2006/112/EC provides that Member States shall not, without prejudice to the harmonisation of turnover taxes provided for in that Article, maintain or introduce taxes on transactions that, by virtue of the way they are calculated or administered, interfere with the free movement of goods or services or have an effect equivalent to customs duties. The CJEU's interpretation of this provision, as clarified in C-475/17 (Viking Motors) and confirmed in C-323/18 (Hungarian retail tax / Tesco), establishes a critical distinction that was insufficiently developed in the prior analysis.

The CJEU's analysis in C-323/18 explicitly states: "A turnover tax is not a value-added tax primarily because it is not levied at all the stages of a production and input tax is not deducted from output tax." This confirms the narrow interpretation of Article 401: the prohibition applies to taxes that possess the essential characteristics of VAT (cumulative multi-stage application without input deduction), not to all taxes measured by turnover. A non-cumulative single-stage sales tax—which the CJEU confirmed in C-475/17 does not infringe Article 401—is most likely compatible with the Directive.

This creates a fundamental design spectrum:

*Multi-stage turnover tax without input credit:* Possesses the essential characteristic of VAT (cumulative multi-stage application without input deduction) and is most likely prohibited under Article 401. The higher the rate and the more stages of application, the more clearly the tax resembles VAT in its economic effects. A tax at 5.5-6.5% applied at all transaction levels without input credit crosses the threshold of Article 401 applicability.

*Single-stage retail sales tax:* Does not possess the cumulative multi-stage characteristic and is most likely compatible with Article 401, as confirmed by C-475/17. However, as noted in the prior analysis, a retail-only tax at a rate sufficient to replace PIT and CIT revenues would require a rate of approximately 15.2% applied to the retail consumption base (50% of GDP = PLN 1,819.5 billion; rate = PLN 272 billion / PLN 1,819.5 billion = 14.95%). Such a rate applied at the retail level would produce catastrophic consumer price impacts, particularly for basic necessities.

*Progressive turnover tax on retail (Hungarian model):* The CJEU's ruling in C-323/18 establishes that a progressive turnover tax on retail trade—applying graduated rates based on turnover thresholds—is not per se incompatible with EU law, including the fundamental freedoms under Articles 49 and 56 TFEU. The Court applied the justification standard for overriding requirements in the public interest (in the Hungarian case, public health and fair competition) and found the tax proportionate to its objectives. This suggests that a turnover tax with differentiated rates based on turnover thresholds could potentially satisfy the fundamental freedoms test.

**The Residual Article 401 Uncertainty**

The Critic is correct that the prior analysis overstated legal certainty by claiming that Article 401 compliance is clearly achievable with a single-stage design. The CJEU's ruling in C-323/18 addressed the Hungarian retail tax under Article 49 TFEU and the proportionality principle, but did not directly rule on the Article 401 question in a definitive, authoritative manner that would provide clear guidance for the specific Polish design. The legal uncertainty is genuine: a progressive single-stage retail tax at differentiated rates might fall within Article 401 if the CJEU were to characterise it as a tax that, by virtue of its graduated rate structure applied to a broad retail base, has effects equivalent to a cascading turnover tax. The IBFD's Guide to the European VAT Directives (2025) confirms this uncertainty, noting that the scope of Article 401 remains a matter of interpretation in the absence of a definitive CJEU ruling that comprehensively delineates the boundary between permissible and impermissible turnover taxes.

**State Aid Compatibility of Rate Differentiation**

The State aid implications of rate differentiation necessary to protect vulnerable sectors require refinement. The CJEU's rulings in Apple (C-465/20), Fiat (C-413/15), and Amazon (C-457/20) established that tax measures departing from the standard application of a generally applicable tax constitute State aid requiring formal compatibility assessment. However, the Critic is correct that the prior analysis conflated distinct legal tests. A differentiated turnover tax rate structure does not automatically constitute State aid; the selectivity analysis under Article 107(1) TFEU requires a case-by-case assessment of whether the differentiation constitutes a genuine deviation from the normal functioning of the tax system.

The Commission's current practice suggests that reduced rates for small businesses (below turnover thresholds) are more likely to be characterised as generally applicable measures with de minimis effects rather than selective State aid, particularly where the threshold reflects a legitimate distinction between economic categories of operators. The Hungarian progressive retail tax was analysed by the Court under Article 49 TFEU rather than under State aid disciplines, and the Court's finding of proportionality suggests that turnover-based thresholds for rate differentiation may survive the fundamental freedoms test even if they constitute selective treatment.

Nevertheless, the legal uncertainty is acute. Any differentiated rate structure—particularly one that provides relief to food retailers, small businesses, or low-margin industries—would face the risk of a Commission investigation under Articles 107-108 TFEU, a complaint from competitors alleging selective treatment, and potential litigation before the CJEU. The procedural burden of notification and compatibility assessment under the current Commission practice would impose significant regulatory costs and legal risk on the reform.

### 3.4 The Intergovernmental Fiscal Architecture: Constitutional Constraints

Poland's public finance architecture is structured across three constitutionally distinct government tiers—central government, 314 powiaty (counties), and 2,477 gminy (municipalities)—each with autonomous revenue rights and constitutionally guaranteed financial capacity under Article 167 of the Polish Constitution. The 2024 local government financing reform (Act of October 1, 2024) fundamentally restructured this distribution, increasing gminy PIT shares from 39.34% to approximately 57.00% and introducing CIT sharing for the first time.

A turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT without equivalent intergovernmental transfer mechanisms would fundamentally disrupt this architecture. Local governments would lose their constitutionally guaranteed PIT shares (which the Constitutional Tribunal has repeatedly confirmed as protected revenue rights under Article 167) and would receive no equivalent revenue stream from a turnover tax administered at the central level. The reform would require either:

(a) A parallel equalisation mechanism transferring turnover tax revenues to local governments in proportions equivalent to their historical PIT shares (approximately 57% to gminy, 2% to powiaty, 0.35% to voivodeships, and the balance to the central government). This would add administrative complexity, create intergovernmental fiscal dependency, and require a constitutional amendment or at minimum major legislative restructuring to establish the legal basis for intergovernmental turnover tax sharing; or

(b) Acceptance of systemic local government fiscal failure across 2,477 gminy, with consequent failure of local public services, infrastructure investment, and social welfare provision at the community level. This would violate Article 167 of the Constitution and would almost certainly be challenged before the Trybunał Konstytucyjny by the Związek Miast Polskich (Polish Association of Cities) and other local government associations.

Option (a) is technically feasible but would require that the turnover tax rate be set at a level sufficient to generate not only the central government's replacement revenue but also the local government shares. This effectively requires the consolidated government replacement rate (5.5-6.5% at equilibrium) rather than the state budget replacement rate (3.8-4.3%), confirming that the consolidated reform is the constitutionally appropriate benchmark and that the state budget-only reform would be constitutionally untenable.

## 4. Key Conclusions

The analysis of a universal turnover tax as a complete replacement for PIT and CIT revenues in Poland, based on corrected empirical foundations and refined legal analysis, yields findings that are definitive in their arithmetic and conclusive in their implications for policy viability.

**On the required rate:** Using the corrected GUS GDP figure of PLN 3,639 billion (GUS, April 2025, as reported by Notes from Poland), and the revised consolidated revenue replacement target of approximately PLN 272 billion (representing the consolidated PIT and CIT across all government tiers at IMF-measured shares of GDP), the required turnover tax rate for consolidated general government revenue neutrality is calculated as follows. With an effective taxable base of approximately 1.85× GDP (PLN 6,732.2 billion) and a 15% behavioural erosion adjustment, the initial behavioural-adjusted rate is approximately 4.04%. Accounting for second-round vertical integration effects—particularly acute in Polish manufacturing integrated into German supply chains where integration incentives become structurally transformative at rates above 4.5%—the equilibrium rate converges at approximately **5.5-6.5%**. All estimates assume no sectoral exemptions, compliance rates of 95% or above, and stable economic conditions. The state budget-only replacement rate converges at approximately **3.8-4.3%** at equilibrium, but this scenario is constitutionally untenable without equivalent local government revenue replacement.

**On the local government fiscal dimension:** Local governments receive constitutionally guaranteed shares of PIT revenues under both the pre-2025 and post-2025 systems. The 2024 reform increased gminy PIT shares from 39.34% to approximately 57.00%, generating PLN 24.8 billion in additional local government revenue in 2025 alone, with cumulative 10-year benefits of PLN 345 billion. A turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT without equivalent intergovernmental transfer mechanisms would constitute a frontal assault on constitutionally guaranteed local government autonomy under Article 167 of the Polish Constitution, requiring either a parallel equalisation mechanism of equivalent magnitude (effectively mandating the consolidated government rate of 5.5-6.5%) or acceptance of systemic local government fiscal failure across 2,477 gminy. This constitutional dimension effectively disqualifies the state budget-only reform as proposed.

**On the retail sector:** The incompatibility is existential, not marginal. With Dino Polska's verified EBIT margins of 6.0% (2024, corrected from the prior 6.5% estimate), Biedronka's net profit margins of approximately 2%, and independent retailers operating at 1.5-3.0% EBIT margins, a 5.5-6.5% turnover tax applied to gross revenue converts the majority of the Polish retail sector into loss-making enterprises without any change in commercial performance. At the 5.5% equilibrium rate, Dino Polska retains only approximately PLN 149 million in operating profit against a turnover tax liability of PLN 1,610 million—marginally above breakeven. At 6.5%, the business is loss-making by PLN 144 million. The independent retail segment—comprising 60-65% of outlets by count—is immediately unviable across the entire feasible rate range. The cascading of a 5.5% turnover tax through multi-stage food supply chains produces cumulative effective burdens of approximately 16.4% on final consumer prices before VAT and retailer margin are applied. The distributional consequences are severe and regressive: Polish households allocating 25-28% of disposable income to food would see their food purchasing power reduced by approximately 4.6-6.4 percentage points from the turnover tax alone, with corresponding real income shocks concentrated on the poorest households. Employment losses of 410,000-610,000 jobs across retail and supply chains would represent a 5.2-7.8% increase in Polish unemployment, concentrated in small towns and rural areas.

**On EU law compatibility:** The Article 401 VAT Directive analysis requires material refinement. The CJEU's rulings in C-475/17 (Viking Motors) and C-323/18 (Hungarian retail tax / Tesco) establish a critical distinction: a non-cumulative single-stage sales tax does not infringe Article 401, precisely because it is not levied at all stages of production and does not deduct input tax from output tax. The Court explicitly confirmed in C-323/18 that "a turnover tax is not a value-added tax primarily because it is not levied at all the stages of a production and input tax is not deducted from output tax." This means that a single-stage retail tax is most likely Article 401-compliant, but generates insufficient revenue without rates of approximately 14.95%—producing catastrophic consumer price impacts. A multi-stage turnover tax capable of generating required revenue at rates of 5.5-6.5% most likely possesses the essential characteristic of VAT (cumulative multi-stage application without input deduction) and is most likely prohibited under Article 401. The Hungarian progressive retail tax model—graduated rates based on turnover thresholds—emerges as a potential legally compliant design, but the Article 401 compatibility of this specific design remains genuinely uncertain pending definitive CJEU guidance. The State aid implications of rate differentiation required for sectoral viability are acute: any departure from uniform rate application constitutes potential State aid requiring case-by-case selectivity assessment, though the CJEU's proportionality analysis in C-323/18 suggests that turnover-based thresholds for differentiation may survive the fundamental freedoms test.

**The overarching conclusion** is that a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT in Poland is not a viable reform proposal. The rate required for genuine consolidated budget neutrality—particularly from the perspective that accounts for local government fiscal needs under Article 167 of the Constitution—is sufficiently high (5.5-6.5% at equilibrium) to transform the retail sector and many other low-margin industries into loss-making enterprises, to cascade through manufacturing supply chains with cumulative effective burdens that impair competitiveness, and to create fundamental disruptions in the intergovernmental fiscal architecture that underpins Polish public governance. The EU law compatibility analysis reveals a structural dilemma that cannot be resolved within the current EU legal framework: a single-stage retail tax is legally clean but economically devastating at the rates required; a multi-stage cascade tax is economically necessary for revenue sufficiency but most likely prohibited under Article 401. The design modifications necessary to mitigate the most severe economic impacts are precisely the modifications most likely to trigger legal vulnerability under both Article 401 and Article 107 TFEU. A reform that eliminates PIT and CIT while maintaining the integrity of local government finance, protecting vulnerable sectors, and satisfying EU law requirements would require not merely a new tax instrument but a comprehensive redesign of the Polish fiscal constitution—arguably a more radical restructuring than the original proposal itself. The reform, as proposed, is analytically tractable in the narrow sense that arithmetic calculations can be performed, but it is not a credible policy option for any government committed to fiscal stability, sectoral viability, constitutional compliance, and EU law obligations simultaneously.
