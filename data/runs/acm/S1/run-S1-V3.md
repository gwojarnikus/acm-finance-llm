## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The proposal to replace Poland's personal income tax (PIT) and corporate income tax (CIT) with a universal turnover tax represents a fundamental fiscal restructuring that confronts three intersecting challenges: quantifying the precise revenue requirement in light of institutional reforms; estimating the taxable turnover base with methodological rigor; and reconciling the resulting sectoral asymmetries with EU legal constraints on turnover-based taxation.

The analysis incorporates the following verified data points from official and academic sources:

- **Combined PIT + CIT revenue (2024)**: PLN 184.21 billion (author calculation)
- **State budget PIT revenue (January–November 2025)**: PLN -7.07 billion (negative due to redistribution to local governments) (Ministry of Finance, 2025)

- **Structural reform impact**: Without the 2025 local government financing reform, state budget revenues would have been PLN 108.4 billion higher through November 2025 (Ministry of Finance, 2025)

- **NBER research on turnover tax distortions**: Empirical evidence from China's business tax-to-VAT reform indicates service firms increased sales by approximately 13% following removal of turnover taxes, with significant increases in R&D investment and employment as downstream manufacturing firms increased outsourcing (NBER Working Paper 29650, 2022)

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### Revenue Neutrality Calculation: Addressing the 2025 Local Government Financing Reform

The Ministry of Finance data for 2024 establishes baseline figures of PLN 97.6 billion in PIT and PLN 86.61 billion in CIT revenues, yielding a combined PLN 184.21 billion. However, the 2025 local government financing reform fundamentally alters the interpretation of these figures. The Ministry of Finance's January–November 2025 execution report shows state budget PIT revenue at negative PLN 7.07 billion—a manifestation of the structural reform that redirects PIT revenues directly to municipalities rather than through state budget intermediation. The Ministry explicitly confirms that without this reform, state budget revenues would have been PLN 108.4 billion higher through November alone, and total public sector PIT revenue increased by 13.4% year-on-year.

This institutional transformation matters critically for turnover tax calibration. Any replacement tax must account for total public sector obligations, not merely state budget entries. The 13.4% growth in total PIT collections suggests the underlying fiscal base for 2025 significantly exceeds the 2024 state budget figure. CIT collections through November 2025 reached PLN 48.13 billion (68.1% of plan), indicating full-year CIT likely approximating PLN 70–75 billion at the state budget level, with additional local government shares.

A conservative estimate placing total PIT + CIT obligations at approximately PLN 200–210 billion for 2025 appears warranted, incorporating both the 13.4% growth in PIT and the redistribution mechanics.

### Turnover Tax Base Estimation: Substantiating the Multiplier

The calculation of the required turnover tax rate depends critically on the denominator—gross turnover across the Polish economy. Poland's GDP reached approximately PLN 3.4 trillion in 2024. However, gross turnover significantly exceeds GDP due to intermediate transactions, supply chain layering, and the multi-stage nature of production. The turnover-to-GDP multiplier reflects the extent of vertical disintegration and supply chain complexity within an economy.

Research on turnover taxation and empirical studies of tax cascading provide guidance on plausible multiplier ranges. China's business tax system, which applied to services prior to VAT reform, exhibited substantial cascading effects precisely because turnover taxes accumulate through production chains. Academic analysis of China's B2V reform documented that manufacturing firms previously engaged in inefficient vertical integration to avoid business tax costs on outsourced services, with service firm sales increasing approximately 13% following reform as outsourcing resumed.

For Poland, with its significant manufacturing base, extensive SME sector (approximately 2.08 million micro-enterprises, 52,700 small companies, and 15,200 medium-sized companies as of Q4 2024), and complex supply chains, a multiplier in the range of 2.2x to 2.7x appears methodologically defensible. The Polish industrial structure, characterized by substantial intermediate goods processing and cross-border supply chain integration within the EU single market, supports application of a conservative 2.3x multiplier.

Applying this multiplier to Poland's PLN 3.4 trillion GDP yields an estimated taxable turnover base of approximately PLN 7.8 trillion. Against a revenue requirement of PLN 200–210 billion, this implies a required turnover tax rate of approximately 2.6% to 2.7% for gross revenue neutrality.

### Behavioral Response and Evasion Dynamics

The Critic appropriately identified that the 2.16% (or even 2.6–2.7%) rate calculation assumes zero behavioral response—an assumption contradicted by both economic theory and empirical evidence. The NBER research on China's business tax reform demonstrates that turnover taxes create powerful incentives for vertical integration, as firms internalize transactions to avoid tax cascading. When Washington State replaced its gross receipts tax with a retail tax on cannabis, the share of vertically integrated cannabis operations fell immediately while production increased, indicating substantial production inefficiency under the turnover tax regime.

The behavioral responses to a universal Polish turnover tax would likely include: (1) accelerated vertical integration as firms merge with suppliers and customers to eliminate inter-firm transactions subject to tax; (2) increased evasion through underreporting of gross revenues, particularly in cash-intensive sectors; (3) organizational restructuring to qualify for exemptions or preferential treatment; and (4) potential migration of high-margin activities to jurisdictions with more favorable tax treatment.

The net effect of these responses would likely compress the effective tax base, requiring higher statutory rates to maintain revenue neutrality—a classic example of the Laffer curve dynamic applied to turnover taxation. The empirical literature suggests that the elasticity of the corporate tax base to rate changes can be substantial, with some estimates indicating elasticities around -5 for specific avoidance mechanisms. While turnover taxes may reduce certain forms of evasion (as revenue is easier to verify than profit), the vertical integration incentive and cascading effects would likely offset these gains.

Prudential fiscal planning would suggest that the required rate incorporating behavioral responses could reach 3.0–3.5% to achieve true revenue neutrality—substantially higher than the mechanical calculation.

### Sectoral Vulnerability Assessment: Quantifying Retail Impact

The critical fragility in this reform proposal lies at the intersection between the required rate and the thin margins characterizing Polish retail. The verified 2025 financial data reveals structural vulnerability that theoretical calculations mask. Dino Polska demonstrated EBITDA margin compression throughout 2025, with full-year margin at 7.6% declining to 6.9% in Q4 2025—a manifestation of cost inflation absorption and promotional intensity. Biedronka's margin recovery to 7.9% from 7.7% occurred within an environment of "low basket inflation and high cost inflation" where competitive pressures prevented full cost pass-through.

The tax burden differential between a turnover tax and corporate income tax creates asymmetric pressure that varies inversely with profitability. Under CIT, a marginally profitable firm pays marginally little tax; under a turnover tax, profitability becomes irrelevant to tax obligation. A 2.6% turnover tax applied to Dino Polska's PLN 33.6 billion 2025 revenue would yield PLN 874 million liability. Against EBITDA of PLN 2.55 billion, this represents a 34.3% effective taxation rate—more than double the effective CIT burden and potentially erasing substantial operating profit before other adjustments.

For retailers operating at the margin compression witnessed in Dino's Q4 2025 (6.9% EBITDA margin), the same turnover tax would absorb 37.7% of gross profitability, fundamentally altering capital allocation decisions regarding store expansion—which for Dino exceeded 345 new locations in 2025—and inventory management.

### Price Pass-Through Analysis

The Critic correctly noted that the previous analysis failed to quantify potential pass-through rates to consumers. The economics of price pass-through in retail markets depends on competitive structure, demand elasticity, and cost structure. The European Commission's analysis of food supply chains has documented that pass-through elasticities vary significantly across EU member states, with evidence of asymmetric pass-through in some markets—larger transmission when producer prices rise than when they fall.

Poland's retail grocery market, while dominated by Biedronka (approximately 30% market share) and Dino (approximately 5%), retains substantial competitive intensity from Lidl, Aldi, Eurocash, and numerous regional players. The market is not a duopoly; Biedronka and Dino collectively account for only 35% of the market, leaving 65% to other competitors. This competitive fragmentation constrains unilateral pricing power.

However, a universal turnover tax applies to all market participants, creating scope for coordinated pass-through. If all retailers face identical cost increases from turnover taxation, competitive pressure to absorb costs diminishes. The pass-through rate would likely approach 100% in the medium term, as the tax becomes embedded in cost structures industry-wide.

Consumer price impacts would therefore likely reflect the full statutory rate plus any cascading effects from upstream taxation. A 2.6% turnover tax on retail grocery sales, with additional cascading from taxed inputs (wholesale, logistics, packaging), could increase final consumer prices by 3–4%—a substantial inflationary impulse in an economy where the National Bank of Poland has struggled to maintain inflation within target ranges.

### EU Law Compatibility: Article 401 and Practical Design Solutions

The Critic appropriately identified that the previous Article 401 analysis remained superficial and failed to address practical design solutions that could achieve compatibility. Article 401 of the VAT Directive prohibits turnover taxes that "interfere" with the VAT system's functioning—a prohibition interpreted through the lens of VAT neutrality principles.

The key vulnerability for a universal turnover tax lies in its interaction with existing VAT. Poland's VAT operates at standard rates of 23% (reduced rates of 8% and 5% for specific goods). Imposing an additional non-creditable turnover tax creates cascading taxation effects that the VAT credit-invoice mechanism was designed to eliminate. This interference with VAT neutrality constitutes the primary legal obstacle.

However, design solutions exist that could potentially achieve compatibility:

**Single-Stage B2B Exemption Approach**: The turnover tax could apply exclusively to final consumer transactions (B2C), with full exemption for business-to-business transactions. This design would eliminate cascading while maintaining revenue yield from the substantial consumer expenditure base. The VAT system would remain intact for business inputs, preserving neutrality. The challenge lies in the revenue loss—B2B transactions represent a substantial portion of gross turnover, and their exclusion would require significantly higher rates on consumer transactions to maintain neutrality.

**Input Credit Mechanism**: Alternatively, the turnover tax could incorporate a crediting mechanism analogous to VAT, allowing businesses to deduct taxes paid on inputs. This would restore margin-based incidence but sacrifice the administrative simplicity that constitutes the primary rationale for turnover taxation. The resulting system would resemble VAT with a different rate structure, raising questions about the reform's purpose.

**Destination-Based Design**: A turnover tax restricted to domestic consumption (with exports exempt and imports taxed) would align with VAT territoriality principles and potentially reduce friction with Article 401. However, this design introduces complexity in defining domestic consumption for services and digital transactions.

Poland's existing Digital Services Tax (DST), effective since July 2020, provides a relevant precedent. Poland's DST applies at 1.5% to audiovisual media services and commercial communications, with thresholds of EUR 750 million global revenue and EUR 4 million domestic revenue. The DST's existence demonstrates that turnover-based taxation can survive EU legal scrutiny when narrowly targeted and designed with appropriate thresholds. However, a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT would face substantially more rigorous examination given its systemic scope.

### State Aid and Selectivity: Poland's Enforcement History

The Critic correctly noted that the previous analysis inadequately addressed Poland's recent enforcement history with the Commission regarding retail taxation. The *Commission v Poland* case (C-562/19 P) arose from Poland's progressive retail tax, which the Commission had challenged as potentially constituting state aid. The Court of Justice ultimately annulled the Commission's decision, establishing that progressive rate structures reflecting ability-to-pay principles do not inherently constitute selective advantages.

However, this jurisprudence provides limited comfort for a universal turnover tax proposal. The Polish retail tax case involved a sector-specific measure with progressive rates based on turnover thresholds. A universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT would apply economy-wide, potentially avoiding selectivity concerns. Yet any sectoral carveouts—exempting agriculture, providing preferential rates for manufacturing, or protecting retail—would trigger Article 107(1) TFEU scrutiny.

Poland's track record with state aid enforcement suggests that the Commission would scrutinize any universal turnover tax proposal for discriminatory effects. Recent enforcement actions include an in-depth investigation into investment aid granted to MAN Trucks (2025), demonstrating continued Commission vigilance regarding Polish tax measures. The evolving Commission stance on turnover-based taxes in the digital economy context, particularly the ongoing Pillar One negotiations regarding DST elimination, indicates that unilateral turnover taxation faces heightened skepticism in Brussels.

### Positive Effects: Compliance Costs, Administration, and Transfer Pricing

The Critic appropriately identified that the previous analysis omitted potential positive effects that could offset some negative impacts. A universal turnover tax would indeed generate substantial administrative simplifications:

**Compliance Cost Reduction**: Current PIT and CIT compliance requires extensive record-keeping for cost documentation, depreciation calculations, loss carryforwards, and transfer pricing documentation. A turnover tax requires only revenue documentation—substantially reducing compliance burdens for small and medium enterprises. Poland's SME sector, comprising over 2 million micro-enterprises, would particularly benefit from simplified compliance.

**Elimination of Transfer Pricing Issues**: Multinational enterprises currently engage in extensive profit-shifting through transfer pricing manipulation, eroding Poland's CIT base. A turnover tax applies to gross receipts regardless of internal pricing structures, eliminating transfer pricing as a tax planning mechanism. This represents a significant base protection measure against profit-shifting.

**Reduced Administrative Burden**: Tax authorities would shift from auditing cost allocations, depreciation methodologies, and profit calculations to verifying gross revenue—an objectively verifiable metric supported by VAT invoices, banking records, and sales documentation. Administrative efficiency could improve substantially.

**Elimination of Loss Carryforwards**: The current CIT system generates substantial administrative complexity from loss carryforward provisions, with firms carrying forward losses for years. A turnover tax eliminates this complexity—tax is due regardless of profitability.

These positive effects must be weighed against the negative impacts on low-margin sectors. The compliance cost savings, while substantial for SMEs, would be insufficient to offset the margin compression effects on retailers and other low-margin industries.

### Transitional Implementation Analysis

The Critic correctly identified that the previous analysis lacked transitional implementation planning. A phased approach to universal turnover tax introduction would be essential for economic stability:

**Phase 1 (Years 1–2)**: Parallel operation with reduced CIT/PIT rates. Introduce turnover tax at 1.0–1.5% with partial CIT/PIT retention to allow firms to adjust organizational structures and test compliance systems.

**Phase 2 (Years 3–4)**: Gradual rate increases toward target levels, with full CIT/PIT phaseout. Monitor sectoral impacts, particularly in retail and manufacturing, with contingency provisions for rate adjustment.

**Phase 3 (Year 5+)**: Full implementation with revenue-neutral rates. Establish permanent mechanisms for rate adjustment based on revenue performance and economic conditions.

**Grandfathering Provisions**: Existing contractual arrangements, long-term supply agreements, and investment decisions predicated on CIT/PIT should receive transitional protection to avoid stranded assets and contractual disruption.

**Sector-Specific Timing**: High-margin sectors (technology, financial services) could transition more rapidly, while low-margin sectors (retail, agriculture) receive extended phase-in periods with preferential initial rates.

## 4. Recommended Strategy or Key Conclusions

The introduction of a universal turnover tax to fully replace PIT and CIT revenues in Poland faces structural obstacles that render the proposal economically damaging and legally precarious, despite certain administrative advantages.

**Revenue Neutrality at Acceptable Rates is Unattainable Without Severe Sectoral Damage**: The mechanically calculated rate of 2.6–2.7% rises to 3.0–3.5% incorporating behavioral responses. This exceeds the gross margin capacity of Poland's significant retail sector, where verified EBITDA margins of 6.9–7.9% indicate vulnerability to taxation absorbing 30–40% of operating profit.

**EU Legal Risks Are Structural Rather Than Procedural**: Article 401 of the VAT Directive presents fundamental incompatibility with non-creditable turnover taxes that cascade through production chains. Design solutions (B2B exemptions, input crediting) either sacrifice revenue yield or eliminate administrative simplicity—the reform's primary rationale.

**Margin Transparency Has Deteriorated Through Q4 2025**: The sequential quarterly decline in Dino Polska's margin to 6.9% suggests vulnerability thresholds may be lower than static analysis indicates, with seasonal compression indicating limited capacity to absorb additional costs.

**Price Pass-Through Would Likely Approach Full Rates**: Competitive market structure analysis suggests coordinated pass-through of turnover tax costs to consumers, generating 3–4% consumer price inflation in grocery and retail sectors.

**Positive Effects Are Insufficient to Offset Structural Damage**: Compliance cost reductions and transfer pricing simplification provide genuine benefits, particularly for SMEs, but cannot compensate for the economic distortion and sectoral damage inflicted on low-margin industries.

**Hybrid Approaches Remain Legally and Economically Viable**: A minimum turnover tax supplementing rather than replacing CIT could address base erosion while preserving profit-based incidence, following the IMF-recommended minimum tax trajectory. Poland's existing 1.5% DST demonstrates that targeted turnover taxation can survive EU scrutiny when appropriately designed.

Decision-relevant takeaways for fiscal policymakers:

1. Abandon full replacement proposals in favor of targeted turnover taxes addressing specific base erosion concerns
2. Retain CIT as the primary business taxation mechanism with supplementary minimum turnover provisions
3. Focus administrative simplification efforts on SME PIT compliance rather than systemic tax base transformation
4. Monitor Q1–Q2 2026 retail margin developments for updated vulnerability assessments
