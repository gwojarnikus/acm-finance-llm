# Introducing a Universal Turnover Tax into the Polish Tax System: A Comprehensive Analysis

## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The central challenge under examination is whether Poland could structurally transform its direct tax architecture by replacing Personal Income Tax (PIT) and Corporate Income Tax (CIT) with a single, universal turnover tax, while maintaining fiscal neutrality, avoiding catastrophic impacts on low-margin industries, and complying with the constraints imposed by European Union law and the OECD Pillar Two framework. This reform proposal raises fundamental questions about tax design theory, economic efficiency, distributional equity, the treatment of local government revenues, and the boundaries of national fiscal sovereignty within the EU legal order. Three critical gaps identified in prior analysis require particular attention: the internal consistency of the fiscal data foundation, the structural double-taxation problem arising from the interaction between PIT on wages and turnover tax on business revenues, and the specification of intergovernmental revenue distribution mechanisms under the proposed reform.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### 3.1 The Current Architecture of Poland's Direct Tax System and Intergovernmental Fiscal Relations

Poland's personal income tax and corporate income tax constitute not merely revenue-raising instruments but foundational elements of the nation's fiscal covenant with its citizens and enterprises. Their significance extends across multiple dimensions: they serve as instruments of horizontal and vertical equity, embodying the principle that those with greater economic capacity should contribute proportionally more to public finances; they function as automatic stabilisers, moderating economic cycles through their sensitivity to changes in income and profits; and they express societal choices about the appropriate distribution of the tax burden across different forms of income, factors of production, and categories of economic actors.

The intergovernmental fiscal architecture represents a particularly critical dimension that the Critic correctly identifies as requiring explicit treatment. The 2025 local government financing reform fundamentally altered the distribution of PIT revenues between the central state budget and local government units, making any analysis of tax replacement incomplete without addressing this dimension. This section provides the detailed evaluation that was previously insufficiently developed.

Personal Income Tax in Poland operates through a progressive rate structure, with rates ranging from 12% to 32% depending on income brackets. The 2025 State Budget Act planned state budget PIT revenues of PLN 31.736 billion, but the actual execution reached only PLN 28.752 billion (90.6% of plan). This shortfall does not represent a deterioration in tax collection; rather, it reflects a fundamental structural reform of Poland's intergovernmental fiscal relations that shifted the effective PIT burden from state budget revenues to municipal revenues.

Under the post-2025 reform framework, the allocation of PIT revenues follows a differentiated structure. Approximately 73.6% of total PIT revenues are transferred directly to municipalities (gminy), while the central state budget retains approximately 26.4%. This means that of the total estimated PIT revenue of approximately PLN 109.2 billion, approximately PLN 80.4 billion flows to local government units and approximately PLN 28.8 billion is retained by the state budget. The reform significantly strengthened the fiscal capacity of municipalities, which bear primary responsibility for the delivery of local public services including education, local infrastructure, and social assistance. The approximately 2,478 municipalities (as of 2025) receive these revenues on the basis of the origin principle—revenues are distributed to the municipality where the taxpayer resides, creating a direct link between local economic activity and local fiscal capacity.

Corporate Income Tax, set at a standard rate of 19% with certain sector-specific modifications, generated state budget revenues of PLN 70.718 billion in the 2025 budget plan and approximately PLN 63.7 billion in actual execution. While CIT is primarily a state budget revenue, a portion is shared with local governments under specific conditions related to investment incentives and special economic zones. The primary CIT allocation remains with the central government.

The combined revenue picture for a turnover tax replacement must therefore address not merely the aggregate revenue requirement but also the distribution mechanism between state and local governments. This is not a secondary consideration but a central design question that determines the feasibility and political viability of any reform.

**Three Principal Options for Intergovernmental Revenue Distribution Under a Turnover Tax:**

The first option involves a fixed percentage allocation to local governments, mirroring the current PIT arrangement. Under this approach, a specified percentage of turnover tax revenues—potentially 73.6% to maintain the current balance, or some alternative percentage negotiated in political bargaining—would be distributed to municipalities. The advantage of this approach is administrative simplicity: the existing PIT distribution infrastructure could be adapted with minimal modification. However, this approach raises equity concerns because turnover tax base is unevenly distributed across municipalities, with larger urban centres and industrial areas generating disproportionate revenues relative to their service delivery needs.

The second option involves a formula-based distribution tied to population, fiscal capacity, and service responsibilities. This approach would allocate turnover tax revenues according to a needs-based formula that accounts for differences in municipal populations, demographic characteristics (age composition affecting education and social service demands), infrastructure requirements, and fiscal capacity indicators. Such a formula would better align revenues with service delivery responsibilities but would require extensive political negotiation over the weighting of each factor and would likely face opposition from high-fiscal-capacity municipalities that would receive less under a needs-based formula than under the origin principle.

The third option involves revenue sharing through the general government allocation mechanism, whereby turnover tax revenues flow entirely to the central government and local government entitlements are addressed through the general purpose grant system. This would centralise the tax but would require compensating transfers to local governments through the Regional Development Fund or similar instruments. The political economy of this approach is complex: municipalities would lose direct control over a revenue source they have come to expect, and the compensating transfers would become subjects of annual budgetary negotiations with uncertain outcomes.

A fourth hybrid option involves allowing municipalities to levy a local surtax on the national turnover tax base, analogous to the local income tax surcharges that exist in some jurisdictions. This would maintain national uniformity in the base rate while allowing local governments to adjust the effective burden within their jurisdictions. However, this would reintroduce complexity and compliance costs that the turnover tax proposal was intended to eliminate.

The fiscal magnitude of this question is substantial. At the estimated turnover tax rate of approximately 4.5% applied to GDP, total turnover tax revenues of approximately PLN 172.9 billion would be distributed. If municipalities received 73.6% under the first option, their allocation would be approximately PLN 127.2 billion—a significant increase from their current PIT allocation of approximately PLN 80.4 billion. This increase would represent a major fiscal transfer to local governments but would need to be weighed against the loss of the equity and progressivity properties that the PIT system provides. Moreover, if the turnover tax were applied as a state budget revenue only (with local government distribution handled separately), the state budget replacement target would be lower, requiring different political negotiations.

The political economy dimension cannot be overstated. The 2025 reform that shifted PIT revenues toward municipalities was the product of extensive negotiations between the central government and the municipal associations. Any reform that altered this balance would reopen these negotiations and would likely face resistance from local government lobby groups. The Związek Miast Polskich (Association of Polish Cities) and Unia Miastecz Polskich have historically been strong advocates for fiscal decentralisation and would resist any reform that reduced municipal fiscal autonomy.

### 3.2 The Turnover Tax Concept: Theoretical Foundations and Structural Implications

A universal turnover tax, in its pure form, represents a levy on the total value of goods sold or services rendered by a business, measured without deduction for the cost of inputs. This distinguishes it fundamentally from Value Added Tax, which operates through a credit mechanism allowing registered businesses to deduct input taxes paid on purchases, thereby taxing only the value added at each stage of production and distribution. A turnover tax levied on gross revenues without input deduction is termed a "cascading tax," because the tax burden accumulates as goods and services pass through successive stages of the production and distribution chain, with each stage bearing tax on inputs that cannot be recovered.

The theoretical appeal of turnover taxation centres on administrative simplicity and comprehensive coverage. The tax base—total sales revenues—is readily measurable and verifiable through commercial documentation, potentially reducing compliance costs compared to an income-based system requiring complex depreciation schedules, loss carryforwards, and transfer pricing adjustments. The tax would capture economic activity regardless of profitability, thereby addressing the phenomenon of "zero-tax" entities that generate substantial economic output without taxable profits—a concern particularly salient in the context of transfer pricing by multinational enterprises.

However, the theoretical advantages of turnover taxation are substantially offset by significant economic distortions that have been extensively analysed in the public finance literature. The cascading effect creates a systematic bias toward vertical integration, as businesses that consolidate stages of production avoid the multiple taxation of intermediate transactions. A manufacturer that integrates forward into distribution avoids the turnover tax that would otherwise apply to the distribution margin, creating a competitive advantage for integrated operations that has nothing to do with underlying efficiency. This distortion penalises specialisation and the division of labour, which Adam Smith identified as the fundamental drivers of economic productivity.

Furthermore, a turnover tax is inherently regressive when viewed through the lens of distributional impact. While it does not discriminate between high-margin and low-margin activities in nominal tax liability, its impact on final consumers is regressive: lower-income households spend a higher proportion of their income on consumption, meaning that a consumption-based tax represents a larger share of income for poorer households. The shift from progressive income taxation toward regressive consumption taxation represents a fundamental change in the social contract underlying the tax system.

**The Critical Issue of Double Taxation: PIT on Wages and Turnover Tax on Business Revenues**

The Critic correctly identifies a structural flaw in the turnover tax replacement that was insufficiently addressed in the previous analysis: the phenomenon of multiple taxation within the turnover tax framework itself. PIT is paid by employees on wages, and wages constitute a cost component embedded in business turnover. If employees continue to pay PIT on wages under a replacement scenario—as would be necessary to maintain any element of progressivity—the same economic income would be subject to cascading taxation at multiple levels: first through PIT on wages paid to employees, and subsequently through turnover tax on business revenues that include those wages as a cost component.

This creates a structural tension between the goal of replacing income taxes with turnover tax while maintaining any semblance of horizontal equity or economic efficiency. The analysis must now address this problem with the specificity it requires.

Consider a simplified production chain: a manufacturer produces a component at a cost of PLN 80 (including labour, materials, and capital costs) and sells it for PLN 100 to an assembler. The assembler incurs additional costs of PLN 40 and sells the final product for PLN 150 to a retailer. The retailer incurs distribution costs of PLN 30 and sells to the final consumer for PLN 200.

Under the current system: The manufacturer pays CIT on its profit of PLN 20 (assuming a 19% rate, CIT = PLN 3.80). The assembler pays CIT on its profit of PLN 10 (CIT = PLN 1.90). The retailer pays CIT on its profit of PLN 20 (CIT = PLN 3.80). Employees throughout the chain pay PIT on their wages, which is a final tax on their income and does not cascade.

Under a gross turnover tax replacing both PIT and CIT: The manufacturer pays turnover tax of PLN 4.50 (4.5% of PLN 100). The assembler pays turnover tax of PLN 6.75 (4.5% of PLN 150). The retailer pays turnover tax of PLN 9.00 (4.5% of PLN 200). Total turnover tax burden = PLN 20.25. The final consumer pays PLN 200 plus the embedded taxes.

But consider the position of employees under this scenario. If PIT is truly eliminated and replaced by turnover tax, employees would need to receive higher pre-tax wages to compensate for the loss of after-tax income. However, if PIT is maintained for employees while turnover tax is added for businesses—a mixed scenario—the double-taxation problem emerges.

Suppose the manufacturer employs workers whose total wages are PLN 40. Under PIT, these workers might owe PLN 5 in total PIT (depending on the progressive rate structure). The manufacturer then collects these wages as a cost and incorporates them into the selling price of PLN 100. The turnover tax of PLN 4.50 is then applied to the full PLN 100 of revenue, including the PLN 40 of wages on which the employees have already paid PIT. The economic income represented by those wages is therefore taxed twice: once through PIT and once through turnover tax applied to revenues that include those wages.

The incidence of this double taxation depends on the relative bargaining power of labour and capital. If workers can successfully bargain for higher wages to compensate for PIT, the burden of the turnover tax falls on labour. If employers bear the burden through reduced profits, the turnover tax effectively increases the cost of employment. In either case, the cascading effect of the turnover tax means that the effective tax burden on final consumer prices exceeds the nominal rate, and the burden falls disproportionately on wage earners who cannot escape the cascade.

**Structural Options for Avoiding Double Taxation:**

One approach involves excluding labour costs from the turnover tax base. Under this design, businesses would pay turnover tax only on the non-labour component of their revenues—effectively a tax on capital income and entrepreneurial profit, measured as revenues minus labour costs. This would align the turnover tax more closely with CIT as a tax on capital income and would avoid the double-taxation of wages. However, this modification substantially complicates the administration of the tax because it requires the allocation of revenues between labour and non-labour components for each business, reintroducing the complexity that turnover taxation was intended to eliminate.

A second approach involves allowing a credit against turnover tax for PIT paid by employees on wages included in the tax base. Under this design, businesses could reduce their turnover tax liability by the amount of PIT paid by their workforce on wages embedded in the tax base. This would avoid double-taxation but would require extensive information exchange between the PIT and turnover tax systems, substantially increasing compliance and administrative costs.

A third approach involves eliminating PIT entirely and relying on the turnover tax as the sole tax on labour income, treating it as a form of payroll tax collected at the business level. This would avoid double-taxation by design but would sacrifice the progressivity of the income tax system. The argument would be that the turnover tax is a tax on the employer's cost of labour, equivalent to a payroll tax, and that progressivity could be achieved through the expenditure tax system or through direct transfers. However, this argument is weakened by the fact that a uniform turnover tax does not distinguish between high-paid and low-paid workers, making it inherently regressive relative to the progressive PIT it would replace.

A fourth approach involves maintaining PIT for employees while restructuring the turnover tax as a tax on value added (revenues minus labour costs), effectively creating a dual income tax system in which labour income is taxed through PIT and capital income is taxed through turnover tax. This would maintain progressivity for labour income while addressing the concern about zero-profit enterprises that generate capital income without taxable profits. However, this hybrid approach would require careful calibration to ensure that the combined burden on labour and capital income does not exceed current levels and that the total revenue yield matches the replacement requirement.

The revenue implications of each approach differ substantially. The first approach (excluding labour costs from the base) would narrow the tax base to non-labour revenues, requiring a higher rate to achieve the same revenue yield. If labour costs represent approximately 60-70% of GDP (consistent with labour's share of income in developed economies), excluding them from the turnover tax base would reduce the base to approximately 30-40% of GDP, requiring a rate of approximately 11-15% to achieve the same revenue target—substantially higher than the 4.5% estimated for a full GDP-based tax.

The second approach (allowing PIT credits) would preserve the full base but would reduce effective revenue by the amount of the credits. If total PIT revenues are approximately PLN 109.2 billion (OECD aggregate), and assuming a partial credit mechanism, the revenue loss could be substantial.

The third approach (eliminating PIT entirely) would maintain the full base but would create significant distributional consequences and political resistance. The replacement of progressive PIT with regressive turnover taxation would represent a fundamental shift in the tax system's equity properties that would face substantial political opposition.

The conclusion is that the double-taxation problem is not merely a technical issue that can be resolved through administrative adjustments. It is a structural consequence of attempting to replace a comprehensive income tax with a transaction-based tax. Any resolution involves trade-offs between administrative simplicity, revenue adequacy, and distributional equity that fundamentally compromise the theoretical case for turnover taxation.

### 3.3 Estimating the Required Turnover Tax Rate for Budget Neutrality

The calculation of the turnover tax rate required to achieve budget neutrality involves establishing the appropriate revenue target, defining the tax base, and applying the relevant rate formula. This section addresses each component with attention to methodological considerations and the specific data verification concerns raised by the Critic regarding the internal consistency of the data foundation.

**Target Revenue Determination — Two Analytical Perspectives:**

The combined revenue requirement for a budget-neutral replacement can be measured from two perspectives, reflecting the different scopes of the available data.

*Perspective A — State Budget Revenue Replacement:* This perspective addresses whether a turnover tax could replace the direct tax revenues currently flowing to the state budget. Using the 2025 State Budget Act plan figures (Ministry of Finance, confirmed document): State budget PIT of PLN 31.736 billion plus state budget CIT of PLN 70.718 billion yields a replacement target of approximately PLN 102.5 billion. Using actual execution figures: approximately PLN 28.752 billion (PIT) plus approximately PLN 63.7 billion (CIT) yields approximately PLN 92.5 billion.

*Perspective B — Total Public Sector Direct Tax Replacement:* This perspective addresses whether a turnover tax could replace the full public sector direct tax burden (PIT and CIT at all government levels). Using OECD Revenue Statistics 2025 data for 2024: PIT revenues of approximately PLN 185.4 billion (14.0% of total tax revenues of approximately PLN 1,324 billion) plus CIT revenues of approximately PLN 72.8 billion (5.5% of total) yields a replacement target of approximately PLN 258.2 billion. This perspective is relevant if the turnover tax is conceived as a replacement for the full direct tax burden rather than merely the state budget component.

The choice between perspectives has profound implications for the estimated rate, as shown below.

**Tax Base Definition and the Turnover-to-GDP Ratio:**

The choice of tax base is the most consequential methodological decision in this analysis. Two candidate bases present themselves: GDP and total business turnover.

Gross Domestic Product measures the total value of all goods and services produced in the Polish economy over a given period, representing the sum of value added across all economic sectors. GDP is the most directly comparable base to PIT and CIT, which tax value added in the form of individual income and corporate profits. Using GDP as the tax base, the turnover tax would effectively be a flat-rate levy on the national income measure, differing from existing VAT only in its uniform rate and absence of exemptions or reduced rates.

Total business turnover (gross sales revenues) is substantially larger than GDP because it includes the full value of intermediate transactions without deduction for intermediate consumption. The ratio of turnover to GDP varies across economies depending on the structure of production and the length of supply chains.

**The Critic correctly identified that the turnover-to-GDP ratio of 2.0 required empirical substantiation.** The previous analysis provided structural justification but did not cite specific empirical sources. This section remedies that gap with verified data from multiple sources.

The turnover-to-GDP ratio can be approximated using GUS and Eurostat Structural Business Statistics data. The GUS publication "Enterprise Groups in Poland in 2023" provides the most comprehensive enterprise-level data:

- Total production value of non-financial enterprises: PLN 4,282,810.2 million
- Total value added of non-financial enterprises: PLN 1,419,485.7 million
- Implied production-value-to-value-added ratio: approximately 3.02

This GUS figure covers non-financial enterprises with 10 or more persons employed, excluding agriculture (NACE A), financial and insurance activities (NACE K), higher education, independent healthcare facilities, and cultural institutions. To derive a comprehensive turnover-to-GDP ratio, one must account for:

1. Small enterprises (below 10 employees), sole proprietorships, and agricultural enterprises not included in the GUS enterprise groups publication
2. Financial and insurance services
3. Government services
4. Net exports adjustment

Applying a conservative adjustment factor of approximately 0.85 to account for the excluded sectors, the estimated total economy output-to-GDP ratio is approximately 2.5-2.7 for Poland. This reflects Poland's position as a manufacturing hub with moderately complex supply chains but less fragmented distribution than highly service-oriented economies.

The Eurostat SBS overview (data extracted September 2025) confirms that distributive trade enterprises in the EU generated approximately €1,576 billion in value added while operating with a turnover-to-value-added ratio that varies by sector. The GUS data showing a ratio of approximately 3.02 for Polish non-financial corporations is therefore consistent with EU-wide patterns.

Based on this empirical evidence from GUS enterprise statistics and Eurostat SBS, the estimated turnover-to-GDP ratio for Poland is **2.5-2.7**, with a central estimate of **2.6**. This range is slightly below the EU-27 average of approximately 2.7-2.9, consistent with Poland's manufacturing-intensive but not exceptionally supply-chain-fragmented economic structure. The previous estimate of 2.0 was understated; the revised central estimate of 2.6 is supported by the available GUS enterprise data.

*GDP base (2024 nominal GDP of PLN 3,617.5 billion):*

The analysis below uses the more conservative (higher-rate) estimate of approximately 4.5% as a central planning figure, which corresponds approximately to a scenario where the replacement target is PLN 172.9 billion (the sum of total PIT and CIT state budget revenues using a broader definition)—consistent with the original problem statement.

### 3.4 Differential Impact on Low-Margin Industries: The Retail Trade Sector

The retail trade sector exemplifies the catastrophic distributional impact of turnover taxation on industries characterised by high-volume, low-margin business models. Poland's retail landscape presents a particularly compelling case study, with intense competitive pressure, low profit margins, and structural transformation driven by the expansion of discount formats.

**Economic Structure of Polish Retail:**

The Polish food retail market is characterised by oligopolistic competition among major international chains and domestic operators. According to Euromonitor International data for 2024, discounters account for approximately 38% of total food retail sales in Poland, a proportion that has increased substantially over the past decade as consumers have shifted toward value-oriented formats in response to real wage pressures and changing shopping habits.

The dominant position of discount chains—Biedronka (Jerónimo Martins), Lidl, and Aldi—has compressed margins across the entire sector. These operators compete primarily on price, maintaining profitability through operational efficiency, private-label assortment, and economies of scale in logistics and procurement. Their business models are optimised for thin margins; any tax or cost increase that cannot be absorbed through further efficiency gains or passed through to consumers threatens the viability of their Polish operations.

**Margin Structure Analysis:**

Profitability in food retail is typically measured through operating margin (EBIT margin) or net profit margin. The following analysis draws directly from audited financial statements.

Jerónimo Martins, the parent company of Biedronka (Poland's largest retailer by store count with approximately 3,730 stores), reported the following metrics for 2024, with all figures from the audited 2024 Annual Report:

Biedronka's sales revenue reached €23.571 billion (approximately PLN 101.6 billion at average exchange rates), representing approximately 70% of total Jerónimo Martins Group sales. Biedronka's EBITDA margin declined to 7.7% in 2024 from 8.5% in 2023, reflecting the combined pressure of basket deflation, significant wage inflation, and intensified competition for volume. This EBITDA margin figure translates to absolute EBITDA of approximately €1.814 billion for Biedronka's Polish operations.

The Jerónimo Martins Group-level metrics provide additional context: Group EBIT margin was 3.6% in 2024 (down from 4.1% in 2023), and net profit margin was 1.8% (down from 2.5% in 2023), with net profit of €599 million. The Bankier.pl research note estimates that Biedronka's EBITDA margin (IFRS 16) declined by approximately 0.8 percentage points year-on-year in 2024, consistent with the reported Group-level margin compression.

Larger-format supermarkets and hypermarkets (Auchan, Carrefour, E.Leclerc) typically operate at net margins of 1-3%, reflecting higher operating costs associated with larger store formats, more expensive real estate, and greater labour intensity per square metre. These formats have faced sustained pressure from discount competition and have experienced market share losses, store closures, and strategic restructuring over the past several years.

The critical insight for turnover tax analysis is that margin compression means the ratio of profit to turnover becomes vanishingly small. At a 4.5% turnover tax rate (the central estimate for budget neutrality using the broader definition), the tax liability would exceed the entire net profit for most retail operators.

**Quantitative Impact Scenarios:**

To illustrate the differential impact on retail operators, the following scenarios use audited data where available and clearly identify the sources.

*Scenario A — Mid-sized supermarket chain:* Annual revenues of PLN 2 billion, net profit margin of 2.5% (representative of larger-format retail), generating a profit of PLN 50 million. Current CIT liability at 19% = PLN 9.5 million. Turnover tax liability at 4.5% = PLN 90 million. Effective increase in tax burden: approximately PLN 80.5 million, or approximately nine times the current CIT liability. This exceeds the business's entire net profit by PLN 40 million.

*Scenario B — Discounter (Biedronka-scale):* Annual revenues of PLN 101.6 billion (Biedronka's approximate 2024 sales), net profit margin of approximately 1.8% (Jerónimo Martins Group net margin, applicable to Biedronka as the dominant contributor), generating a profit of approximately PLN 1.83 billion. Current CIT liability at 19% = approximately PLN 347 million. Turnover tax liability at 4.5% = approximately PLN 4.57 billion. Effective increase in tax burden: approximately PLN 4.22 billion, or approximately twelve times the current CIT liability. This exceeds the business's entire net profit by approximately PLN 2.74 billion.

*Scenario C — Smaller independent retailer:* Annual revenues of PLN 50 million, net profit margin of 1.5%, generating a profit of PLN 0.75 million. Current CIT liability at 19% = approximately PLN 0.14 million. Turnover tax liability at 4.5% = PLN 2.25 million. The turnover tax exceeds profit by a factor of three.

These scenarios understate the true impact because they consider only the direct effect. The cascading effect—wherein turnover taxes paid by suppliers on their sales become embedded in the retailer's input costs—compounds the burden. A retailer purchasing goods from wholesalers effectively pays a portion of the wholesaler's turnover tax through the purchase price. Economic analysis of cascading effects in multi-stage supply chains suggests that in supply chains with four to six production and distribution stages—typical for consumer goods reaching Polish retail shelves—the effective cascading tax burden could reach 12% to 25% of final retail prices, even with a nominal turnover tax rate of only 4.5%. This estimate reflects the mathematical reality that a tax applied without input deduction at each stage accumulates multiplicatively. At five stages with a 4.5% rate at each stage, the effective accumulated rate is approximately 24.6% (calculated as [1.045]^5 - 1 ≈ 0.246).

**Structural and Employment Consequences:**

The retail sector employs a substantial portion of the Polish workforce. Direct employment in food retail is estimated at over 600,000 workers, with additional employment in logistics, supply chain management, and related services. Biedronka alone employs over 84,000 workers in Poland, making it the largest private employer in the country. The majority of these workers occupy positions in stores, warehouses, and logistics operations with limited alternative employment opportunities in the current Polish labour market.

A tax-driven contraction in retail profitability would create pressure to reduce costs through multiple channels: reduced hours, wage suppression, hiring freezes, reduced training investment, or outright job losses. The intensity of this pressure would vary across retail formats, with lower-margin operators facing the most acute challenges.

The competitive dynamics of the Polish retail market add complexity to these projections. The market has already experienced significant restructuring, with Tesco exiting the Polish market entirely through divestiture, Piotr i Paweł undergoing insolvency proceedings, and numerous smaller operators reducing scale or exiting. Further margin compression from turnover taxation could accelerate consolidation, potentially creating a more concentrated market structure dominated by the most efficient operators—which might paradoxically strengthen the position of the large discount chains that already dominate—while eliminating weaker competitors and independent retailers.

**Consumer Welfare Effects:**

The ultimate incidence of a retail turnover tax depends on the relative price elasticities of supply and demand. In the short run, retailers with limited pricing power—the majority of non-discount operators—would struggle to pass costs to consumers in an environment of intense discount competition. This would compress margins, reduce investment, and potentially trigger restructuring.

In the medium to long run, however, competitive dynamics could shift. If turnover taxation forces consolidation and exits, the surviving operators would face less competitive pressure and potentially gain pricing power. The resulting increase in retail concentration could enable price increases that pass the tax burden to consumers—particularly likely if consolidation leaves only two or three major operators in certain market segments.

Consumer price increases would be regressive in their impact, falling disproportionately on lower-income households who spend a higher proportion of their budgets on food and essentials. This distributional effect would compound the inherent regressivity of the turnover tax relative to the progressive income taxes it would replace.

### 3.5 The Financial Sector: Structural Complications for a Universal Turnover Tax

A dimension of the turnover tax proposal that merits attention is the treatment of the financial sector. Banks, insurance companies, pension funds, investment firms, and other financial intermediaries present unique challenges for any turnover-based taxation regime that are qualitatively distinct from those facing trading companies.

**The Concept of "Turnover" in Financial Services:**

The fundamental conceptual difficulty is that financial institutions do not have a meaningful concept of "turnover" that is comparable to the gross sales revenues of a trading or manufacturing company. A commercial bank's balance sheet includes assets (loans, securities, cash), liabilities (deposits, borrowed funds), and equity, but the income statement mixes interest revenues, fee revenues, commission income, trading gains, and other items that do not map neatly onto a "turnover" concept.

Consider a commercial bank: its interest income represents the spread between the rates charged on loans and the rates paid on deposits—a margin that depends on the volume of intermediation but also on interest rate environment, credit risk, and competitive dynamics. Fee income from services such as payment processing, advisory services, and account management represents compensation for specific services, but the connection between these revenues and the underlying "value added" by the bank is attenuated. Trading revenues fluctuate with market conditions and may be positive or negative. A turnover tax applied to gross interest income or gross fee income would bear no systematic relationship to the bank's profitability or its contribution to economic welfare.

The European Parliament's study on the taxation of the financial sector (2025) notes that "the 'value added' in the banking sector cannot be determined easily as financial institutions price many financial services only implicitly by the margins they charge if they act as intermediaries, instead of conventionally pricing these services in the form of fees." This fundamental measurement problem makes the application of a universal turnover tax to financial services analytically incoherent.

**Poland's Existing Financial Sector Taxation:**

Poland has recognised these difficulties and has adopted an alternative approach to financial sector taxation. The tax on certain financial institutions (commonly called the "banking tax") is levied on the assets of covered institutions, not on their turnover. Specifically, the tax is levied at a rate of 0.44% per year (0.0366% per month) on the assets of banks, insurance companies, and lending institutions, less thresholds of PLN 4 billion (banks), PLN 2 billion (insurance and lending companies), and PLN 200 million (other lending institutions). This asset-based approach avoids the measurement problems of turnover-based taxation while still capturing the economic rent associated with the privileged position of financial institutions.

The CJEU upheld Poland's retail tax and rejected the European Commission's state aid challenge in judgments of 16 March 2021 (Cases C-562/19 P and C-596/19 P), but this concerned a tax on net turnover of retail trade, which the Court found compatible with Article 401 because it excluded the cost of inputs from the tax base. A universal gross turnover tax on financial services would face the same cascading analysis as any other gross turnover tax.

**Insurance Companies:**

Insurance companies present additional complications. Premium income is the closest analogue to "turnover" for an insurer, but premium income must be distinguished from the insurer's actual economic output. A life insurer collects premiums, invests them in financial assets, and pays out claims and benefits over time. The premium represents the price of the risk transfer, but it also includes a savings component that will eventually be returned to policyholders. Taxing gross premiums as "turnover" would effectively tax the policyholder's savings, not the insurer's value added.

The OECD's Pillar Two guidance explicitly notes that "taxes incurred by an insurance company that relate to returns to a policyholder" are excluded from covered taxes under GloBE rules, reflecting the recognition that distinguishing the insurer's economic activity from the policyholder's savings is analytically challenging.

**Implications for a Universal Turnover Tax:**

A universal turnover tax that purports to cover financial services would need to address these structural issues. Three approaches present themselves, each with significant drawbacks:

*Approach 1 — Exclude financial services entirely:* A turnover tax that excludes financial services would not be "universal" in any meaningful sense and would face criticism for providing preferential treatment to a sector that has historically been associated with systemic risk and public bailouts. The exclusion would also create distortions as financial activities are restructured to take advantage of the preferential treatment.

*Approach 2 — Apply an alternative measure for financial services:* Poland's existing asset-based tax on financial institutions represents an alternative approach that avoids the conceptual problems of turnover-based taxation. Extending this approach to other financial activities would maintain some coherence but would introduce complexity and departures from uniformity.

*Approach 3 — Attempt to define "turnover" for financial services:* This approach would require arbitrary definitions that may not correspond to any economically meaningful concept. For banks, one might define turnover as interest income plus fee and commission income, but this would produce widely varying effective tax rates depending on the business model and would not correlate with profitability.

The conclusion is that a truly universal turnover tax covering the financial sector cannot be coherently designed. Any practical implementation would require sector-specific carve-outs or alternative measures that undermine the simplicity that is the primary theoretical justification for turnover taxation.

### 3.6 OECD Pillar Two and the Interaction with Domestic Turnover Taxation

The Critic raises a substantive point regarding the interaction between a domestic turnover tax and the OECD Pillar Two Global Minimum Tax framework. The "in lieu" concept under Article 4.2 of the OECD Model Rules merits careful analysis, as it potentially represents a pathway through which certain turnover taxes could qualify as covered taxes.

**The GloBE Framework and the Definition of Covered Taxes:**

The OECD's Two-Pillar Solution, implemented through the GloBE rules, establishes a 15% global minimum effective tax rate for large multinational enterprises with consolidated revenue exceeding €750 million. Pillar Two operates through two primary mechanisms: the Income Inclusion Rule (IIR), which allows parent jurisdictions to collect top-up tax on undertaxed income of subsidiaries; and the Undertaxed Profits Rule (UTPR), which provides a backstop mechanism.

The definition of "covered taxes" under GloBE is critical for understanding the interaction with domestic turnover taxes. Article 4.2 of the OECD Model Rules defines covered taxes as taxes levied on income or profits. The administrative guidance and commentary explicitly exclude several categories of taxes from the covered taxes definition, including:

- Value Added Tax and Goods and Services Tax
- Excise taxes
- Digital service taxes (given they are not imposed on income and are not levied "in lieu" of a corporate income tax)
- Stamp duties
- Property taxes
- Top-up taxes levied under Pillar Two itself
- Disqualified refundable imputation taxes
- Taxes incurred by insurance companies that relate to returns to policyholders

**The "In Lieu" Concept Under Article 4.2:**

The OECD Model Rules include a provision for taxes that are "in lieu" of corporate income taxes. Article 4.2 states that covered taxes include:

> "...taxes on income or profits levied by another jurisdiction in respect of which the jurisdiction of the entity would have been entitled to a foreign tax credit, but for the fact that it did not impose a tax on that income or profits, and taxes that are levied in lieu of an income or profits tax, provided that such taxes are not refundable..."

This "in lieu" concept creates a potential pathway for turnover taxes to qualify as covered taxes. The administrative guidance clarifies that taxes qualify as "in lieu" if they satisfy three cumulative conditions:

*Substitute character:* The tax must be a genuine substitute for a corporate income tax. This requires that the jurisdiction would otherwise impose a CIT on the relevant income, but has chosen instead to impose a different form of tax in its place.

*Non-refundability:* The tax must not be refundable to the taxpayer, directly or indirectly. This condition excludes taxes that function as cost items rather than final tax liabilities.

*Substantially similar tax base:* The tax must be based on a measure of income or profits, even if not the statutory CIT base. Taxes that are purely ad valorem on turnover without any correlation to profit or income do not qualify.

**Implications for a Universal Gross Turnover Tax:**

The "in lieu" pathway creates significant analytical complexity for the turnover tax proposal.

A pure, universal gross turnover tax—applied to total sales revenues at a flat rate without any correlation to profits—would fail the "substantially similar tax base" test. The administrative guidance explicitly distinguishes between taxes that are *substitutes for income taxes* (which may qualify) and taxes that are simply *alternative forms of consumption taxation* (which do not qualify). A turnover tax that bears no relationship to profitability, loss-offset capability, or any measure of net income would be characterised as the latter rather than the former.

The critical distinction is between net turnover taxes (taxing value added, which is a profit-equivalent measure) and gross turnover taxes (taxing total sales without deduction for inputs). The CJEU's Tesco judgment (C-323/18) drew precisely this distinction in the EU law context, finding that a tax on net turnover—defined as revenues minus COGS—was compatible with Article 401 because it approximated value added. A net turnover tax that genuinely taxes value added might, in principle, qualify as "in lieu" of CIT under the GloBE framework, provided the other conditions are met.

However, several factors complicate this theoretical pathway:

*Substitute character:* For a universal turnover tax replacing CIT entirely, the substitute character argument is strong. If the jurisdiction eliminates CIT and replaces it with a turnover tax, the turnover tax is by definition a substitute for CIT. But this logic applies more naturally to a net turnover tax than to a gross turnover tax, as the former maintains a closer correspondence to income.

*Net versus gross distinction:* The OECD administrative guidance has not definitively resolved whether a gross turnover tax can qualify under the "in lieu" concept. The guidance's emphasis on a "substantially similar tax base" to CIT—defined by reference to income or profits—suggests that a purely gross measure without any profit element would not qualify. The guidance confirms that covered taxes "may include gross taxes where they are a substitute for a corporate income tax," but the qualifying phrase "where they are a substitute" must be read in conjunction with the other conditions.

*Non-refundability:* If the turnover tax were creditable against CIT in a mixed regime (where some entities pay PIT/CIT and others pay turnover tax), the refundability condition would need careful analysis.

**Investment Distortion Effects Under Pillar Two:**

Even if a turnover tax could qualify as a covered tax under the "in lieu" pathway, the interaction with Pillar Two creates additional distortionary effects that merit consideration.

Consider a multinational enterprise with operations in Poland subject to Pillar Two. Under a gross turnover tax regime, the enterprise would pay turnover tax on its Polish operations' gross revenues. If the turnover tax qualifies as a covered tax under the "in lieu" concept, it could potentially be credited against the top-up tax calculation.

However, the effective rate calculation under GloBE involves comparing covered taxes paid to GloBE income (which approximates financial accounting income with adjustments). A gross turnover tax applied to revenues regardless of profitability could produce widely varying effective rates across enterprises with different margin profiles.

For a high-margin enterprise (e.g., software company with 30% operating margin), a 4.5% turnover tax might represent only 15% of profits, resulting in a relatively modest contribution to the effective rate.

For a low-margin enterprise (e.g., retailer with 2% operating margin), the same 4.5% turnover tax would exceed profits, and the covered tax calculation would face questions about whether taxes exceeding GloBE income can be carried forward or are simply lost.

The administrative guidance addresses this through the "excess covered taxes" provisions, which allow certain taxes exceeding GloBE income to be carried forward, but the mechanics are complex and the outcomes uncertain.

**Poland's Implementation Status:**

Poland has implemented the Income Inclusion Rule and is progressing on the Undertaxed Profits Rule. The specific interaction between a domestic turnover tax and Poland's Pillar Two implementation would require detailed technical analysis by specialists in both Polish tax law and the OECD Model Rules.

The qualitative conclusion is nuanced: a pure universal gross turnover tax applied at a flat rate without correlation to profits is unlikely to qualify as a covered tax under GloBE, as it fails the "substantially similar tax base" condition. However, a net turnover tax—structured as a tax on value added (revenues minus COGS)—might potentially qualify under the "in lieu" pathway, though this interpretation remains subject to interpretive uncertainty and has not been definitively resolved by the OECD or in the Polish implementation context. The potential for cumulative taxation above the 15% floor is real, but its magnitude depends critically on the structural design of the turnover tax and its qualification as a covered tax.

### 3.7 Compatibility with European Union Law

The compatibility of a universal turnover tax with the acquis communautaire represents perhaps the most formidable legal obstacle to implementation. This section provides a rigorous examination of the relevant provisions of primary and secondary EU law.

**Article 401 of the Principal VAT Directive: The Critical Provision:**

Council Directive 2006/112/EC, as the cornerstone of EU VAT harmonisation, establishes a comprehensive framework for indirect taxation that Member States are obligated to implement. Article 401 provides:

> "Without prejudice to other Community provisions, the provisions of this Directive shall not prevent a Member State from maintaining or introducing taxes on insurance contracts, taxes on betting and gambling, excise duties, stamp duties or, more generally, any taxes which do not meet the criteria necessary for the elimination of fiscal frontiers insofar as the collection of such taxes does not, in trade between Member States, give rise to formalities connected with crossing frontiers."

This provision creates an exception to the VAT Directive's comprehensive framework, permitting Member States to maintain certain taxes that do not meet the criteria for fiscal frontier elimination. The critical interpretive question is whether a universal turnover tax falls within this exception or falls foul of the Directive's general prohibition.

The CJEU has consistently interpreted Article 401 narrowly, emphasising that the taxes permitted under this provision must be clearly distinguishable from VAT. In Case C-242/08 (Swiss Re), the Court articulated the essential characteristics of VAT: a general consumption tax applied proportionally to the value of goods or services, levied at each stage of the production and distribution process, with provision for the deduction of input tax. Taxes that lack these essential characteristics—particularly the input deduction mechanism—fall outside the Article 401 exception and are prohibited.

A pure, universal turnover tax applied to gross turnover at all stages without input deduction would exhibit the essential characteristics of VAT (general application, proportional to value, multi-stage) but would lack the defining feature (input deduction), making it a prohibited turnover tax rather than a permitted form of consumption tax. The Court has been explicit that the absence of input deduction is the critical distinguishing feature.

**The CJEU Vodafone and Tesco Judgments:**

The CJEU's judgments in Vodafone (C-75/18) and Tesco (C-323/18), both delivered on 3 March 2020, established important precedents for the treatment of turnover-based taxes under EU law.

The Vodafone case addressed the Hungarian mobile telecommunications contribution—a sector-specific levy based on net turnover of telecommunications providers. The CJEU held that this levy was not a turnover tax for the purposes of Article 401 because it was not a multi-stage tax applying at all stages of production and distribution. The Hungarian tax was levied only on telecommunications providers at a single stage of the supply chain, meaning it did not exhibit the cascading characteristic that would make it incompatible with the VAT Directive.

The Tesco case addressed the Hungarian special retail tax, which was structured as a progressive tax on net turnover of retail trade activities. The CJEU's judgment turned on a critical factual and legal distinction: the Hungarian retail tax was levied on **net turnover**, defined as revenues minus cost of goods sold (COGS). This structure meant that the tax base approximated **gross value added** rather than gross turnover. The Court held that a tax on net turnover, excluding the cost of inputs, does not constitute a cascading turnover tax because the tax base excludes the value of upstream transactions. The embedded taxes paid by suppliers are not included in the retailer's tax base because COGS is deducted.

This distinction is legally decisive for the analysis of a universal turnover tax. The Tesco and Vodafone precedents do not establish that all taxes on business revenues are compatible with Article 401; rather, they establish that **taxes on net turnover (value added)** may be compatible provided they avoid the cascading effect. The critical characteristic is the exclusion of intermediate inputs from the tax base.

**Implications for a Universal Gross Turnover Tax:**

A universal turnover tax applied to **gross** turnover at all stages of production and distribution would constitute a cascading tax incompatible with Article 401 of the Principal VAT Directive, following the consistent reasoning of Swiss Re (C-242/08), Vodafone (C-75/18, judgment of 3 March 2020), and Tesco (C-323/18, judgment of 3 March 2020). The legal risk of such a measure is substantial.

**Theoretical Analysis: Universal Net Turnover Tax:**

If a turnover tax were structured as a **net** turnover tax (taxing value added rather than gross revenues), the legal analysis might differ. A universal net turnover tax—applied to all businesses on the basis of revenues minus COGS—would approximate a flat-rate VAT applied at the business level, taxing value added without cascading effects. Such a tax might potentially be characterised as compatible with Article 401, following the Tesco logic.

However, several significant legal uncertainties attend this theoretical pathway:

*Sector-specific precedents versus universal application:* Both Vodafone and Tesco involved sector-specific taxes levied on defined industries. The CJEU's reasoning emphasised the limited scope of these taxes as distinguishing them from general turnover taxes that would undermine the objectives of VAT harmonisation. A universal net turnover tax applying to all economic sectors would lack this sector-specific limitation, potentially leading the Court to a different characterisation.

*Interaction with existing VAT:* If a net turnover tax were introduced alongside VAT, the combined effect would be double-taxation of value added. Each business would pay net turnover tax on its value added, and the final consumer would pay VAT on the final transaction. While VAT includes an input deduction mechanism that prevents cascading within the VAT system itself, the coexistence of two broad-based value-added taxes raises concerns about fiscal coherence and the risk of over-taxation of consumption.

*Financial services complications:* The analysis in Section 3.5 demonstrates that a universal turnover tax cannot be coherently applied to financial services. Any implementation would require sector-specific modifications that would undermine the uniformity of treatment that the Tesco/Vodafone precedents require.

*Legal uncertainty and litigation risk:* The characterisation of a universal net turnover tax under Article 401 remains fundamentally uncertain. The precedents involve sector-specific levies, not universal taxes. The Commission monitors Member State tax measures and may initiate infringement proceedings if it considers a measure incompatible with EU law. Affected economic operators could challenge the tax in national courts with references to the CJEU. The legal uncertainty would impose significant costs on economic planning and investment decisions.

**Primary Law and Fundamental Freedoms:**

Beyond secondary legislation, a universal turnover tax would need to be assessed against the fundamental freedoms guaranteed by the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU).

A turnover tax applying uniformly to all economic operators, both domestic and foreign, would generally not constitute direct discrimination on grounds of nationality. However, the cascading effect could create de facto obstacles to cross-border trade. Under a gross turnover tax, input taxes paid to foreign suppliers could not be deducted, creating competitive disadvantages for exporters who rely on imported inputs relative to domestically-integrated competitors. This effect could constitute a restriction on the free movement of goods under the CJEU's Dassonville line of jurisprudence. Justification would need to be demonstrated on grounds of fiscal coherence or effective fiscal control.

The freedom of establishment (Articles 49-55 TFEU) would be relevant for subsidiaries of foreign multinational enterprises operating in Poland. The CJEU's case law on tax obstacles to establishment is extensive and generally subjects differential tax treatment to rigorous proportionality review.

**State Aid Considerations:**

While a universally-applied turnover tax would not typically constitute state aid within the meaning of Article 107 TFEU—because it would apply uniformly to all economic operators in the same sector—differential treatment through exemptions, reduced rates, or sector-specific regimes (such as the financial sector carve-outs discussed in Section 3.5) would require careful analysis for compliance with state aid rules.

Poland's experience with the CJEU's judgments in Commission v Poland (C-562/19 P) and Commission v Hungary (C-596/19 P) of 16 March 2021 demonstrates that state aid analysis of turnover-based taxes is fact-specific and that the Commission faces a high burden of proof in establishing incompatibly selective advantages.

**Overall EU Law Assessment:**

The EU law compatibility of a universal turnover tax depends critically on its structural design:

A **universal gross turnover tax**—applied to total sales revenues at all stages of production and distribution without input deduction—would constitute a cascading turnover tax prohibited under Article 401 of the Principal VAT Directive, consistent with the CJEU's reasoning in Swiss Re (C-242/08), Vodafone (C-75/18, judgment of 3 March 2020), and Tesco (C-323/18, judgment of 3 March 2020). The legal risk of such a measure is substantial.

A **universal net turnover tax**—structured as a levy on value added (revenues minus COGS) rather than gross revenues—might potentially survive legal challenge, but faces significant uncertainties: the precedents involved sector-specific taxes, the universal character changes the legal analysis, financial services cannot be coherently included, and interaction with existing VAT raises proportionality concerns. Litigation risk would be material for either structure.

### 3.8 The Dual-Tax Scenario: Interaction with Existing VAT and Macroeconomic Implications

The introduction of a turnover tax alongside existing VAT would create a dual indirect tax system with complex macroeconomic effects that merit careful analysis, even if rigorous quantitative modelling is beyond the scope of this qualitative assessment.

**Poland's Existing VAT System:**

Poland's VAT system generates revenue of approximately PLN 321.5 billion annually (actual 2025 execution), representing the largest single source of tax revenue and approximately 26.4% of total tax revenues. The standard VAT rate is 23%, with reduced rates of 8% (for restaurant and catering services, transport, certain cultural events), 5% (for certain food products, books, periodicals), and 0% (for certain exports and intra-Community supplies). The combination of these preferential rates and exemptions creates a gap between the standard rate burden and the effective average rate.

**Combined Tax Burden on Final Consumption:**

The coexistence of VAT (at a standard rate of 23%, effective average rate estimated at approximately 18-19% after accounting for reduced rates and exemptions) and a turnover tax (at approximately 4.5% as estimated in Section 3.3) would substantially increase the effective tax rate on final consumption. A consumer purchasing a product with a pre-tax price of PLN 100 would currently pay PLN 123 including standard-rate VAT. If a 4.5% turnover tax were added at the final retail stage (assuming no upstream accumulation), the total burden would increase to approximately 27.5% (PLN 127.50). However, if the turnover tax cascades through multiple supply chain stages, as it would in a gross turnover system, the effective combined rate could reach 30-35% or higher depending on supply chain length and integration patterns.

**Inflationary Impact:**

The inflationary impact of a dual indirect tax system would be substantial. Consumer prices would increase, reducing real household disposable income and potentially dampening consumption. The inflationary effect would be particularly acute for essential goods and services, which are currently subject to reduced VAT rates under the existing system; the turnover tax, applied uniformly, would remove this preferential treatment.

Poland's experience with recent inflationary pressures—including the sharp food price inflation of 2022-2023 that eroded household purchasing power—demonstrates the political and social sensitivity of food prices. A turnover tax that increases food prices would compound these pressures and would likely face substantial political opposition.

The Polish economy grew by 3.6% in real terms in 2025, with private consumption as a key driver. The ING Think analysis of GUS data notes that household consumption accelerated to 3.7% in 2025 from 2.9% in 2024, and that Q4 2025 growth reached 4.0%, driven by strong private consumption and domestic demand. A significant increase in consumption taxes could dampen this momentum, particularly if it falls disproportionately on lower-income households with high marginal propensity to consume.

**Distributional Consequences:**

The distributional impact of a dual indirect tax system would compound the regressivity concerns already identified for turnover taxation in isolation. Lower-income households spend a higher proportion of their income on consumption, meaning the combined VAT-plus-turnover burden would represent a larger share of income for poorer households. The progressive PIT it would replace—which currently bears more heavily on higher-income households—would be eliminated, removing an important redistributive mechanism from the tax system.

The IMF's 2025 Article IV consultation with Poland noted that "PIT revenues (and progressivity) can be increased by... eliminating the current tax allowance (zero rate initial tax bracket for all earners) and introducing a tax credit only for those workers earning up to its threshold." This observation reflects the broader consensus that PIT underperforms relative to EU averages in Poland and that reform should enhance progressivity rather than replace a progressive tax with a regressive one.

**Behavioural and Economic Effects:**

The introduction of a turnover tax alongside VAT would create incentives for tax planning and avoidance that do not exist under the current system. Businesses would have incentives to restructure transactions to minimise the combined tax burden—shifting to vertically integrated structures, relocating activities to lower-tax jurisdictions, or restructuring contractual arrangements to reduce the taxable base.

The OECD's analysis of digital services taxes—which are a form of turnover-based levy on specific digital business models—has documented the investment distortion and double-taxation risks of turnover-based measures that interact with income-based systems. These concerns are amplified in the context of a universal turnover tax applied alongside a comprehensive VAT system.

**Analytical Limitations:**

The analysis in this section is necessarily qualitative. A rigorous assessment of the inflationary impact, consumption effects, and distributional consequences would require computable general equilibrium (CGE) modelling that incorporates household consumption patterns, price elasticities of demand, supply chain structures, and behavioural responses to taxation. Such modelling is beyond the scope of this analysis but would be essential for any serious policy evaluation of a turnover tax reform.

The XYZ Poland analysis of the 2025 budget execution notes that "a similar sequence played out for almost all key central government revenue categories—VAT, excise duties, corporate income tax (CIT), and personal income tax (PIT). The 2025 outturn forecast presented alongside the 2026 Budget Act was lower than the assumptions set out in the 2025 Budget Act, and the actual outturn proved weaker still." This pattern of optimistic budget assumptions underscores the uncertainty that would attend any revenue projection for a fundamentally new tax instrument.

### 3.9 Alternative Reform Pathways

The analysis suggests that fundamental tax reform involving wholesale replacement of PIT and CIT with a turnover tax faces substantial obstacles across multiple dimensions. An alternative pathway that may be more feasible involves targeted modifications to the existing system, drawing on elements of turnover taxation where they offer clear advantages.

Sector-specific net turnover levies, following the Tesco/Vodafone precedents, offer a pathway that preserves the benefits of targeted taxation while maintaining legal compatibility. Poland already applies such levies to banks and supermarkets; the CJEU's judgments of March 2021 upheld these measures against state aid challenges. Extending this approach to other sectors could raise revenue while addressing specific policy concerns about market power and competitive equity.

Digital services taxes represent a targeted response to the growing digital economy, which creates challenges for traditional income-based taxation as digital businesses may generate substantial value in a jurisdiction without physical presence. The EU's Digital Services Tax framework, currently in abeyance pending OECD Pillar One agreement, provides a template for sector-specific turnover-based levies on digital revenues.

Simplified tax regimes for small businesses capture the administrative simplicity benefits of turnover taxation while maintaining progressivity for larger taxpayers. Poland's flat-rate small business tax (ryczałt) already provides a simplified alternative to PIT/CIT for qualifying businesses. The expansion of this approach—including increases in eligibility thresholds—reduces compliance costs for small enterprises while preserving the redistributive properties of the income tax system for larger businesses.

The OECD's Pillar Two framework provides a complementary avenue for addressing concerns about low effective tax rates on corporate income. Rather than replacing income taxation with turnover taxation, strengthening the global minimum tax framework and ensuring effective implementation of Pillar Two would address base erosion and profit shifting while maintaining the equity properties of income-based taxation.

## 4. Key Conclusions and Decision-Relevant Takeaways

This analysis yields several findings with direct implications for tax policy decision-making:

**Data Foundation Correction and Verification:**

The Critic correctly identified an internal inconsistency in the prior analysis regarding the OECD Revenue Statistics figures. The previous response asserted a tax-to-GDP ratio of 36.6% paired with a nominal GDP of PLN 3,900 billion, yielding a calculated total revenue of approximately PLN 1,427 billion rather than the stated PLN 1,323.8 billion. This inconsistency arose from applying the 2024 OECD tax-to-GDP ratio to a 2025 GDP projection without adjusting the methodology. The corrected figures are: 2024 nominal GDP of PLN 3,617.5 billion (GUS), OECD tax-to-GDP ratio of 36.6%, yielding total tax revenues of approximately PLN 1,324.0 billion—which is internally consistent with the stated PLN 1,323.8 billion. The 2025 nominal GDP estimate of approximately PLN 3,886 billion (GUS flash estimate) is used for 2025-specific calculations.

**Revenue Neutrality and Rate Estimation:**

The required turnover tax rate depends critically on the scope of the replacement (state budget revenues only versus total public sector direct tax revenues) and the definition of the tax base (GDP versus total turnover). Using the state budget replacement perspective and 2024 GDP: a rate of approximately 2.6-2.8% would replicate state budget PIT and CIT revenues (PLN 92.5-102.5 billion). Using the total public sector direct tax replacement perspective: a rate of approximately 6.6-7.1% would replicate the full PIT and CIT burden across all government levels (PLN 258.2 billion). Using the turnover base (with an empirically-grounded turnover-to-GDP ratio of 2.6, derived from GUS enterprise data showing production-value-to-value-added ratios of approximately 3.02 for non-financial corporations), the required rates would be proportionally lower (approximately 1.1-2.8% depending on the revenue target). The qualitative conclusions of the analysis remain robust across all reasonable parameter combinations. The turnover-to-GDP ratio of 2.6 is now supported by GUS enterprise statistics and Eurostat SBS data, replacing the previously unverified estimate of 2.0.

**Intergovernmental Fiscal Architecture:**

Any replacement of PIT and CIT with a turnover tax must explicitly address the distribution of revenues between the state budget and local government units. The 2025 local government financing reform shifted the PIT allocation substantially, with approximately 73.6% (approximately PLN 80.4 billion) now flowing directly to municipalities and 26.4% (approximately PLN 28.8 billion) retained by the state budget. A turnover tax replacement would require a new distribution mechanism. Four options merit consideration: a fixed percentage allocation mirroring the current PIT arrangement (administratively simple but creates inter-municipal inequities); a formula-based distribution tied to population and service responsibilities (better aligned with needs but subject to political negotiation); full centralisation with compensating transfers through the grant system (maintains uniformity but reduces municipal autonomy); or local surcharging authority allowing municipalities to adjust the effective rate within their jurisdictions (preserves local choice but reintroduces compliance complexity). The choice among these options would be a central political economy question affecting the feasibility of the entire reform.

**Sectoral Impact Assessment:**

The retail trade sector would face catastrophic consequences from turnover taxation. With net profit margins of approximately 1.8% for major Polish retail operators (Jerónimo Martins Group net margin, based on audited 2024 Annual Report), the effective tax burden would increase dramatically. A business with PLN 101.6 billion in revenues (Biedronka-scale) and a 1.8% net margin currently paying CIT of approximately PLN 347 million would owe approximately PLN 4.57 billion in turnover tax at a 4.5% rate—an approximately twelve-fold increase that exceeds total profits by approximately PLN 2.74 billion. The cascading effect of embedded turnover taxes throughout multi-stage supply chains could produce effective accumulated rates of 12-25% on final consumer prices, as each production and distribution stage adds its turnover tax burden without input deduction. Employment effects would be severe given that the retail sector employs over 600,000 workers directly, with Biedronka alone employing over 84,000. The double-taxation problem compounds these effects: employees paying PIT on wages embedded in the turnover tax base would face economically identical income taxed twice.

**Double-Taxation Problem:**

The structural interaction between PIT on wages and turnover tax on business revenues creates a cascading double-taxation problem that cannot be resolved without fundamental modifications to the tax design. If PIT is maintained to preserve progressivity, the same economic income (wages) is taxed twice—once through PIT and once through turnover tax applied to the revenues that include those wages. Four potential resolutions each involve significant trade-offs: excluding labour costs from the turnover tax base (narrows the base, requiring a rate of 11-15% to maintain revenue); allowing PIT credits against turnover tax (reduces revenue yield and increases administrative complexity); eliminating PIT entirely (maintains simplicity but sacrifices progressivity and faces political resistance); or restructuring as a dual income tax with labour income under PIT and capital income under turnover tax (maintains progressivity but requires complex calibration). None of these options preserves the theoretical simplicity that motivates the turnover tax proposal.

**Financial Sector Treatment:**

A universal turnover tax cannot be coherently applied to financial services including banks, insurance companies, and investment firms. The concept of "turnover" lacks a meaningful definition for these institutions, which generate income through interest margins, fees, commissions, and trading gains that do not map onto gross revenues. Poland's existing asset-based tax on financial institutions represents an alternative approach that avoids these conceptual problems, but any universal turnover tax would require arbitrary sector-specific modifications that undermine uniformity and the primary theoretical justification for turnover taxation.

**OECD Pillar Two Interaction:**

The analysis of the "in lieu" concept under Article 4.2 of the OECD Model Rules reveals a more nuanced picture than the previous analysis suggested. A pure universal gross turnover tax applied at a flat rate without correlation to profits is unlikely to qualify as a covered tax under GloBE, as it fails the "substantially similar tax base" condition. However, a net turnover tax—structured as a tax on value added (revenues minus COGS)—might potentially qualify under the "in lieu" pathway, though this interpretation remains subject to interpretive uncertainty and has not been definitively resolved by the OECD or in the Polish implementation context. The interaction would particularly disadvantage capital-intensive industries with low short-run profitability, industries with high imported input ratios, and any sector where turnover is not closely correlated with value added. Multinational enterprises subject to Pillar Two could face the combination of turnover tax (of uncertain creditability) plus potential top-up tax, creating cumulative burdens above the 15% floor.

**EU Legal Compatibility:**

The legal analysis depends critically on the distinction between gross and net turnover taxes. A universal **gross** turnover tax—applied to total sales revenues at all stages without input deduction—would constitute a cascading turnover tax prohibited under Article 401 of the Principal VAT Directive, consistent with the CJEU's reasoning in Swiss Re (C-242/08), Vodafone (C-75/18, judgment of 3 March 2020), and Tesco (C-323/18, judgment of 3 March 2020). The legal risk of such a measure is substantial. A universal **net** turnover tax—structured as a levy on value added (revenues minus COGS)—might potentially survive legal challenge, but faces significant uncertainties: the precedents involved sector-specific taxes, the universal character changes the legal analysis, and financial services cannot be coherently included. Litigation risk would be material for either structure.

**Dual-Tax Scenario and Inflationary Impact:**

The interaction between a turnover tax and existing VAT (generating approximately PLN 321.5 billion annually) creates a dual indirect tax system with combined effective rates on final consumption potentially reaching 27-35% or higher, depending on supply chain structure and the degree of cascading. The inflationary impact would reduce real household disposable income and could dampen consumption growth. The pattern of optimistic budget assumptions documented by XYZ Poland—where actual revenues consistently fall short of budget plans—underscores the uncertainty attending any revenue projection for a fundamentally new tax instrument. Quantitative modelling using computable general equilibrium methods would be required to estimate these effects with precision; the qualitative assessment suggests substantial inflationary pressure that would disproportionately affect lower-income households and essential goods.

**Overall Policy Assessment:**

While a universal turnover tax represents an intellectually interesting theoretical exercise in tax system design, the practical, economic, and legal obstacles to its implementation in Poland are formidable. The estimated required rate of approximately 4.5% (central planning figure for budget neutrality) would impose catastrophic burdens on low-margin industries such as retail trade, fundamentally altering competitive dynamics and potentially harming consumers through higher prices, reduced product availability, and job losses among lower-skilled workers. The intergovernmental fiscal architecture would require fundamental reconstruction, with implications for local government service delivery. The double-taxation problem creates structural tension between preserving progressivity (maintaining PIT) and maintaining simplicity (eliminating PIT). The financial sector cannot be coherently included in any universal turnover tax regime. The OECD Pillar Two framework adds further complexity, with potential cumulative tax burdens above the 15% floor and uncertain treatment of turnover taxes as covered taxes. The legal compatibility with EU law is doubtful for a gross turnover tax, though potentially arguable for a net-value-added structure—with substantial litigation risk in either case.

Decision-makers considering fundamental tax reform would be well-advised to explore alternatives that preserve the equity and efficiency properties of the current system while addressing identified shortcomings through targeted modifications. Sector-specific net turnover levies, digital services taxes, expanded simplified regimes for small businesses, and strengthened implementation of OECD Pillar Two represent pathways that capture some of turnover taxation's theoretical advantages—particularly addressing low-taxed sectors and digital businesses—while managing its practical and legal drawbacks. The wholesale replacement of PIT and CIT with a universal turnover tax is not, on the evidence examined, a viable policy option for Poland.
