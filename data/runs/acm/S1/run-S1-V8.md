## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The core challenge lies in determining whether Poland could constitutionally and economically replace its income-based tax pillars (Personal Income Tax and Corporate Income Tax) with a single turnover tax while maintaining fiscal neutrality, accounting for the full complexity of Poland's multi-level fiscal architecture including local government revenue sharing and social security contributions, without triggering catastrophic market distortions in low-margin sectors or violating European Union legal frameworks.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### The Scope of Revenue Replacement: Beyond State Budget PIT and CIT

Any meaningful assessment of turnover tax feasibility must first establish the precise revenue replacement target. The initial scope of "replacing PIT and CIT" conceals substantial fiscal complexity within Poland's multi-tiered governance structure. The 2024 actual execution figures of PLN 97.6 billion (PIT) and PLN 86.6 billion (CIT) totaling PLN 184.2 billion represent only the central state budget's share. Local government units—municipalities, districts, and voivodeships—historically received substantial portions of PIT revenues under the 2003 Act on Local Government Income.

The magnitude of this intergovernmental fiscal flow received dramatic illustration in 2025, when the new Act on Local Government Income (October 1, 2024) came into force. According to Ministry of Finance estimates, without this reform, state budget revenues in 2025 would have been PLN 108.4 billion higher, reflecting the substantial redistribution to municipalities through direct PIT and CIT shares rather than general subsidies. While 2024 operated under different sharing arrangements (higher percentage shares but different calculation bases), local government PIT/CIT transfers likely approximated PLN 30–40 billion, bringing total PIT/CIT replacement requirements to roughly PLN 215–225 billion.

More consequentially, the Critic's observation regarding ZUS premiums introduces a fundamental question about reform scope. Social security contributions in Poland function as employment taxes—functionally equivalent to labor taxation despite their separate legal characterization. In 2024, ZUS insurance premiums reached approximately PLN 347 billion, with total FUS revenues (including state subsidies) approaching PLN 410–425 billion. These figures dwarf the combined PIT and CIT revenue of PLN 184 billion. If a universal turnover tax aims to genuinely replace "income taxation" in its broad economic sense, the revenue target expands dramatically:

- State budget PIT + CIT: PLN 184 billion
- Local government PIT/CIT shares: PLN 30–40 billion (estimated)
- ZUS insurance premiums: PLN 347 billion
- **Total replacement target**: PLN 561–571 billion

This expanded scope fundamentally alters the arithmetic of turnover tax rate calculations.

### GDP-to-Turnover Multiplier: Empirical Uncertainty and Methodological Constraints

The relationship between GDP (value added) and aggregate turnover (gross receipts) constitutes a critical but empirically underdetermined variable. The Critic correctly identified that my previous analysis employed multipliers (1.8–2.2) derived from U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis data on gross output-to-GDP ratios, without Polish-specific empirical grounding.

For Poland's economy in 2024, nominal GDP reached PLN 3.64 trillion. The turnover tax base—that is, total gross receipts of all taxable entities—depends on supply chain length, intermediate transaction intensity, and sectoral composition. Poland's economy features substantial manufacturing and complex supplier networks, potentially generating higher turnover-to-GDP ratios than service-dominated economies. However, definitive Polish data on aggregate gross output remains elusive in available sources.

Adopting a transparently uncertain approach, I employ a sensitivity range of multipliers from 1.5 to 2.5, acknowledging the absence of direct Polish empirical verification:

- At multiplier 1.5 (base PLN 5.46 trillion): State budget neutrality requires **3.4%**; full replacement (including ZUS) requires **10.3%**
- At multiplier 2.0 (base PLN 7.28 trillion): State budget neutrality requires **2.5%**; full replacement requires **7.7%**
- At multiplier 2.5 (base PLN 9.10 trillion): State budget neutrality requires **2.0%**; full replacement requires **6.2%**

These calculations assume universal coverage without exemptions—politically unsustainable. Exemptions for small enterprises, essential goods, or specific sectors would narrow the base and escalate required rates by 30–50%, pushing comprehensive replacement rates potentially to **8–12%**.

### Impact on Low-Margin Industries: Empirical Evidence from Polish Retail

The Critic appropriately challenged my previous margin estimates for Polish retail. Verified data from leading Polish retailers provides empirical grounding:

**Dino Polska (FY2024):**
- Revenue: PLN 29.3 billion
- EBITDA: PLN 2.32 billion
- **EBITDA margin: 7.9%** (down from 8.7% in 2023)
- Operating profit: PLN 1.91 billion (6.5% margin)
- Net profit: PLN 1.51 billion (5.1% margin)

**Żabka Group (FY2024):**
- System sales: PLN 27.3 billion
- Adjusted EBITDA: PLN 3.5 billion
- **Adjusted EBITDA margin: 12.8%**

These empirical figures indicate that Poland's leading retail operators achieve EBITDA margins of 8–13%, with net margins typically 2–4 percentage points lower. A universal turnover tax of 6–9% (required for comprehensive PIT/CIT/ZUS replacement) would consume 50–75% of EBITDA for major operators—manageable for established players but potentially existential for smaller competitors and new entrants.

The pathology of turnover taxation manifests most severely through **tax pyramiding**. Unlike value-added taxes, which apply only to value added at each stage with credit mechanisms preventing cascading, turnover taxes apply to gross receipts at every transaction. In Poland's retail supply chains—where goods flow from manufacturers through distributors, wholesalers, and finally to retailers—each intermediate transaction bears the full tax burden. By the time products reach consumers, embedded taxes have compounded through multiple production layers.

The Tax Foundation's research on gross receipts taxes demonstrates that "high-volume, low-margin businesses face disproportionate burdens that can be especially harsh, and perhaps confiscatory" (Tax Foundation, 2024). Polish retail's 8–13% EBITDA margins, while higher than my previous estimates, remain thin relative to the 6–9% turnover tax rates required for comprehensive replacement. A startup retailer investing heavily in expansion (generating high turnover but minimal or negative profits) would face crushing tax obligations, while established, profitable firms with lower turnover intensity would benefit—creating perverse competitive dynamics.

Research from the University of Connecticut School of Law indicates that turnover taxes reduce investment in machinery and equipment, discourage capital formation, and particularly disadvantage startups and loss-making enterprises that must pay tax regardless of profitability (Spring, 2022). This creates structural distortions favoring mature incumbents over innovative challengers—precisely the opposite of what dynamic competition requires.

### Constitutional and EU Law Compatibility

**Polish Constitutional Requirements:** Article 217 of the Constitution mandates that taxes be enacted by statute, with precise specification of taxable subjects, rates, reliefs, and exemptions. This requirement for statutory precision presents significant implementation challenges for unprecedented tax structures. The Constitutional Tribunal has demonstrated active scrutiny of tax legislation, including its 2023 ruling on structural definitions in the Local Taxes and Fees Act. Any universal turnover tax would require meticulously drafted legislation satisfying constitutional standards of legal certainty and non-retroactivity.

**EU Legal Framework:** The European Court of Justice's jurisprudence on turnover taxes establishes a complex landscape of constraints and possibilities. In *Commission v. Poland* (C-562/19 P) and *Commission v. Hungary* (C-596/19 P), the CJEU ruled in May 2021 that progressive turnover taxes on retailers did not constitute illegal state aid, provided they were not designed with "manifest discriminatory selectivity." The Court accepted turnover as "a relevant indicator of a person's ability to pay" commensurate with profits.

However, the conflict with the VAT Directive (Directive 2006/112/EC, Article 401) presents more substantial obstacles. Article 401 prohibits domestic taxes that "can be characterised as turnover taxes" having effects comparable to VAT. According to CJEU jurisprudence in *Vodafone Magyarország* (C-75/18), a tax infringes the VAT system if it exhibits cumulative essential characteristics of VAT: general application to goods and services, proportionality to price, collection at production/distribution stages, and final burden on consumers.

A universal turnover tax designed to replace PIT and CIT would likely meet these criteria and face challenge as functionally equivalent to VAT but without deduction rights. However, the CJEU has indicated that turnover taxes may survive scrutiny if they avoid all four essential VAT characteristics—for instance, by applying fixed amounts per transaction unrelated to price, or by applying at limited production stages.

Poland's experience with the retail sales tax introduced in 2016 provides empirical caution. Despite ultimate CJEU validation in 2021, the tax generated only PLN 4.63 billion in 2024 against theoretical potential of PLN 15–20 billion, reflecting years of legal uncertainty, partial implementation, and behavioral adaptation. This experience illustrates that even legally permissible turnover taxes face substantial implementation barriers.

### Macroeconomic and Fiscal Implications

PIT and CIT function as automatic stabilizers—revenues decline during recessions as profits and incomes fall, cushioning economic downturns. A turnover tax, being transaction-based, would exhibit procyclical or acyclical characteristics. During Poland's 2020 COVID-19 contraction, income tax revenues declined accommodate economic hardship. A turnover tax would have extracted similar or greater revenue even as millions of businesses faced revenue collapses, potentially accelerating bankruptcies and deepening recessionary spirals.

The distributional consequences present additional political economy challenges. Income taxes incorporate progressivity and ability-to-pay principles. A turnover tax, despite theoretical progressive rate structures, ultimately taxes transactions rather than economic capacity. A loss-making technology startup with high turnover (funding growth through investment) would face disproportionate burdens compared to a profitable rentier with minimal transaction volumes. This inversion of equity principles would face fierce resistance from high-growth sectors.

## 4. Recommended Strategy or Key Conclusions

This analysis, incorporating corrections and the Critic's valid concerns, leads to decisive conclusions regarding the viability of a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT in Poland.

### Key Findings:

**First**, the revenue replacement scope substantially exceeds initial calculations. Beyond the PLN 184.2 billion in state budget PIT and CIT revenues, comprehensive replacement must account for approximately PLN 30–40 billion in local government PIT/CIT shares (pre-2025 reform) and PLN 347 billion in ZUS premiums, bringing total replacement requirements to approximately PLN 560–570 billion. This triples the required turnover tax base compared to state-budget-only calculations.

**Second**, acknowledging empirical uncertainty regarding the GDP-to-turnover multiplier and using a conservative range of 1.5–2.5, the required turnover tax rates fall in these ranges:
- For state budget neutrality alone: **2.0–3.4%**
- For comprehensive replacement including ZUS and local governments: **6.2–10.3%**

Accounting for inevitable exemptions and base erosion, effective rates would likely reach **8–14%**.

**Third**, Poland's retail sector demonstrates EBITDA margins of 8–13% for leading operators, with smaller competitors likely achieving thinner margins. A turnover tax of 6–9% would consume 50–75% of EBITDA for major retailers and potentially exceed profits for marginal operators. Tax pyramiding through supply chains would amplify effective burdens, creating sectoral distress and competitive distortion favoring vertically integrated incumbents over independent retailers.

**Fourth**, constitutional requirements under Article 217 demand statutory precision for unprecedented tax structures, while EU law presents significant compatibility risks. Although the CJEU has validated progressive turnover taxes against state aid challenges, Article 401 of the VAT Directive prohibits taxes exhibiting essential VAT characteristics. A universal turnover tax replacing income taxes would likely face infringement proceedings and years of legal uncertainty.

**Fifth**, using 2024 planned budget figures versus actual execution introduces material discrepancies. State budget PIT execution (PLN 97.6 billion) fell short of plan (PLN 109.2 billion) by PLN 11.6 billion, while CIT execution (PLN 86.6 billion) exceeded plan (PLN 70.0 billion) by PLN 16.6 billion. Grounding calculations in realized rather than projected outcomes is essential.

**Sixth**, the macroeconomic stabilizer function of income taxation would be lost under a turnover tax regime. Automatic fiscal stabilization during recessionary periods would weaken, amplifying business cycle volatility and increasing procyclicality of public finances.

### Decision-Relevant Takeaways:

For policymakers contemplating fundamental tax reform in Poland, the universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT presents prohibitive obstacles on economic, legal, and fiscal grounds. The theoretical appeal of broad bases and low rates collides with the microeconomic reality of heterogeneous profit margins, the fiscal necessity of addressing local government revenues and social security contributions, the legal constraints of EU membership, and the political economy of business survival.

Alternative approaches offer superior efficiency and legal certainty:
- **VAT base broadening** eliminates tax pyramiding while maintaining consumption-tax logic
- **Income tax expenditure rationalization** improves existing system efficiency
- **Distributed profits taxation** reforms CIT without turnover-based distortions
- **ZUS contribution restructuring** addresses labor tax burden through payroll-based rather than turnover-based mechanisms

The Polish retail tax experiment demonstrates that even legally permissible turnover taxes fail as comprehensive revenue instruments when confronted with economic reality. A universal turnover tax adequate to replace Poland's income tax pillars would require rates so high as to trigger sectoral collapse, political resistance, and legal challenge—rendering it unviable as fiscal policy.
