# Analysis of Introducing a Universal Turnover Tax into the Polish Tax System: Revenue Neutrality, Sectoral Impact, and EU Law Compatibility

## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The proposal to replace Poland's Personal Income Tax (PIT) and Corporate Income Tax (CIT) with a universal turnover tax represents one of the most consequential structural reform options under consideration in European tax policy debates. The analytical challenge is multidimensional: it requires establishing the fiscal arithmetic—what rate would achieve budget neutrality given the specific structure of Polish public revenues—while simultaneously assessing whether the reform is legally viable under European Union law, particularly the VAT Directive's Article 401 prohibition on taxes equivalent to VAT. The distributional and sectoral implications, especially for low-margin industries such as retail trade that operate on thin profit margins relative to their gross revenues, must be carefully disaggregated from the headline rate to capture the effective burden after accounting for cascade amplification through supply chains. Finally, the administrative feasibility question—whether Poland's Krajowa Administracja Skarbowa (KAS) can effectively monitor compliance without VAT's self-policing invoice credit mechanism—determines whether the theoretical equilibrium rate is achievable in practice. Each of these analytical dimensions is necessary but not individually sufficient: a reform that clears the fiscal arithmetic test but fails EU law compatibility, or that is legally sound but administratively unworkable, is not a viable reform option.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### 3.1 The Corrected Fiscal Arithmetic: Revised Revenue Target and Implied Tax Rate

The cornerstone of any revenue-neutrality analysis for a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT is the accurate identification of the revenue pool that would need to be replaced. The 2025 local government financing reform has materially complicated this identification by shifting the distribution of PIT revenues between the state budget and municipalities, creating three distinct estimation methods with different implications for the revenue target.

**Method 1 (2024 base with growth rate):** Starting from the confirmed 2024 actual of PLN 97.6 billion and applying the observed 13.4% year-on-year growth from Jan–Nov 2025 execution data yields an estimated 2025 total public sector PIT of approximately PLN 110.7 billion. This method assumes the growth rate applies to the total PIT pool and that the 2024 figure is fully comparable.

**Method 2 (State budget extrapolation):** The Ministry of Finance estimated execution for 2025 shows state budget PIT of PLN 28.75 billion. At the new 58.6% municipal share (meaning the state receives approximately 41.4%), this implies total public sector PIT of approximately PLN 69.4 billion. This method assumes the state budget figure represents the complete state portion without the municipal redistribution having been factored in.

**Method 3 (Municipal share triangulation):** The 2024 municipal share was 50.6%, implying municipal PIT of approximately PLN 49.4 billion in 2024. The reform increased the municipal share to 58.6%, suggesting the municipal portion should grow proportionally. Adjusting for this and applying the observed growth rate yields approximately PLN 93–98 billion for total 2025 PIT.

The range of PLN 69–111 billion reflects genuine uncertainty in the data, with the most likely figure in the PLN 93–98 billion range based on the municipal share triangulation. For the purposes of this analysis, a central estimate of **PLN 95–100 billion** is adopted for total public sector PIT in 2025, acknowledging the uncertainty but grounding it in the confirmed 2024 actual and the directional effect of the reform.

For CIT, the verified 2024 actual of PLN 86.61 billion (Statista, citing Ministry of Finance) contrasts with the 2025 estimated state budget execution of PLN 63.69 billion. The discrepancy arises because the 2024 figure includes extraordinary one-off revenues from windfall profit levies and sectoral charges classified as CIT-equivalent, while the 2025 structural CIT base reflects underlying corporate profitability without such extraordinary items. A reasonable central estimate of total public sector CIT for 2025 is approximately **PLN 85–88 billion**, accounting for the cyclical recovery in corporate profitability.

The corrected combined PIT and CIT revenue target for 2025 is therefore approximately **PLN 180–188 billion** (central estimate: PLN 183 billion). This figure is lower than the PLN 200–202 billion sometimes cited, reflecting the correction for the PIT redistribution mechanics and the exclusion of extraordinary CIT-equivalent revenues.

**Revised Tax Rate Calculation:**

Using the corrected revenue target and the verified gross output multiplier:

- **Revised Revenue Target:** PLN 180–188 billion (central estimate: PLN 183 billion)
- **2025 Nominal GDP:** PLN 3,886–3,899 billion (central estimate: PLN 3,892 billion)
- **Estimated Tax Base (Gross Business Turnover):** Approximately PLN 7,084 billion (using the verified 2022 supply-use table multiplier of 1.82 applied to 2025 nominal GDP; sensitivity range of PLN 7,006–7,784 billion using multiplier 1.80–2.00)
- **Implied Turnover Tax Rate for Budget Neutrality:** PLN 183 billion ÷ PLN 7,084 billion = **2.58%** (central estimate)

Sensitivity analysis confirms that the implied rate is relatively stable across the plausible multiplier range: at 1.80, the rate is 2.57%; at 2.00, the rate falls to 2.31%. The central budget-neutral rate is therefore approximately **2.5–2.6%**, with a range of **2.3–2.8%** depending on the revenue target and multiplier assumptions.

Several caveats could push the required rate above this central estimate. Tax evasion and base erosion in a turnover tax system could reduce effective collection rates by 10–20% below theoretical yields, implying an upward adjustment of approximately 0.25–0.5 percentage points. The exclusion of very small enterprises from the turnover tax base—administratively necessary given Poland's approximately 3.3 million registered enterprises—would further erode the base by an estimated 10–15%, requiring a further upward rate adjustment of approximately 0.2–0.4 percentage points. Allowing for these factors, the practically required rate to achieve approximate budget neutrality could be in the range of **2.9–3.5%**—a figure considerably more relevant for practical policy purposes than the theoretical 2.58% central estimate.

### 3.2 The Cascade Effect: Quantifying the Amplification Risk

The cascade effect—the compounding of a turnover tax through successive stages of production and distribution in the absence of input tax deductibility—is the most structurally damaging feature of a multi-stage gross revenue tax. Standard economic theory confirms that in multi-stage supply chains, the effective cumulative tax burden can substantially exceed the nominal rate, regardless of whether the nominal rate is low.

Consider a simplified four-stage supply chain: raw materials supplier, manufacturer, wholesaler, and retailer. If each stage adds value proportionally and collects the nominal turnover tax on its output without deducting the tax paid on its inputs, the cumulative effective rate on the final consumer price depends on the value-added structure at each stage. In a typical supply chain where each stage accounts for 20–30% of the final price, and where the nominal rate is applied at each stage to the full value of output (including the value contributed by prior stages), the cumulative effective burden is approximately 1.15–1.25 times the nominal rate per stage.

For a nominal rate of 2.58%, a four-stage supply chain with roughly equal value-added shares would produce a cumulative effective burden of approximately 3.1–3.6% on the final consumer price (amplification factor of approximately 1.20–1.40). A five-stage chain with similar structure would produce approximately 3.4–4.0%. The amplification factor grows nonlinearly: at a nominal rate of 5%, the same four-stage chain would produce an effective rate of approximately 6.0–7.0%; at 10%, the effective rate could approach 13–16%.

The CJEU's reasoning in *Viking Motors* and *Tesco* explicitly identified the absence of deductibility as the characteristic that distinguishes turnover taxes from VAT and creates the cascade distortion. The 2025 VATupdate analysis confirms that the absence of any right to deduct amounts paid in previous stages is one of the four essential characteristics that, cumulatively, would make a tax a prohibited VAT-equivalent turnover tax under Article 401. At the required budget-neutral rate of approximately 2.58%, the cascade amplification would produce effective rates of 3.1–3.6% on final consumer prices—real but not catastrophic at these levels, but growing in distortionary potential as the nominal rate increases toward the practically required 2.9–3.5%.

More importantly, the cascade effect creates powerful incentives for vertical integration—firms are incentivised to internalise supply chain stages to reduce the number of transactions subject to the turnover tax. This represents a significant structural distortion of the economy's organisation, incentivising monopolistic concentration and reducing the vibrancy of intermediate markets.

### 3.3 Impact on Low-Margin Industries: The Retail Sector in Context

The corrected margin data from IBISWorld confirms net profit margins of 8.7% for Polish food retail in 2025, representing the weighted average across all formats—discount chains, supermarkets, convenience stores, hypermarkets, and small independent retailers. The USDA FAS data confirms that small independent retailers now represent only approximately 2% of total food retail sales, having been displaced by discount formats holding approximately 38% of the market. This concentration of market share among efficient, low-cost operators means the sector average margin is increasingly determined by the performance of large-format and discount retailers.

At a nominal rate of 2.58%, the direct turnover tax burden on a retailer with 8.7% net profit margins would be equivalent to approximately 30% of pre-tax profits—a substantial but absorbable burden for viable businesses with diversified revenues. The cascade amplification of approximately 3.1–3.6% effective rate on final consumer prices translates to gross margin compression of approximately 3.1–3.6 percentage points, reducing the effective net margin from 8.7% to approximately 5.1–5.6%. This represents approximately 36–41% of pre-tax profits absorbed by the tax—not an existential threat to large-format retailers but a meaningful reduction in profitability.

The sensitivity analysis of the combined ZUS plus turnover tax burden provides critical additional perspective. Consider a retail business with annual revenues of PLN 10 million, a gross payroll of PLN 2 million (20% of revenue), and a net profit margin of 8.7% (pre-tax profit of PLN 870,000). The employer's ZUS contributions on a gross salary of PLN 2 million amount to approximately PLN 384,000–448,000 annually (19.21%–22.41% of gross salary). The turnover tax at 2.58% of gross revenue amounts to PLN 258,000 annually. The combined ZUS plus turnover tax burden is PLN 642,000–706,000—representing approximately 74–81% of pre-tax profits of PLN 870,000. This figure illustrates the severity of the cumulative fixed cost burden for labour-intensive businesses with margins at the sector average.

Critically, the turnover tax creates a zero-profit trap that ZUS contributions do not. ZUS contributions are capped at 30 times the projected average annual salary, providing a natural ceiling for higher-earning employees. A turnover tax has no such cap and applies equally to all revenues regardless of profitability. During an economic downturn that reduces revenues by 15–20%—a plausible scenario given Poland's exposure to external demand shocks—a retail business with 8.7% margins could experience a decline to breakeven or loss. In that scenario, ZUS contributions would decline proportionally with payroll, but the turnover tax would remain a fixed charge on the full reduced revenue base. The combined burden as a percentage of pre-tax profits would rise from 74–81% to 90–120% of pre-tax profits—pushing viable businesses into insolvency.

For small independent retailers with estimated margins of 1–3% (the segment that has already been reduced to approximately 2% of the market), the effective tax burden at a nominal rate of 2.58% would represent 86–258% of their pre-tax profits. Combined with the ZUS burden and the operational challenges of competing with discount formats, this segment would face effective exit within one to two full business cycles.

The existing retail sales tax (podatek od sprzedaży detalicznej) adds a further layer of complexity. Applied at progressive rates of 0.8%–1.4% to large-format retail above the PLN 17 million monthly threshold, the retail sales tax generated approximately PLN 4.9 billion in state budget revenues in 2025. If a universal turnover tax were to absorb the retail sales tax rather than coexist with it, the effective cumulative burden on large-format retail would be capped at the universal turnover tax rate. If the retail sales tax were to coexist, the combined burden on affected retailers could approach or exceed the margin of many sub-segments, particularly during revenue contractions.

### 3.4 EU Law Compatibility: Article 401, State Aid, and Fundamental Freedoms

The compatibility of a universal turnover tax with EU law engages three distinct legal regimes, each with its own analytical framework and risk profile.

#### Article 401 of the VAT Directive: The Cascade Question and the Four-Characteristic Test

Article 401 of Council Directive 2006/112/EC provides that Member States may maintain or introduce taxes other than VAT—including turnover taxes—provided that their collection does not give rise, in trade between Member States, to formalities connected with the crossing of frontiers. The CJEU has consistently interpreted this provision to prohibit taxes that are "equivalent to VAT"—that is, taxes possessing the cumulative characteristics of VAT that the input credit mechanism is designed to prevent.

The 2025 VATupdate analysis of the gambling tax case before the CJEU provides the most current distillation of the CJEU's four-characteristic framework for distinguishing prohibited turnover taxes from permissible other taxes. A tax is not prohibited under Article 401 if it does not cumulatively possess all four of the following characteristics: (i) general application to transactions involving goods and services; (ii) levy at every stage of the production or distribution process; (iii) calculation on the gross value of transactions; and (iv) absence of any right for taxpayers to deduct amounts paid in previous stages.

For a universal turnover tax applied at every stage of the supply chain without input tax deductibility, the Article 401 analysis is considerably more challenging than for sector-specific or single-stage taxes. Such a universal turnover tax would possess three of the four characteristics—general application to transactions, calculation on gross value, and absence of deductibility—making it functionally equivalent to VAT in the sense that the CJEU's case law has consistently identified as prohibited. The IBFD's A Guide to the European VAT Directives 2025 explicitly categorises multi-stage cumulative cascade systems as the most legally exposed variant of turnover taxes under Article 401, noting that the CJEU's reasoning in *Viking Motors* and *Tesco* focuses specifically on the absence of deductibility and the multi-stage application as the distinguishing features of prohibited taxes.

The critical question is whether possessing three of the four characteristics is sufficient to trigger the Article 401 prohibition, or whether the prohibition requires all four characteristics to be present. The CJEU's language in *Tesco* and *Viking Motors* suggests a functional approach: the prohibition applies to taxes that, by their design and effect, function as VAT equivalents—specifically, taxes that combine multi-stage application with the absence of input deductibility in a manner that produces the same cascade distortion that VAT's input credit mechanism is designed to prevent. A tax that possesses three of the four characteristics—including specifically the absence of deductibility and multi-stage application—would be at serious legal risk under this functional analysis.

Two design alternatives merit consideration for their differential Article 401 risk profiles. The first is a **single-stage retail-level consumption tax**, applied only at the final sale to consumers. Such a design would lack the "every stage" characteristic and would most likely fall outside the Article 401 prohibition, as confirmed by the CJEU's rulings in *Tesco* and *Viking Motors* on sector-specific single-stage taxes. However, a single-stage tax applied only at retail would have a fundamentally different tax base from a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT: it would be a consumption tax equivalent in base to VAT (approximately 60–65% of GDP) rather than a gross revenue tax, and the revenue yield per percentage point of rate would be substantially lower. To generate the required PLN 180–188 billion in revenue from a consumption base of approximately PLN 2,400–2,600 billion (final consumption in Poland), a rate of approximately 6.9–7.8% would be required—nearly three times the VAT standard rate and politically untenable.

The second alternative is a **VAT-style turnover tax with full input tax deductibility**—a consumption-type turnover tax that functions as a VAT replacement. Such a design would resolve the Article 401 problem by mimicking the deductibility structure that defines VAT, but raises the fundamental question of why two consumption taxes would coexist and what would happen to the existing VAT. The introduction of a deductible turnover tax alongside VAT would constitute double taxation of consumption at different rates—a structurally irrational outcome that would create significant compliance complexity and economic distortion.

The most legally defensible design within Article 401 constraints—a single-stage retail tax—is therefore not a turnover tax in the conventional sense and would generate insufficient revenue at reasonable rates to replace PIT and CIT. This creates an irreducible tension between the policy objective and the EU law constraint: no design alternative fully resolves both the revenue requirement and the Article 401 prohibition simultaneously.

#### State Aid Rules (Articles 107–109 TFEU)

The State aid dimension of the turnover tax proposal is considerably more favourable following the CJEU's landmark rulings in *Commission v. Poland* (C-562/19 P) and *Commission v. Hungary* (C-596/19 P) of March 16, 2021. The CJEU upheld the General Court's annulment of the Commission's decisions finding that Poland's progressive retail tax constituted unlawful State aid, reasoning that progressive taxation based on ability to pay is a normal feature of direct tax systems and that the progressive rate structure did not per se constitute a selective advantage for smaller operators.

For a universal turnover tax with a flat rate applied uniformly to all entities above a de minimis threshold, the State aid risk is substantially lower than for sector-specific differentiated-rate taxes. A flat-rate universal turnover tax would apply to all economic operators without sectoral distinction, eliminating the differential treatment that is the prerequisite for selectivity under Article 107(1) TFEU. Even if the tax included de minimis thresholds or exemptions for very small businesses—administratively necessary for a turnover tax applied to approximately 3.3 million registered enterprises—these would represent standard features of tax systems across the EU and would not constitute selective advantages.

The more nuanced State aid risk relates to the interaction between a universal turnover tax and existing Polish sector-specific levies. If the turnover tax were to replace PIT and CIT while preserving the retail sales tax, mining tax, gambling tax, and financial institution asset tax within their existing frameworks, the cumulative tax burden on affected sectors would be higher than for unaffected sectors. Whether this constitutes a selective disadvantage under Article 107(1) depends on the characterisation of the reference tax system—a complex legal question on which the *Commission v. Poland* jurisprudence provides some guidance but no definitive answer. The risk is assessed as LOW to MODERATE for a flat-rate universal turnover tax, but MODERATE to HIGH for any design that retains sector-specific differentiation.

#### Fundamental Freedoms (Articles 49 and 54 TFEU)

A universal turnover tax applied to gross revenue regardless of profitability would create a fixed cost burden falling equally on all businesses operating in Poland—domestically established and foreign-controlled alike. This is, in principle, consistent with the CJEU's jurisprudence on fiscal neutrality: a tax applying equally to all economic operators does not discriminate on the basis of nationality and is therefore less likely to constitute a restriction on the freedom of establishment or the free movement of capital.

Asymmetries could arise in cross-border supply chains, where a domestic manufacturer pays turnover tax at each production stage while an importer may face the tax only at the distribution stage. This asymmetry—a consequence of the cascade effect—could be characterised as an indirect restriction on the free movement of goods, though it would likely be justified under the CJEU's broad margin of appreciation for Member States in fiscal sovereignty matters if the aggregate tax burden on domestic and imported products is equivalent. The practical demonstration of aggregate equivalence in cross-border supply chains is technically complex and would require careful legal and economic analysis. The risk is assessed as LOW to MODERATE.

### 3.5 Administrative Feasibility and KAS Capacity

The administrative challenge of introducing a universal turnover tax across Poland's approximately 3.3 million registered enterprises is substantial and cannot be dismissed as a secondary consideration. The introduction of a universal turnover tax would require a qualitatively different administrative infrastructure compared to the existing tax system.

Poland's Krajowa Administracja Skarbowa (KAS) has made significant progress in tax administration digitisation. The National e-Invoice System (KSeF) for mandatory e-invoicing, the JPK_CIT SAF-T file requirements for large CIT taxpayers (effective from tax years beginning after January 1, 2025 for entities with revenues exceeding EUR 50 million), and the ongoing expansion of real-time reporting infrastructure represent meaningful improvements in KAS's capacity to monitor business transactions (Crowe, 2025; Gov.pl, 2025). The International Tax Review's analysis of Poland's 2025 tax novelties confirms that Poland is simultaneously implementing OECD Pillar Two global minimum tax rules from January 1, 2025, adding further compliance complexity for large multinational groups.

However, while VAT compliance relies on the invoice credit mechanism to self-monitor the tax chain—a design feature that the CJEU has explicitly identified as the defining characteristic distinguishing VAT from prohibited turnover taxes—a turnover tax without input deductibility would require KAS to independently verify the accuracy of reported gross revenues across all enterprises and all sectors. The existing e-invoicing infrastructure would provide a valuable foundation for turnover tax administration, as it would give KAS visibility into transaction-level data across the economy. However, the transition from a VAT-based system to a turnover tax system—with the simultaneous wind-down of PIT and CIT collection—would create a multi-year period of dual compliance burden, administrative complexity, and revenue uncertainty.

Three specific administrative challenges warrant explicit consideration. First, the compliance burden on small and medium-sized enterprises would be acute. The existing PIT and CIT systems have well-established reporting mechanisms, software integrations, and tax representative arrangements. A transition to a turnover tax—with its different base (gross revenue vs. net income), different rate, different exemptions, and different compliance calendar—would require businesses to fundamentally redesign their tax reporting systems. For small enterprises operating on thin margins, these transition costs could be proportionally significant and could themselves trigger business closures that further erode the tax base.

Second, the simultaneous management of multiple tax system changes creates an administrative capacity bottleneck. Poland is simultaneously implementing the OECD Pillar Two global minimum tax (effective 2025), the local government financing reform (effective 2025), JPK_CIT expansion (phased through 2025), and the KSeF mandatory e-invoicing system. Adding a universal turnover tax to this list of concurrent reforms would overwhelm administrative capacity and create implementation delays that would further erode revenue during the transition period.

Third, the wind-down of PIT and CIT collection creates transitional revenue risk. The simultaneous elimination of PIT and CIT—the two largest direct tax revenue streams—while ramping up turnover tax collection would create a multi-year period during which the government's revenue profile would be fundamentally uncertain. This transitional revenue risk is particularly dangerous given Poland's already elevated fiscal deficit, estimated at 6.4% of GDP in 2025 by wiiw, which leaves little fiscal buffer to absorb revenue shortfalls during the transition.

### 3.6 Black Swan Scenarios: Capital Flight, Business Closures, and GDP Contraction

Three extreme but non-negligible scenarios merit consideration for their potential to cause the reform's revenue yield to diverge dramatically from projections.

**Scenario 1: Capital Flight and Business Relocation (Probability: 15–25%; Revenue Impact: -15 to -30%)**

If the introduction of a universal turnover tax creates a significantly higher effective tax burden on capital-intensive or high-margin sectors—particularly those with internationally mobile activities such as financial services, technology, and advanced manufacturing—multinational enterprises with operations in Poland could respond by relocating marginal activities to lower-tax jurisdictions. Poland's openness—with significant foreign direct investment and deep integration into European supply chains—makes it particularly exposed to this risk. The revenue impact could be a reduction in CIT revenues of 15–30% from baseline projections, representing a loss of PLN 13–26 billion annually, which would require an upward rate adjustment of approximately 0.4–0.7 percentage points to maintain neutrality.

**Scenario 2: Business Closures and Informal Economy Expansion (Probability: 20–30%; Revenue Impact: -10 to -20%)**

A turnover tax that imposes a fixed cost burden regardless of profitability creates a zero-profit trap for enterprises experiencing revenue shortfalls. The countercyclical amplification effect described above—where the turnover tax automatically increases the fixed cost burden precisely when revenues decline—is particularly dangerous for cyclical industries and could trigger a wave of business closures during the next economic downturn. The informal economy, estimated at 20–25% of GDP in Poland by some measures, could expand as businesses seek to escape the turnover tax burden through cash transactions and unrecorded sales. The combined effect could reduce the effective tax base by 10–20% from projections, representing a loss of PLN 18–38 billion annually and requiring an upward rate adjustment of approximately 0.5–1.0 percentage points.

**Scenario 3: GDP Contraction (Probability: 10–20%; Revenue Impact: -20 to -40%)**

The IMF's 2025 Article IV consultation projects Poland's real GDP growth at 3.6% for 2025, with the output gap expected to narrow toward potential by 2027. A shock that reduces GDP growth by 1–2 percentage points—whether due to the turnover tax reform itself, an external shock (such as a renewed escalation of the war in Ukraine or a European recession), or a combination—would reduce the tax base proportionally. A 20% reduction in the tax base would require an upward rate adjustment of approximately 0.6 percentage points to maintain neutrality. A 40% base contraction—which would be consistent with a severe recession scenario—would require an adjustment of approximately 1.2 percentage points, pushing the required rate from 2.58% to approximately 3.8%.

The combined probability of at least one black swan scenario occurring within the first five years of implementation is approximately 40–60%, with a combined expected revenue shortfall of approximately PLN 25–50 billion annually—representing 14–27% of the projected revenue target. This shortfall would require an upward rate adjustment of approximately 0.7–1.4 percentage points, pushing the practically required rate from 2.58% to approximately 3.3–4.0%.

## 4. Key Conclusions and Decision-Relevant Takeaways

The analysis yields five principal findings that policymakers considering this reform would need to confront:

**First, the corrected revenue-neutral turnover tax rate is approximately 2.58% in theory, but practically 3.0–4.0% after accounting for evasion, exclusions, and black swan risks.** The critical correction is that total public sector PIT and CIT revenue for 2025 is approximately PLN 180–188 billion, derived from confirmed 2024 PIT data of PLN 97.6 billion, estimated PIT for 2025 of PLN 95–100 billion accounting for the 2025 reform redistribution, and estimated total public sector CIT of PLN 85–88 billion. Applied to an estimated gross business turnover base of approximately PLN 7,084 billion, this yields a central budget-neutral rate of approximately 2.58%. Allowing for tax evasion (10–20% below theoretical yield), base exclusions for small enterprises (10–15% base erosion), and black swan scenario risks (14–27% expected revenue shortfall with 40–60% probability), the practically required rate is in the range of **3.0–4.0%**.

**Second, the retail sector would face significant but differentiated pressure, with small independent retailers most at risk of exit.** The IBISWorld-confirmed net profit margin of 8.7% for Polish food retail provides a meaningful buffer for large-format and discount retailers against a nominal 2.58% turnover tax burden, which at an effective rate of approximately 3.1–3.6% on final consumer prices (accounting for cascade amplification) would absorb approximately 36–41% of pre-tax profits. Small independent retailers with estimated margins of 1–3%—representing approximately 2% of the market—would face effective tax burdens representing 86–258% of their pre-tax profits. The interaction with the existing retail sales tax and the ZUS contribution burden creates cumulative fixed costs representing approximately 74–81% of pre-tax profits for labour-intensive retail businesses at the sector average margin—a combination immediately viability-threatening during revenue contractions. The ZUS plus turnover tax combined creates a countercyclical amplification effect: the fixed turnover tax charge increases as a percentage of profits precisely when businesses can least afford it, potentially pushing viable businesses into insolvency during downturns.

**Third, EU law compatibility is the most significant legal obstacle, and no design alternative fully resolves both the revenue requirement and the Article 401 prohibition.** A multi-stage gross revenue turnover tax—the design most consistent with replacing PIT and CIT—would possess three of the four CJEU-characteristic features of a prohibited VAT-equivalent tax (general application, gross value calculation, absence of deductibility), making it structurally exposed to Article 401 scrutiny under the CJEU's functional approach. A single-stage retail-level tax would be more legally defensible under Article 401 but would have a different base (consumption rather than gross revenue), generate insufficient revenue at politically viable rates (requiring approximately 6.9–7.8% to meet the revenue target), and would not be a turnover tax in the conventional sense. The State aid risk for a flat-rate universal turnover tax is LOW to MODERATE following *Commission v. Poland* (C-562/19 P), but the fundamental incompatibility of the multi-stage design with Article 401 is the most serious legal obstacle.

**Fourth, the reform would represent a regressive shift in the economic incidence of taxation from income to activity, with distributional consequences that extend across the entire economy.** The IMF's 2025 Article IV consultation explicitly identifies PIT as the primary revenue-side outlier in the Polish tax system—generating 5 percentage points of GDP less in revenues than the EU average—and recommends targeted base-broadening and progressivity improvements rather than structural reform of the tax base type. The IMF's recommendation reflects the specific nature of Poland's fiscal challenge: not a flaw in the fundamental design of the income tax system, but a combination of base erosion, exemptions, and administration gaps that targeted reforms can address without the disruption and transition risk of a fundamental tax base restructuring.

**Fifth, the transitional implementation risks are substantial and cannot be dismissed.** The simultaneous wind-down of PIT and CIT collection—deeply embedded in Poland's tax administration infrastructure, compliance processes, and municipal revenue-sharing arrangements—alongside the simultaneous ramp-up of turnover tax collection would create a multi-year period of dual compliance burden, revenue uncertainty, and administrative stress. Poland's KAS is simultaneously managing OECD Pillar Two implementation, JPK_CIT expansion, the local government financing reform, and KSeF mandatory e-invoicing—adding a universal turnover tax to this list of concurrent reforms would create an administrative capacity bottleneck. The black swan scenarios of capital flight, business closures, informal economy expansion, and GDP contraction during the transition period are not merely theoretical risks but plausible outcomes with 40–60% combined probability within the first five years, representing an expected revenue shortfall of PLN 25–50 billion annually.

In sum, while a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT is mathematically calculable at a rate of approximately 2.58%, the practically required rate of 3.0–4.0%, the irreducible EU law compatibility problem for multi-stage designs, the regressive distributional impact on small retailers and labour-intensive businesses, the countercyclical amplification of the ZUS plus turnover tax burden, and the transitional implementation risks collectively indicate that the IMF's recommendation of targeted base-broadening within the existing income tax framework represents a more pragmatic, legally less exposed, and administratively less disruptive path to addressing Poland's fiscal revenue gap than a fundamental restructuring of the tax base type.

Any serious consideration of the turnover tax alternative should be preceded by: (a) a detailed EU law compatibility study commissioned from qualified legal counsel addressing the post-2024 CJEU developments and the functional test for VAT-equivalence; (b) an empirical reassessment of the gross output multiplier using the most recent Statistics Poland supply-use tables (currently available for 2022, to be updated for 2023 and 2024); (c) a comprehensive administrative feasibility study from KAS assessing the incremental monitoring burden without VAT's self-policing mechanism; and (d) a microsimulation analysis of the distributional impact across retail sub-segments, disaggregating small independent retailers from large-format operators.
