# Asset Allocation Strategy for a Pension Fund Under Persistent Elevated Inflation (4–5% Annually) with Moderate Economic Growth in Developed Economies: A Five-Year Strategic Framework

---

## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The central challenge confronting this pension fund is a structural mismatch between its long-dated, inflation-linked liabilities and a capital market environment in which persistently elevated inflation erodes the real value of conventional fixed-income instruments, compresses price-to-earnings multiples, and introduces compounding uncertainty into actuarial assumptions. In a world of moderate but below-trend GDP growth in developed economies, the fund cannot rely on robust nominal earnings expansion to compensate for inflation's corrosive effect on purchasing power; it must instead engineer a portfolio in which real asset ownership, inflation-linked income streams, and disciplined risk positioning collectively generate a genuine real return — that is, a return above the 4–5% inflation baseline — while preserving sufficient liquidity and diversification to honour benefit obligations as they fall due.

---

## 2. Data Verification (Grounding)

Before presenting the strategic framework, it is essential to anchor the analysis in the most current data available, drawn from recent industry studies and market intelligence.

Global pension assets reached a record USD 68.3 trillion in 2025, rising 9.6% year-on-year, with defined contribution (DC) savings driving the majority of growth. Among the seven largest pension markets — Australia, Canada, Japan, Netherlands, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and the United States — DC plans now constitute 63% of total assets (Thinking Ahead Institute, 2026, February 9).

From an asset allocation perspective, public pension funds in the United States have materially restructured their portfolios in response to inflationary and volatile conditions. Average equity allocations fell to approximately 38% in the first half of 2025, down from 51.4% in the same period of 2021, while fixed income allocations increased modestly to 24.5% from 22.0%. Critically, total alternative investment allocations rose sharply to 31.8% in H1 2025 from 24.5% in H1 2021, reflecting a sustained structural shift toward illiquid, real-asset-heavy strategies (National Conference on Public Employee Retirement Systems, 2026).

Private equity and private credit remain central to pension fund strategy, with approximately 62% of global pension funds reporting allocations above their private equity targets as of mid-2025, according to S&P Global Market Intelligence. However, the nature of PE exposure has shifted: pension funds are favouring lower-leverage strategies, mid-market buyouts, growth equity, and sector-specialist funds with stronger organic cash flow generation, in part because elevated inflation pressures can erode portfolio company margins and lengthen exit timelines. Cash velocity — the ability to return capital to investors — has become a more important evaluation criterion than NAV growth (Cherry Bekaert, 2025).

Private infrastructure has emerged as a particularly attractive destination for pension capital, driven by its combination of predictable cash flows, long-term inflation-linked contractual revenues, and tangible asset appreciation. At current elevated base rates, private credit also offers improved risk-adjusted returns relative to the zero-interest-rate policy era, prompting pension funds to increase their stakes in floating-rate debt instruments (Chronograph, 2025).

T. Rowe Price's June 2025 research on capital market assumptions notes that fiscal support and AI-related investment should remain important growth drivers, and that inflation trends and central bank actions — particularly in the United States, where strong capital spending may keep yields relatively elevated — will be key determinants of portfolio outcomes over the forecast horizon.

Finally, 60% of pension funds direct sustainable investment allocations into venture capital, private equity, or private debt, and climate risk is considered a material investment factor by 82% of limited partners surveyed by PEI in 2025, reflecting the growing integration of long-term structural considerations into asset allocation decisions (PEI, 2025 LP Perspectives Survey).

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### 3.1 The Inflation Environment and Its Portfolio Implications

Persistently elevated inflation at 4–5% annually does not merely reduce the real return on fixed-income holdings; it fundamentally reshapes the risk-return trade-offs across virtually every asset class. Understanding these transmission mechanisms is prerequisite to constructing a coherent allocation framework.

For nominal bonds, the primary impact is the erosion of purchasing power in coupon and principal payments. While rising nominal yields that accompany inflation provide some补偿, the mark-to-market losses on existing holdings can be substantial, as evidenced by the 2022 bond market dislocations when simultaneous inflation and rate hikes produced double-digit losses in conventional fixed-income indices. Inflation-linked bonds, particularly Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), partially address this vulnerability, as their principal value adjusts semi-annually with changes in the Consumer Price Index, providing a direct hedge for the nominal fixed-income sleeve. However, TIPS also carry duration risk and their real yields can be volatile; in periods of deflation or falling inflation expectations, they underperform nominal Treasuries.

Equities, in theory, should provide an inflation hedge over sufficiently long horizons, as corporate revenues and earnings nominally grow with prices. However, elevated inflation creates a more nuanced picture. Companies with strong pricing power — particularly in consumer staples, energy, and selected technology subsectors — can pass on rising costs to customers and protect profit margins. Companies lacking pricing power, or those with high operating leverage and fixed costs, suffer margin compression. Moreover, the equity risk premium tends to compress when inflation is elevated, because the discount rate applied to future cash flows rises with nominal interest rates, placing downward pressure on price-to-earnings multiples even if earnings growth remains positive in nominal terms. Historical data from the 1970s suggests that equity markets can deliver positive nominal returns during high-inflation periods, but with significantly higher volatility and a wide dispersion of outcomes across sectors.

Real assets — encompassing real estate, infrastructure, commodities, and natural resource equities — have the most direct and intuitive relationship with inflation. Real estate values and rental income tend to rise with replacement costs and consumer prices over the medium term; infrastructure assets with concession agreements often incorporate explicit inflation escalation clauses. Commodities are perhaps the most immediate and pure inflation hedge, as their spot prices respond quickly to changes in the general price level. Gold, while often touted as an inflation hedge, has a more complex relationship with inflation — it performed well during the 1970s but delivered mixed results relative to CPI during the 2010s, suggesting that its role is better characterised as a tail-risk hedge and portfolio diversifier rather than a reliable inflation-specific exposure.

The moderate growth backdrop is equally consequential. In a high-inflation, low-growth "stagflationary" scenario — which this framework deliberately avoids by stipulating moderate rather than recessionary growth — the allocation problem becomes considerably more acute, because neither equities nor conventional fixed income can be relied upon to deliver positive real returns with any confidence. The moderate growth assumption provides a critical anchor: developed economy GDP growth in the range of 1.5–2.5% annually implies that corporate earnings can grow in nominal terms at 5–7% (assuming a modest 2–3% inflation assumption embedded in nominal GDP), providing fundamental support for equity valuations even as the discount rate remains elevated.

### 3.2 Liability-Driven Considerations

Pension funds operate under a unique constraint: the actuarial liability. For a typical defined-benefit pension fund, the present value of future benefit obligations is computed using a discount rate — often linked to high-quality corporate bond yields — that must be compared against portfolio returns. In an elevated inflation environment, the fund faces two simultaneous pressures. First, if its liabilities are indexed to inflation (as many public sector and final-salary pension obligations are), the absolute size of the liability grows with CPI, requiring higher nominal returns to maintain funded status. Second, the discount rate used to present-value the liability may rise with nominal yields, partially offsetting the liability growth, but the net effect is typically negative for funded status when inflation is high and rate adjustments lag behind price increases.

This liability structure argues strongly for an asset allocation that is explicitly liability-aware — a principle broadly known as liability-driven investing (LDI). In practice, this means maintaining a core of high-quality, duration-matched fixed-income assets (including inflation-linked bonds) that move in concert with the liability, and supplementing this core with real-return assets that are expected to outperform CPI over the medium to long run, thereby reducing the funded status deficit.

### 3.3 The Role of Alternatives in an Inflationary Environment

The dramatic reallocation toward alternatives documented in the industry data — from 24.5% in 2021 to 31.8% in 2025 for public pensions in the United States — reflects a structural recognition that liquid, mark-to-market asset classes alone cannot deliver the combination of inflation protection, stable cash flows, and illiquidity premium that pension fund liabilities demand.

Private infrastructure is particularly well-suited to the current environment. Assets such as regulated utilities, toll roads, airports, water treatment facilities, and renewable energy projects typically operate under long-term concession agreements with revenues tied to regulated tariffs that escalate with inflation. The cash flow predictability — often resembling senior debt in its stability — makes infrastructure an ideal bridge asset between fixed income and equities, providing a real return with lower volatility than listed equities. The illiquidity premium, historically in the range of 150–300 basis points over comparable public market equivalents, compensates the fund for locking up capital.

Private credit, particularly direct lending to mid-market companies, offers floating-rate exposure that is structurally advantaged in a rising-rate environment. As base rates remain elevated at 4–5%, the all-in yield on direct lending strategies — typically SOFR plus 500–700 basis points — provides attractive real returns, and the floating-rate structure means that the income stream rises with inflation (or at least with monetary policy rates). For pension funds with moderate liquidity needs and a 5–7 year investment horizon, direct lending can be a core alternative allocation.

Private equity, while more challenged in an inflationary environment — particularly for leveraged buyouts where debt service becomes more burdensome and exit multiples may compress — still offers potential value in growth equity and venture capital strategies focused on productivity-enhancing and AI-adjacent sectors. The key is selectivity: favouring businesses with durable competitive moats, strong pricing power, and manageable leverage rather than highly leveraged financial engineering strategies.

Real assets through open-end or interval fund structures provide daily or quarterly liquidity while maintaining the underlying inflation linkage of direct real estate, infrastructure, and natural resources. These vehicles have grown substantially in sophistication and scale over the past decade, making them increasingly accessible for pension fund allocations.

### 3.4 Geopolitical and Structural Considerations

The current investment landscape is shaped by several structural forces beyond inflation and growth. Geopolitical fragmentation is prompting pension funds to reassess geographic allocations, with some US state plans — including Kansas and Texas — actively divesting from Chinese securities in response to political mandates (Chronograph, 2025). While this creates country-specific reallocation dynamics, the broader implication is that pension funds must increasingly price geopolitical risk into their asset allocation decisions, potentially requiring broader diversification across non-correlated markets and careful consideration of supply chain resilience in their private asset portfolios.

Technological disruption, particularly the rapid adoption of generative AI, is reshaping corporate earnings trajectories and sector-level inflation dynamics. AI-driven productivity gains could partially offset inflationary pressures in labour-intensive sectors, but they also introduce concentrated technology exposure and valuation risk for pension funds that increase their technology allocations in search of growth.

Climate change, acknowledged as a material investment risk by 82% of institutional investors (PEI, 2025), creates both physical risk (damage to real asset portfolios) and transition risk (stranded assets in carbon-intensive industries). For a pension fund with a multi-decade horizon, integrating climate scenario analysis into asset allocation is not merely an ethical imperative but a risk management necessity.

### 3.5 Liquidity Management

A frequently underweighted dimension of asset allocation in inflationary environments is liquidity. Elevated inflation often coincides with elevated market volatility, and pension funds with large illiquid alternative allocations may find themselves forced to meet benefit payments or rebalance mandates by selling liquid assets at inopportune times. The industry trend toward increasing alternative allocations — while strategically sound — must be balanced against liquidity stress testing. A common framework is to ensure that at least 18–24 months of projected benefit payments are maintained in liquid or semi-liquid instruments, even as the majority of the portfolio pursues the real-return and inflation-protection objectives described above.

## 4. Recommended Asset Allocation Strategy

Drawing together the macroeconomic analysis, the empirical data on current pension fund practice, and the liability-driven imperatives of the fund, the following five-year asset allocation strategy is proposed. The framework is structured around four strategic pillars: liability matching, real-asset inflation protection, return-seeking diversification, and risk management. All figures are indicative target allocations with permitted tactical deviations of ±3–5 percentage points under the fund's investment policy framework.

### 4.1 Pillar 1 — Liability Matching and Inflation-Linked Fixed Income (Target: 25–30% of Total Assets)

The foundation of the portfolio must be a high-quality fixed-income sleeve that explicitly addresses the inflation-linked nature of the fund's actuarial liabilities. Within this pillar, two sub-allocations are recommended.

First, direct Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) and inflation-linked government bonds from sovereign issuers with credible fiscal positions — principally the United States, Germany, and the United Kingdom — should constitute approximately 12–15% of total assets. These instruments provide a direct, liquid, and duration-matched hedge against the inflation component of the liability. Their real yield, rather than nominal yield, is the relevant return metric, and in the current environment of positive real yields on TIPS, they offer genuine real return generation rather than mere nominal preservation.

Second, investment-grade corporate bonds with inflation-linked features — including floating-rate notes and collateralised loan obligations (CLOs) that reset with short-term rates — should constitute approximately 10–12% of total assets. The floating-rate component ensures that income keeps pace with monetary policy rates in an inflationary environment, while the investment-grade credit rating maintains portfolio quality. In the current base rate environment, these instruments can deliver all-in yields in the range of 5.5–7.0%, providing meaningful real returns above the inflation baseline.

The combined fixed-income sleeve, at 25–30% of assets, should be duration-managed to approximate the duration of the actuarial liability, with periodic rebalancing as the liability profile evolves. The key insight is that nominal government bonds alone are insufficient in an elevated inflation scenario; the inflation linkage must be explicit and direct.

### 4.2 Pillar 2 — Real Assets with Inflation Escalation (Target: 20–25% of Total Assets)

The real-asset sleeve is the portfolio's primary engine of real return generation and inflation outperformance. Three sub-categories are recommended.

Core listed real estate investment trusts (REITs) and unlisted real estate funds targeting commercial, industrial, and residential properties in supply-constrained markets should constitute approximately 8–10% of total assets. REITs offer liquidity and transparency through public market listings while providing the inflation-linkage benefits of direct property ownership, including rental income that tends to escalate with market rents and replacement costs. The 2020–2025 period demonstrated both the resilience of logistics and residential REITs during inflationary episodes and their vulnerability to rising interest rates, underscoring the importance of selecting sub-sectors with structural demand tailwinds and limited rate sensitivity.

Private infrastructure — encompassing regulated utilities, transportation assets, digital infrastructure (data centres, fibre networks), and renewable energy projects — should constitute approximately 8–10% of total assets. As noted in industry research, infrastructure assets with explicit inflation escalation clauses in their concession agreements represent perhaps the most direct inflation hedge available to institutional investors (Chronograph, 2025). The predictable, long-duration cash flows are particularly valuable for matching long-dated pension liabilities. Infrastructure investments can be accessed through open-end infrastructure funds, co-investments alongside established general partners, and selectively through listed infrastructure equities for liquidity management purposes.

Commodities and natural resource equities should constitute approximately 4–5% of total assets. A diversified commodity exposure — whether through total return swaps, commodity futures indices, or commodity-focused private equity — provides the most immediate and direct hedge against CPI surprises and commodity price spikes. Natural resource equities, including energy and mining companies with strong balance sheets and disciplined capital allocation, offer a hybrid exposure: the inflation linkage of commodities combined with the equity risk premium and potential dividend yields of equity ownership. The weighting is kept modest (4–5%) because commodities do not generate income, exhibit high volatility, and can underperform during economic slowdowns — risks that are partially mitigated but not eliminated by the moderate growth assumption.

### 4.3 Pillar 3 — Return-Seeking Equities and Private Alternatives (Target: 35–40% of Total Assets)

The return-seeking sleeve is where the fund captures the equity risk premium and illiquidity premium necessary to outpace its 4–5% inflation benchmark on a portfolio-wide basis. The recommended allocation represents a deliberate shift from the conventional 50–55% equity allocation typical of pension funds in the pre-2020 era, reflecting the changed capital market assumptions and the need to reduce dependence on any single asset class.

Developed market equities should constitute approximately 18–22% of total assets, with a deliberate tilt toward sectors that have historically demonstrated pricing power during inflationary periods: consumer staples, energy, healthcare, and select technology companies with dominant market positions and high free cash flow conversion. The geographic allocation should be broadly diversified across the United States, European Union, United Kingdom, and Japan, with a combined target of 50–60% of the equity sleeve in the United States reflecting its larger weight in global market capitalisation. The moderate growth assumption provides fundamental support for equity earnings, while the inflation backdrop argues for a value-tilt within the equity allocation, as value stocks (with lower duration characteristics in earnings terms) tend to outperform growth stocks during periods of rising real yields and elevated inflation.

Emerging market equities, at 5–7% of total assets, should be approached with greater selectivity given geopolitical risks and the currency volatility that often accompanies inflationary pressures in developing economies. The allocation should focus on commodity-exporting emerging markets (which benefit from global inflation) and Asia ex-China for structural growth exposure, while reducing or eliminating single-country exposures that carry significant geopolitical tail risk. Currency hedging should be actively managed for this sleeve.

Private equity — encompassing buyouts, growth equity, and venture capital — should constitute approximately 7–10% of total assets. Consistent with the industry trend toward higher PE allocations and the data indicating that 62% of pension funds are overweight their PE targets, this sleeve is justified by the historical illiquidity premium and the potential for outperformance relative to public markets over a five-year horizon. However, the composition of the PE sleeve should differ materially from the pre-2020 consensus. Leveraged buyouts with high financial leverage are disadvantaged in an elevated inflation environment, as rising interest expenses compress returns and uncertain exit multiples create valuation risk. Instead, the allocation should favour mid-market buyouts with moderate leverage, growth equity strategies in sectors benefiting from structural tailwinds (healthcare technology, climate solutions, AI infrastructure), and co-investments alongside established general partners to reduce fee drag and improve cash velocity. The emphasis on demonstrated cash flow generation and capital return discipline, as highlighted by current LP evaluation criteria (Cherry Bekaert, 2025), should govern manager selection.

Private credit, at 5–7% of total assets, provides floating-rate income with a premium over public credit markets. In the current base rate environment, direct lending to middle-market companies can generate all-in yields of 8–12%, representing a compelling real return above the inflation baseline with default risk managed through careful credit selection and diversification. This allocation benefits from the elevated rate environment while avoiding the duration risk of fixed-rate bonds.

### 4.4 Pillar 4 — Risk Management and Tactical Reserves (Target: 10–15% of Total Assets)

The final pillar addresses portfolio resilience, opportunistic deployment, and liquidity management. This does not imply sitting idle in cash — the inflation erosion of cash holdings is the primary risk — but rather maintaining a diversified set of liquid, defensive assets that can be deployed tactically as market conditions evolve.

Investment-grade short-duration bonds and money market instruments (including government securities, commercial paper, and short-maturity corporate bonds) should constitute approximately 5–7% of total assets. The duration of this sleeve should be kept short (under two years) to minimise mark-to-market sensitivity to rate changes while maintaining the option to extend duration when real yields are attractive. These instruments also serve as the fund's primary liquidity buffer, ensuring that benefit payments can be met without forced liquidations of illiquid assets.

Gold and other precious metals, at 2–3% of total assets, serve as a tail-risk hedge and portfolio diversifier. While gold's inflation-hedging credentials are nuanced, it retains its role as a store of value during currency debasement episodes, geopolitical crises, and systemic financial stress — risks that are non-trivial in the current environment of elevated geopolitical tension and fiscal imbalance in developed economies.

Tactical and opportunistic allocations — including secondaries, rescue financing, and structured credit — should constitute approximately 3–5% of total assets. These strategies offer superior risk-adjusted returns in dislocated market conditions, and the current environment of elevated uncertainty creates periodic opportunities for well-capitalised investors to earn premium returns. Secondaries, in particular, have emerged as a favoured strategy for pension funds seeking to access private market returns with reduced blind-pool risk and shorter effective duration (Chronograph, 2025).

## 4.5 Implementation and Governance Considerations

**Rebalancing Discipline.** With a five-year horizon and elevated market volatility, the rebalancing framework must be rules-based but flexible enough to accommodate tactical opportunities. A corridor-based rebalancing approach — where deviations of more than ±3–5 percentage points from target trigger rebalancing — balances transaction costs against the risk of systematic drift. The illiquid alternative allocations (private equity, private credit, private infrastructure) should be rebalanced on a commitment pacing basis rather than a market-value basis, as their illiquidity makes immediate rebalancing impractical.

**Liability-Driven Investment Overlay.** The fixed-income and real-asset sleeves should be monitored against liability proxy indices that reflect the fund's specific actuarial assumptions. When funded status improves, the return-seeking sleeve can be modestly increased; when funded status deteriorates, the liability-matching sleeve should be reinforced. This dynamic approach prevents the common error of increasing risk precisely when the funded status is weakest.

**Manager Selection in Alternatives.** Given that the recommended framework allocates approximately 30–35% of assets to alternative investments, manager selection is not a secondary concern — it is the primary determinant of whether the theoretical return premiums of these asset classes are actually realised. The fund should concentrate commitments among established general partners with proven track records, diversified across vintage years to avoid concentration risk, and supplemented by co-investments to reduce fee drag. The shift toward evaluating managers on cash velocity and capital return discipline, as documented in the industry data, should be formalised in the fund's manager evaluation framework.

**ESG and Climate Integration.** Consistent with the data indicating that climate risk is a material consideration for 82% of institutional investors, the fund should integrate climate scenario analysis into its asset allocation process. For real assets, this means conducting physical risk assessments on property and infrastructure portfolios; for private equity, it means incorporating transition risk into credit analysis; for public equities, it means applying systematic ESG screens alongside financial analysis. The goal is not to sacrifice returns for ethical considerations, but to ensure that structural risks are priced into the portfolio rather than ignored.

**Currency Risk Management.** Approximately 45–50% of the fund's equity and alternative exposure will be denominated in currencies outside the base currency of the liability. For major currency pairs (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY), a partial hedging programme — covering 50–70% of foreign currency exposure — should be implemented using rolling forward contracts. The remaining unhedged exposure is intentional, as it provides diversification benefit and captures the equity risk premium associated with foreign currency exposure. For emerging market currencies, hedging costs and liquidity constraints argue for a more selective approach, focusing hedging activity on the highest-conviction positions.

## 4.6 Scenario Analysis and Stress Testing

The proposed allocation should be evaluated against three representative scenarios over the five-year horizon to ensure robustness.

Under a **baseline scenario** — moderate growth of 1.5–2.5%, inflation of 4–5%, and central banks maintaining restrictive but stable policy rates — the portfolio is expected to generate a nominal return in the range of 6.5–8.0% annually, representing a real return of approximately 1.5–3.5% above inflation. The TIPS sleeve provides the inflation hedge, infrastructure and private credit generate stable real income, and equities contribute growth-oriented returns. Funded status is expected to improve modestly if actuarial assumptions are met.

Under a **stagflationary scenario** — growth below 1%, inflation above 5% — the real-return assets (infrastructure, commodities, TIPS) become the portfolio's most important contributors, while equity and private credit returns face headwinds. The short-duration fixed-income positioning prevents the mark-to-market losses that devastated long-duration bond portfolios in 2022. Portfolio real returns may compress to near zero or marginally negative, but the funded status deterioration should be limited relative to a conventional high-equity, long-duration portfolio.

Under an **inflation resolution scenario** — in which central banks succeed in bringing inflation back to the 2–3% target over two to three years, and developed economy growth accelerates to 2.5–3.5% — the equity and private equity sleeves should benefit from the simultaneous expansion of earnings multiples and earnings growth. Infrastructure and TIPS, while their inflation outperformance moderates, will have locked in real returns during the high-inflation period. The portfolio is well-positioned to benefit from any such scenario by maintaining the diversified structure rather than making dramatic shifts based on near-term inflation forecasts.

## 5. Key Conclusions and Decision-Relevant Takeaways

The proposed allocation strategy rests on five foundational conclusions that the fund's investment committee and trustees should internalise as guiding principles over the five-year horizon.

First, the fund must explicitly abandon the pre-2020 consensus that a conventional 60/40 equity-bond portfolio is the default optimal allocation. The structural shift toward elevated and persistent inflation, combined with the re-pricing of the equity risk premium in a higher-rate environment, means that the 60/40 portfolio's expected real return is materially lower than historical averages, while its inflation risk is inadequately addressed. The 2025 industry data confirms that leading pension funds have already migrated toward this conclusion, with average equity allocations falling to 38% and alternative allocations rising above 30%.

Second, real assets — specifically private infrastructure, inflation-linked real estate, and selective commodities — are not an optional enhancement but a structural necessity. Their unique combination of inflation linkage, long-duration cash flows, and illiquidity premium makes them the primary vehicle through which the fund can close the gap between its required real return and the return available from conventional asset classes. An allocation of 20–25% to real assets, while higher than historical norms for many funds, is fully justified by the current and projected capital market environment.

Third, private alternatives — private equity and private credit — deserve a substantial but reformed allocation. The key is not whether to invest in private markets, but how. The evidence from recent years suggests that leverage-heavy, financial-engineering-dependent buyout strategies face structural headwinds in an inflationary environment, while cash-generative growth strategies and floating-rate credit are structurally advantaged. The fund should apply these criteria rigorously in manager selection and vintage-year pacing.

Fourth, the liability-matching programme using TIPS and inflation-linked bonds is the portfolio's non-negotiable foundation. No matter how attractive real assets and equities may appear, the pension fund's primary fiduciary duty is to meet its obligations to beneficiaries. The TIPS and floating-rate credit allocation, at 25–30% of assets, should be managed with the same actuarial discipline as the liability itself, with duration matched and rebalancing triggered by liability movements rather than market timing.

Fifth, governance and manager selection are not soft considerations but the primary determinants of net-of-fee alpha generation, particularly in private markets. With 30–35% of assets in alternatives, the difference between top-quartile and median manager performance — typically 200–400 basis points annually — can add up to 1–2 percentage points of return enhancement or impairment on the total portfolio. The fund should invest in building internal investment management capabilities, co-investment relationships, and rigorous performance attribution frameworks to ensure that the theoretical return premiums of its alternative allocations are actually captured.

In sum, this five-year asset allocation strategy is designed not merely to survive the elevated inflation environment but to generate genuine real returns above the 4–5% inflation baseline, while maintaining the liquidity, diversification, and risk discipline that a pension fund's fiduciary obligations demand. The strategy is deliberately positioned away from the conventional 60/40 model toward a more resilient, multi-asset structure in which real assets, inflation-linked securities, and return-seeking private alternatives collectively deliver the risk-adjusted performance that the fund's beneficiaries are entitled to expect over the long run.
