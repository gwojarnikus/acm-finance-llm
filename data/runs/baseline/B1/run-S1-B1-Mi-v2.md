## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The introduction of a universal turnover tax to fully replace Poland's Personal Income Tax (PIT) and Corporate Income Tax (CIT) constitutes one of the most radical restructuring proposals that could be contemplated within the Polish fiscal system. Such a reform would fundamentally alter the relationship between tax liability and economic activity, shifting from an income-based assessment model to one grounded entirely in transactional volume. The core challenge lies in estimating a rate that generates budget-neutral revenue while confronting three deeply interconnected problems: the macroeconomic distortion inherent in taxing gross turnover rather than net income, the structural devastation it would inflict on low-margin industries such as retail trade, and its fraught compatibility with the European Union's legal architecture governing indirect taxation and the internal market. This analysis addresses all three dimensions using current Ministry of Finance data, sector-level evidence, and the evolving body of CJEU jurisprudence on turnover-based impositions.

The key distinction is that a "true" turnover tax does not allow input tax credit and is not levied at every stage of production and distribution (IBFD, 2024; Kofler, 2021; Lexxion EU State Aid, 2024).

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### 3.1 The Fiscal Arithmetic: Calculating the Required Turnover Tax Rate

The starting point of any rigorous analysis is a clear quantification of the revenue baseline that the proposed turnover tax would need to replace. According to the most recently available full-year data, PIT yielded approximately PLN 97.6 billion in 2024, while CIT produced PLN 86.61 billion (Ministry of Finance, 2025; Statista, 2025). The combined revenue from both income taxes therefore amounts to roughly PLN 184.2 billion per annum. It is this sum that a hypothetical universal turnover tax would need to replicate to achieve genuine budget neutrality at the central government level, setting aside the more complex question of local government financing implications.

The fundamental challenge, however, lies in the denominator. The rate of a turnover tax is calculated not against income or profit, but against the gross value of all taxable transactions. The relevant denominator is therefore total economy-wide turnover, which is substantially larger than GDP because it captures intermediate transactions throughout the production and distribution chain. GDP, by contrast, measures only value added at each stage—that is, the final contribution to output after subtracting intermediate consumption.

To estimate the total turnover base, economists typically apply a ratio derived from the relationship between total sales (as captured through VAT-compliant transaction data) and GDP. In the Polish economy, the ratio of total VATable turnover to GDP is estimated at approximately 2.2 to 2.8 times, reflecting the economy's extensive supply chains and significant intermediate sector. For the purposes of a conservative estimate, this analysis adopts a midpoint multiplier of 2.5, implying that total economy-wide turnover in 2024 was approximately PLN 9,044 billion (PLN 3,618 billion × 2.5).

Dividing the required revenue base (PLN 184.2 billion) by this estimated total turnover base yields a required turnover tax rate of approximately **2.04%**. However, this calculation assumes several important simplifications: first, that the tax base is perfectly coextensive with the estimated turnover figure; second, that there are no exemptions, reduced rates, or thresholds; and third, that the behavioural response of economic actors (in terms of tax evasion, transaction restructuring, or demand destruction) can be ignored.

If the multiplier is set more conservatively at 2.0, the required rate rises to approximately 2.5%. If the multiplier is 3.0, reflecting a more complex supply chain structure, the rate falls to approximately 1.7%. The true budget-neutral rate, therefore, almost certainly lies in the range of **1.7% to 2.5%**, with a central estimate of approximately **2.0%**.

It should be emphasised that this rate applies uniformly to all B2B and B2C transactions throughout the economy. The apparent simplicity of a single rate conceals enormous distributional complexity. A retailer's turnover includes the full selling price of goods sold, not merely its margin. A discounter generating gross revenues of PLN 20 billion but a net margin of only 2% would pay PLN 400 million in turnover tax on profits of PLN 400 million—effectively an additional 100% tax on profit. This mechanical operation of a turnover tax lies at the heart of its most severe economic distortions.

### 3.2 The Structural Problem of Low-Margin Industries: The Case of Retail Trade

Poland's retail sector deserves particular scrutiny not only because of its economic significance—it accounts for approximately 13% of GDP and represents one of the largest employers in the services sector—but because it epitomises the structural vulnerability of low-margin businesses to turnover-based taxation (EMIS Insights, 2025).

The Polish retail market is characterised by an intensely competitive, price-sensitive structure. As of 2024, discounters held approximately 38% of total food retail sales, convenience outlets commanded 25%, while supermarkets (15%) and hypermarkets (5%) served primarily urban middle-class consumers (Euromonitor via USDA FAS, 2025). This market architecture reflects a consumer base that remains acutely price-conscious following the high-inflation period of 2022–2023, during which food price inflation peaked at 14.4% (Green Seed Group, 2025). Polish households allocate over 27% of disposable income to food and beverage purchases, making them among the most food-expenditure-intensive consumers in the EU (USDA FAS, 2025).

The net profit margins of the dominant retail formats are notoriously thin. Discount chains such as Biedronka (Jerónimo Martins) and Lidl operate on EBIT margins typically in the range of 3–5%, with net margins after financing costs often falling to 1.5–3.0%. This reflects the fundamental economics of the discount model: high volumes, low prices, and stringent cost control. Supermarkets and hypermarkets tend to report slightly higher gross margins (20–30% on cost of goods sold) but face significantly higher operating costs (real estate, labour, logistics), compressing net margins to 2–4%. Smaller convenience stores and independent grocers, facing competitive pressure from franchise networks such as Żabka, frequently operate at net margins below 2% (EMIS Insights, 2025).

Against this backdrop, consider the impact of a 2% turnover tax. For a discounter with a net margin of 3%, the turnover tax of 2% represents approximately 67% of pre-tax profit. For an independent grocer operating at a 1.5% margin, the turnover tax of 2% exceeds total profit, transforming a profitable or marginally viable business into a loss-making enterprise. The tax is, in effect, levied on gross revenues rather than on the value created by the business, making it fundamentally regressive from a profitability standpoint.

The cascading effects extend well beyond the immediate profit-and-loss statement. Retailers would face an acute dilemma: absorb the additional tax cost, which further compresses already thin margins and potentially triggers losses; or pass the cost forward to consumers through higher prices, which in a price-sensitive market would suppress demand and reduce sales volumes; or attempt to renegotiate supply contracts to shift the burden backward to suppliers, which would in turn destabilise the agricultural and manufacturing sectors upstream.

There is also a structural dimension to the problem. Large retail chains, particularly multinational discounters, have greater capacity to reorganise their supply chains, shift taxable activities to lower-tax jurisdictions, or utilise transfer pricing mechanisms to allocate profits to entities in more favourable tax environments. Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which constitute the overwhelming majority of retail establishments in Poland, lack these capabilities. The turnover tax would therefore exert a disproportionate competitive pressure on SMEs, potentially accelerating the consolidation of the Polish retail market in favour of large chain operators. This represents a significant anti-competitive consequence that would likely attract scrutiny under both EU and Polish competition law.

Furthermore, the retail sector's GVA growth of merely 0.43% in 2023 and 3.0% in 2024, compared to overall GDP growth of 2.9% in 2024 (EMIS Insights, 2025), underscores its structural vulnerability. A sector barely growing in real terms, facing intense price competition, elevated labour costs (Poland's minimum wage reached PLN 4,666 per month as of January 2025, a significant increase), and post-inflationary consumer caution, would be severely strained by a new tax burden calculated on gross revenues rather than profitability.

### 3.3 The Compatibility of a Universal Turnover Tax with European Union Law

The EU law dimension of this proposal is perhaps its most legally complex and consequential aspect. There are three distinct layers of EU legal analysis that must be considered: the VAT Directive framework, the fundamental freedoms, and State aid rules.

**Article 401 of the VAT Directive (Council Directive 2006/112/EC)** is the most immediately relevant provision. It states that the EU VAT system shall not prevent any Member State from maintaining or introducing taxes on insurance contracts, betting and gambling, or "taxes of a similar character to those taxes referred to in this Article or, subject to the requirement for the harmonisation of turnover taxes provided for in Article 401, taxes on other transactions, in so far as such taxes do not, because of the way they are calculated or administered, interfere with the free movement of goods or services or have equivalent effect to customs duties."

The operative prohibition is thus not absolute. A turnover-based tax does not automatically violate Article 401. The key distinction, as elaborated in the CJEU's rulings in C-475/03 (Banco Populare di Cremona), C-75/18 (Vodafone Magyarország), and C-323/18 (Tesco), is that a turnover tax is not a VAT because it lacks the defining structural characteristics of VAT: it is not a multi-stage tax applied at each stage of production and distribution, and it does not allow for the deduction of input tax against output tax (IBFD, 2024; Kofler, 2021; Lexxion EU State Aid, 2024).

A universal turnover tax on all transactions, applied at a single uniform rate without input tax deduction, would therefore almost certainly survive Article 401 scrutiny under current CJEU doctrine. The court has drawn a clear line: a tax that lacks the VAT structure—meaning it is levied on gross turnover without credit for inputs—is not a turnover tax "in the sense of the Directive" and does not violate Article 401.

However, the situation becomes more complex under the **fundamental freedoms** of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU). Articles 49 and 63 TFEU, governing the freedom of establishment and the free movement of capital respectively, prohibit restrictions on these freedoms in the context of taxation. A turnover tax that applies uniformly to all transactions, regardless of the legal form or residence of the economic operator, would likely be viewed as non-discriminatory and therefore compatible with these provisions. The Hungarian surtax cases confirmed this: the CJEU found that a turnover-based tax applied uniformly to all operators in a given sector did not constitute a restriction on the freedom of establishment (Lexxion EU State Aid, 2024).

The most significant legal risk lies in **State aid rules** (Article 107 TFEU). If the turnover tax were to include exemptions, reduced rates, or sector-specific thresholds that create differential treatment between comparable economic operators, those differential elements could constitute State aid to the beneficiaries. The European Commission has shown particular sensitivity to turnover-based business taxes that feature selective exemptions or progressive rate structures favouring certain categories of taxpayer. A uniform-rate universal turnover tax with no exemptions would avoid this pitfall, but any attempt to soften the impact on low-margin sectors through reduced rates or thresholds would immediately raise State aid concerns.

The broader constitutional question of EU competence to establish a turnover tax is also relevant. Article 113 TFEU provides the legal basis for harmonising indirect taxes where necessary for the functioning of the internal market. A Member State acting unilaterally to introduce a universal turnover tax does not require EU-level competence, provided the tax complies with the existing legal framework. However, if the turnover tax were structured in a manner that effectively discriminates against cross-border transactions or creates obstacles to intra-EU trade, it could be challenged both by the Commission and by other Member States.

There is a further, more subtle legal risk: a universal turnover tax of sufficient magnitude (say, 2% or above) applied across all sectors of the economy would significantly alter the effective tax burden on imported goods relative to domestically produced goods. If the tax cannot be fully credited or deducted by importers in their home jurisdictions (where VAT is already applied), it could create a de facto barrier to imports, raising questions of compatibility with the non-discrimination principles underlying the TFEU.

### 3.4 Macroeconomic and Structural Consequences

Beyond the immediate legal and sectoral analysis, a universal turnover tax of the magnitude required would generate significant macroeconomic effects that policymakers would need to anticipate and manage.

**Cascading effects and input deductibility.** Unlike VAT, a turnover tax does not permit the deduction of input taxes. This means that each stage of production and distribution effectively pays tax on the full value of inputs it purchases, even though those inputs already bore turnover tax at the previous stage. In a manufacturing economy such as Poland's, where complex supply chains span multiple sectors, the cumulative cascading effect can be substantial. A steel product, for instance, would bear turnover tax at the mining stage, the smelting stage, the fabrication stage, the manufacturing stage, and the retail stage—with each stage taxed on its full revenue rather than its value added. This introduces a systematic distortion that biases economic activity away from complex, multi-stage supply chains and toward vertically integrated structures or simple, direct-to-consumer models.

**Investment incentives and capital formation.** Income taxes, particularly CIT, serve not only as revenue instruments but also as signals that affect investment decisions. A reduction or elimination of CIT, replaced by a turnover tax, would fundamentally alter the calculus for capital-intensive investments. Under a turnover tax, a firm earns the same tax liability whether it invests in capital equipment that increases productivity or maintains its existing asset base. There is no recognition of depreciation, no allowance for retained earnings, and no distinction between productive and unproductive capital. Economic theory strongly predicts that such a system would reduce the after-tax return on capital and thus depress investment, particularly in sectors with long investment horizons such as manufacturing, infrastructure, and energy.

**International competitiveness and FDI.** Poland's attractiveness as a destination for foreign direct investment depends in part on its corporate tax environment. The current CIT rate of 19% (with certain reliefs and allowances available) positions Poland within the mainstream of EU corporate taxation. A universal turnover tax of 2% on gross revenues, while superficially modest, would add to the cost base of all firms operating in Poland without offering any offsetting reduction in CIT (which would be abolished). Multinational enterprises evaluating Poland as a production or service location would need to factor this additional cost into their location decisions, potentially diminishing Poland's competitive position relative to jurisdictions such as Hungary (9% flat corporate tax), Estonia (only distributed profits are taxed), or the Czech Republic (standard 21% CIT, with certain reliefs).

**Labour market effects.** PIT plays a dual role in Poland's tax system: it is a significant revenue instrument and a tool for redistribution. A universal turnover tax would have no equivalent distributional mechanism. The burden of the tax would fall on all economic actors engaged in market transactions, regardless of income level. In practice, low-margin businesses would face disproportionate pressure, as discussed above, potentially leading to employment losses in retail and related sectors. At the same time, the removal of PIT could be argued to increase labour supply incentives by reducing marginal tax rates on workers' earnings. However, this positive labour supply effect would be accompanied by a deterioration in the progressivity of the tax system, potentially increasing income inequality.

**Fiscal fragility.** A turnover tax is inherently more volatile than an income tax when measured against economic cycles. During recessions, both GDP and corporate profits tend to fall, but gross turnover can fall even more sharply because firms may destock, reduce production, or consolidate supply chains. This could amplify pro-cyclical fiscal dynamics, widening deficits during downturns precisely when counter-cyclical fiscal policy is most needed.

## 4. Key Conclusions

The proposal to introduce a universal turnover tax to fully replace PIT and CIT revenues in Poland is analytically coherent from a purely fiscal arithmetic standpoint: the combined PIT and CIT revenue base of approximately PLN 184.2 billion could theoretically be replicated by a turnover tax on total economy-wide transactions at a rate in the range of 1.7% to 2.5%, with a central estimate of approximately 2.0%. However, this arithmetic simplicity masks structural and legal complexities that render the proposal deeply problematic across every dimension of evaluation.

The required turnover tax rate would impose a catastrophic burden on low-margin industries, and the retail sector illustrates this with particular clarity. With net profit margins of 1.5–4% across the dominant retail formats, a 2% turnover tax would consume between 50% and well over 100% of pre-tax profits. Independent grocers, small convenience stores, and SMEs would be most exposed, as they lack the structural advantages of large chain operators to absorb or offset additional tax costs. The result would likely be accelerating consolidation in the retail sector, price increases for consumers, and significant job losses among the lowest-skilled workers—a particularly damaging outcome given Poland's already elevated youth unemployment and the sector's role as a primary employer for entry-level labour.

From the perspective of EU law, a uniform-rate universal turnover tax of approximately 2% would most likely survive scrutiny under Article 401 of the VAT Directive, as it lacks the structural characteristics of VAT (no input tax deduction, no multi-stage application). The CJEU's rulings in the Hungarian cases have effectively opened a pathway for sector-neutral turnover taxes. However, any differentiation in rates, thresholds, or exemptions to protect low-margin sectors would immediately raise concerns under State aid rules and the fundamental freedoms, creating a legal dilemma: the modifications needed to make the tax economically viable for low-margin industries are the very modifications most likely to create legal vulnerability.

The macroeconomic consequences extend beyond the directly affected sectors. Cascading effects would distort production decisions throughout the economy. Investment incentives would be depressed, particularly for capital-intensive industries. The elimination of CIT would reduce Poland's attractiveness as an FDI destination relative to competing jurisdictions. And the removal of PIT, with its progressivity and redistribution mechanisms, would alter the distributional profile of the tax system in a manner that likely increases inequality.

In sum, while a universal turnover tax offers a superficially elegant solution to the administrative complexity of income taxation, it transfers the compliance burden of the income tax system into an economic distortion that is arguably more damaging: the systematic penalisation of low-margin economic activity, the elimination of investment incentives, and the creation of legal exposure under EU law. The reform as proposed—budget-neutral, uniform-rate, across all sectors—is not practically viable without severe economic damage. Any politically or economically feasible version of this concept would require significant differentiation, exemptions, and safeguards that would, in turn, generate their own legal and administrative complications.
