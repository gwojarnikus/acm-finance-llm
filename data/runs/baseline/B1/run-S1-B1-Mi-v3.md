# Introducing a Universal Turnover Tax into the Polish Tax System: A Comprehensive Analysis

## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The core challenge under examination is whether Poland could replace its primary direct taxes—Personal Income Tax (PIT) and Corporate Income Tax (CIT)—with a single, universal turnover tax applied to all business transactions, while preserving the current level of fiscal revenues and without violating European Union legal constraints. This represents a fundamental restructuring of the tax system with profound implications for economic efficiency, sectoral equity, fiscal sustainability, and compliance with supranational law.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### 3.1 The Current Architecture of Poland's Direct Tax System

Poland's personal income tax and corporate income tax constitute foundational pillars of the nation's fiscal architecture, collectively generating revenues that fund essential public services, social welfare programmes, and infrastructure investments. The significance of these two tax instruments extends beyond mere revenue generation; they serve as instruments of horizontal and vertical equity, progressivity, and economic stabilisation.

Personal Income Tax in Poland operates through a progressive rate structure, with rates typically ranging from 12% to 32%, depending on income brackets. In 2024, PIT revenues amounted to approximately PLN 97.6 billion, representing approximately 7.4% of total tax revenues and roughly 2.7% of GDP (Ministry of Finance data via Statista, 2025). The revenue from PIT is shared between the central government budget and local government budgets, with the 2025 local government financing reform significantly increasing the share transferred to municipalities—resulting in a sharp drop in the state budget's PIT portion, while total public sector PIT revenue increased by 13.4% year-on-year.

Corporate Income Tax, set at a standard rate of 19% (with recent sector-specific modifications for the banking sector reaching up to 30% temporarily), generated approximately PLN 70.7 billion in state budget revenues planned for 2025 (Ministry of Finance, State Budget Revenue document). CIT revenues in January-November 2025 reached PLN 58.1 billion, representing a 6.2% increase year-on-year, indicating continued robustness in corporate profitability despite macroeconomic headwinds.

Taken together, PIT and CIT contribute approximately PLN 168.3 billion annually to the public finance system. This figure, however, represents only the direct revenue effect; the broader macroeconomic impact of these taxes encompasses behavioural responses, compliance costs, and their role in shaping investment decisions, labour supply, and consumption patterns.

### 3.2 The Turnover Tax Concept and Its Theoretical Foundations

A universal turnover tax, in its pure form, is a tax levied on the total value of goods sold or services rendered by a business, regardless of profitability. Unlike VAT, which operates through a credit mechanism allowing for input tax deduction and operates at each stage of production and distribution, a turnover tax typically does not provide for the deduction of input costs. It is therefore sometimes referred to as a "cascading tax" because the tax burden accumulates as goods and services pass through successive stages of production and distribution.

The theoretical appeal of a turnover tax lies in its apparent simplicity. Administration would be straightforward, as the tax base is readily measurable and verifiable through sales invoices. Compliance costs could potentially be lower than under a complex income-based system, particularly for small and medium-sized enterprises. The tax would capture economic activity regardless of profit margins, thereby addressing the phenomenon of "zero-tax" entities that generate substantial economic output without taxable profits.

However, the theoretical advantages of turnover taxation are substantially offset by significant economic distortions. Most critically, the cascading effect—wherein the tax paid on inputs cannot be credited against tax collected on outputs—creates a bias toward vertical integration, as businesses that consolidate stages of production avoid the multiple taxation of intermediate transactions. This distorts market structure, encourages monopolisation, and penalises specialisation and the division of labour, which are fundamental drivers of economic efficiency.

Furthermore, a turnover tax is inherently regressive when viewed from the perspective of value added. While it does not discriminate between high-margin and low-margin activities in terms of tax liability, its impact on the final consumer is regressive because the tax burden represents a larger proportion of income for lower-income households who spend a higher share of their earnings on consumption.

### 3.3 Estimating the Required Turnover Tax Rate for Budget Neutrality

To determine the turnover tax rate that would preserve budget neutrality—replacing the revenue currently derived from PIT and CIT—the analysis must establish the appropriate tax base and target revenue level.

**Tax Base Determination:**

The natural tax base for a turnover tax is Gross Domestic Product at market prices, which represents the total value of all goods and services produced within the Polish economy. Using the 2024 nominal GDP figure of approximately PLN 3,617.5 billion provides a reasonable approximation of the annual economic throughput that would be subject to the turnover tax.

**Target Revenue:**

The combined revenue from PIT and CIT in 2024 amounted to approximately PLN 168.3 billion (PLN 97.6 billion + PLN 70.7 billion). This figure represents the minimum revenue target for a budget-neutral replacement.

**Calculation of Required Rate:**

Applying the formula:

> Required Turnover Tax Rate = (PIT + CIT Revenue) / GDP
> Required Turnover Tax Rate = PLN 168.3 billion / PLN 3,617.5 billion
> Required Turnover Tax Rate ≈ 4.65%

This calculation suggests that a universal turnover tax applied at a rate of approximately 4.65% on all business turnover would be required to replicate the current revenue yield from PIT and CIT combined. However, several important caveats must be considered.

First, the above calculation assumes that the tax base is identical to GDP, which may not hold in practice. A turnover tax levied on business transactions could capture both business-to-business and business-to-consumer sales, potentially yielding a broader or narrower base depending on whether final consumption expenditure or total sales is used. If the tax base is narrower than GDP (for example, if certain sectors or transaction types are exempted), the required rate would be proportionally higher.

Second, behavioural responses to the new tax regime would likely reduce the effective tax base. Economic agents would adjust their behaviour in response to the tax change, potentially reducing economic activity, altering transaction structures (such as increasing vertical integration to minimise cascading effects), or shifting to untaxed economic sectors.

Third, the calculation does not account for the interaction with existing VAT. Poland currently operates a VAT system with a standard rate of 23%, along with reduced rates of 8%, 5%, and 0%. If a turnover tax were introduced alongside existing VAT, the combined tax burden on consumption would increase substantially, likely triggering inflationary pressures and reducing household purchasing power.

### 3.4 Differential Impact on Low-Margin Industries: The Retail Trade Sector

The retail trade sector presents a particularly compelling case study for examining the distributional impact of turnover taxation. Poland's retail landscape is characterised by intense price competition, high turnover volumes, and notoriously thin profit margins.

According to industry data, discounters account for approximately 38% of total food retail sales in Poland, with the sector dominated by multinational chains including Biedronka (Jerónimo Martins), Lidl, Auchan, and Carrefour (USDA FAS, 2025). The competitive dynamics of this market exert sustained downward pressure on profit margins. Major retail chains in Poland typically operate on net profit margins ranging from approximately 1% to 3%, with discounters and value-oriented formats generally operating at the lower end of this range.

The introduction of a universal turnover tax at the estimated rate of approximately 4.65% would fundamentally alter the economic calculus for retail businesses. Consider the following illustrative scenario:

A discounter with annual revenues of PLN 1 billion and a net profit margin of 2% currently generates a profit of PLN 20 million, on which it pays CIT at 19%, resulting in a tax liability of approximately PLN 3.8 million. Under a turnover tax regime, the same business would owe PLN 46.5 million (4.65% of PLN 1 billion in revenues), representing an effective increase in tax burden of approximately PLN 42.7 million, or more than eleven times the current CIT liability.

Even accounting for the elimination of PIT (which applies to individual entrepreneurs rather than corporate entities), the cumulative effect would be to transform many retail businesses from profitable enterprises into loss-making operations, or to force substantial price increases that would erode consumer welfare.

The cascading nature of the turnover tax compounds this problem. A retailer purchasing goods from wholesalers would pay the turnover tax on its sales but would also bear a portion of the turnover tax paid by its suppliers embedded in the cost of goods acquired. This embedded tax cannot be recovered, creating a multiple taxation effect that is particularly punishing for businesses with high ratios of purchased inputs to final sales—a characteristic typical of retail operations.

The EuroCommerce response to similar proposals in other EU jurisdictions captures the essential dilemma: "A tax on turnover fundamentally misunderstands a business model which has provided a real benefit to consumers in offering the best products and service at very competitive prices. A tax which wipes out a retailer's already low profit can only lead to two outcomes: either the consumer pays more, or the retailer does not invest or even reduces its presence in the country" (EuroCommerce, 2021).

Polish retail faces additional structural vulnerabilities. The sector has undergone significant consolidation and disruption, with major international players like Tesco exiting the market (closing 36 stores in 2019 alone) and domestic chains like Piotr i Pawel failing entirely. The introduction of a turnover tax would accelerate these competitive pressures, potentially favouring larger integrated operators capable of capturing internal efficiencies, while disadvantaging independent retailers, franchise operations, and smaller format stores.

The distributional consequences extend beyond the retail sector itself. Food retailing is a labour-intensive industry employing a substantial portion of the Polish workforce. A tax-driven contraction in retail profitability could translate into job losses, wage suppression, or degradation of working conditions, disproportionately affecting lower-skilled workers who have fewer alternative employment opportunities.

### 3.5 Compatibility with European Union Law

The compatibility of a universal turnover tax with the acquis communautaire represents perhaps the most formidable legal obstacle to implementation. This analysis examines the relevant provisions of primary and secondary EU law, as well as pertinent jurisprudence from the Court of Justice of the European Union.

**Article 401 of the Principal VAT Directive:**

Council Directive 2006/112/EC, as the cornerstone of EU VAT harmonisation, establishes a comprehensive framework for indirect taxation. Article 401 provides:

> "Without prejudice to other Community provisions, the provisions of this Directive shall not prevent a Member State from maintaining or introducing taxes on insurance contracts, taxes on betting and gambling, excise duties, stamp duties or, more generally, any taxes which do not meet the criteria necessary for the elimination of fiscal frontiers insofar as the collection of such taxes does not, in trade between Member States, give rise to formalities connected with crossing frontiers."

The critical interpretive question is whether a universal turnover tax qualifies as a tax that "does not meet the criteria necessary for the elimination of fiscal frontiers"—i.e., whether it can be characterised as something other than a turnover tax that would be prohibited under this provision.

The CJEU has developed a nuanced analytical framework for this determination. In case C-475/03 (Banco Populare di Cremona), the Court established that the decisive criterion is whether the tax in question exhibits the essential characteristics of VAT: namely, whether it is a general consumption tax applied proportionally to the value of goods or services and levied at each stage of the production and distribution process, with provision for the deduction of input tax.

A universal turnover tax would fail this test in two fundamental respects. First, it would not provide for input tax deduction, meaning that the tax paid on inputs would be embedded in costs without credit. Second, the tax would be levied on turnover rather than on value added, creating the cascading effect described above.

In cases C-75/18 (Vodafone) and C-323/18 (Tesco-Global Áruházak), the CJEU addressed the compatibility of Hungarian sector-specific turnover taxes with Article 401. In Vodafone, the Court upheld the Hungarian turnover-based advertisement tax, finding that it did not constitute a "turnover tax" for the purposes of the VAT Directive because it was not a multi-stage tax applying at all stages of production and distribution. Similarly, in Tesco, the Court upheld the Hungarian special retail tax.

However, the critical distinction between these sector-specific taxes and a universal turnover tax lies in the scope of application. The Hungarian taxes were levied on specific sectors (telecommunications and retail, respectively) and were calibrated to the revenue-raising capacity of those sectors. A universal turnover tax, by contrast, would apply to all economic sectors and all transactions, making it far more likely to be characterised as a general turnover tax that interferes with the harmonised VAT system.

**Primary Law and Fundamental Freedoms:**

Beyond secondary legislation, a universal turnover tax would need to be assessed against the fundamental freedoms guaranteed by the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU), particularly the free movement of goods (Articles 28-37), the freedom of establishment (Articles 49-55), and the freedom to provide services (Articles 56-62).

A turnover tax that applies uniformly to all economic operators, both domestic and foreign, would generally not constitute a violation of these freedoms on grounds of discrimination. However, the cascading effect of such a tax could create de facto disadvantages for businesses engaged in cross-border trade, particularly those involved in complex supply chains spanning multiple Member States. Input taxes paid to foreign suppliers could not be deducted, creating a competitive disadvantage for exporters relative to domestically-integrated competitors.

**State Aid Considerations:**

While a universally-applied turnover tax would not typically constitute state aid within the meaning of Article 107 TFEU, differential treatment of certain sectors or business categories could raise state aid concerns. Poland would need to ensure that any exemptions, reduced rates, or special regimes within the turnover tax system comply with the substantive and procedural requirements of state aid rules.

**The Principle of Fiscal Neutrality:**

The CJEU has consistently held that the harmonised VAT system embodies the principle of fiscal neutrality, which requires that equivalent transactions be treated equivalently for tax purposes. A universal turnover tax that creates distortions between different types of economic activity could be challenged as incompatible with this principle, even if it does not formally constitute a violation of Article 401.

In light of the foregoing, the introduction of a universal turnover tax in Poland would face substantial legal obstacles under EU law. While the sector-specific taxes examined by the CJEU in Vodafone and Tesco were upheld, these decisions turned on their limited scope and sector-specific calibration. A comprehensive turnover tax applying to all economic activity would likely be characterised as incompatible with the harmonised VAT system and could be challenged before the Commission or the CJEU by Member States or affected economic operators.

### 3.6 Broader Economic and Fiscal Considerations

Beyond the immediate questions of revenue adequacy, sectoral impact, and legal compliance, the introduction of a universal turnover tax would raise several broader economic and fiscal considerations warranting examination.

**Interaction with the Existing VAT System:**

Poland's current VAT system generates revenue of approximately PLN 349.5 billion annually (planned for 2025), representing the largest single source of tax revenue. A turnover tax operating alongside VAT would create a dual indirect tax system with complex interaction effects. The combined burden of VAT (at up to 23% on final consumption) and turnover tax (at approximately 4.65% on business transactions) would substantially increase the effective tax rate on consumption, potentially triggering inflation and reducing real household income.

Moreover, the simultaneous operation of two broad-based consumption taxes would create compliance complexity, increase administrative burden, and raise risks of double taxation or unintended gaps in coverage.

**Tax Progressivity and Redistribution:**

Both PIT and CIT contribute to the redistributive function of the tax system. PIT, in particular, embodies the principle of vertical equity, imposing higher effective tax rates on higher-income individuals. A replacement with a turnover tax would fundamentally alter the redistributive character of the tax system, likely increasing regressivity. While turnover taxes capture economic activity regardless of the income level of the ultimate consumer, they do so in a manner that disproportionately burdens lower-income households who spend a higher proportion of their income on consumption.

**Macroeconomic Stability:**

Direct taxes, including PIT and CIT, serve as automatic stabilisers, dampening economic fluctuations by varying tax receipts in response to changes in income and profits. A turnover tax, being levied on transaction volumes rather than profits, would provide less stabilisation effect. During economic downturns, turnover tends to decline less sharply than profits, potentially providing less fiscal cushion. Conversely, during booms, turnover growth may outpace profit growth, creating upward revenue volatility.

**International Tax Competition:**

Poland's corporate tax rate of 19% positions the country within the mainstream of EU corporate taxation, below the euro area average of 22.2% (Trading Economics, 2026). A shift to turnover-based taxation would alter Poland's profile in international tax competition. Foreign direct investment decisions are influenced by the structure and level of taxation, and a turnover tax regime, with its inherent cascading effects, could deter investment in capital-intensive industries while favouring low-margin, high-turnover service activities.

**Implementation and Transition Challenges:**

The transition from an income-based to a turnover-based tax system would entail substantial administrative and economic disruption. Businesses would need to restructure accounting systems, re-evaluate pricing strategies, and adapt supply chain relationships. Tax authorities would need to develop new compliance and enforcement frameworks. The transition period would likely see temporary revenue shortfalls as economic agents adjusted to the new regime.

## 4. Key Conclusions and Decision-Relevant Takeaways

The analysis yields several key findings with direct implications for tax policy decision-making:

**Regarding Revenue Neutrality:**

A universal turnover tax would need to be set at approximately 4.65% of GDP-equivalent turnover to replicate the current combined yield from PIT and CIT (approximately PLN 168.3 billion annually). However, this rate should be understood as a rough approximation, subject to substantial uncertainty arising from behavioural responses, base erosion, and the definition of the tax base.

**Regarding Sectoral Impact:**

The retail trade sector would be disproportionately harmed by turnover taxation. With profit margins typically ranging from 1% to 3%, the effective tax burden on retailers would increase dramatically—potentially by an order of magnitude compared to current CIT liabilities. This would likely result in consumer price increases, reduced investment, consolidation of the sector, and potential job losses, particularly among lower-skilled workers.

**Regarding EU Legal Compatibility:**

A universal turnover tax would face significant legal challenges under EU law, particularly Article 401 of the Principal VAT Directive. While CJEU jurisprudence has upheld sector-specific turnover taxes (as in Vodafone and Tesco), a comprehensive turnover tax applying to all economic activity would likely be characterised as incompatible with the harmonised VAT system. Poland would need to carefully consider the risk of legal challenge and the potential for infringement proceedings before the Commission or the CJEU.

**Regarding Systemic Implications:**

The replacement of direct taxes with a turnover tax would fundamentally alter the character of Poland's tax system, reducing progressivity, increasing regressivity, distorting economic decision-making through cascading effects, and potentially undermining the principles of fiscal neutrality and fair competition embedded in the EU legal order.

**Overall Assessment:**

While a universal turnover tax represents an intellectually interesting theoretical exercise in tax system design, the practical, economic, and legal obstacles to its implementation in Poland are formidable. The estimated required rate of approximately 4.65% would impose catastrophic burdens on low-margin industries such as retail trade, fundamentally altering competitive dynamics and potentially harming consumers through higher prices or reduced product availability. The legal compatibility with EU law is doubtful, particularly with regard to the harmonised VAT framework. Decision-makers considering fundamental tax reform would be well-advised to explore alternatives that preserve the equity and efficiency properties of the current system while addressing identified shortcomings through targeted modifications rather than wholesale replacement.
