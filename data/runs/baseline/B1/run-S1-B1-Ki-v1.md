## 1. Problem Diagnosis

The proposal to replace Poland's Personal Income Tax (PIT) and Corporate Income Tax (CIT) with a universal turnover tax represents a radical structural transformation of the fiscal system, requiring careful quantification of the requisite tax rate, assessment of sectoral economic impacts—particularly on low-margin industries such as retail trade—and verification of compliance with European Union legal frameworks, including State aid rules and the VAT Directive.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### Estimating the Required Turnover Tax Rate

To achieve budget neutrality in replacing PIT and CIT revenues, the universal turnover tax must generate approximately PLN 184.2 billion annually based on 2024 actual collections. The critical variable for rate calculation is the taxable base—defined as the aggregate turnover of all taxable entities in Poland. 

Using Poland's GDP of approximately PLN 3,653 billion as a proxy for total economic turnover (acknowledging that GDP represents value-added rather than gross turnover), we encounter the first fundamental challenge: a turnover tax applies to gross receipts, not net value-added. The relationship between GDP and total turnover depends on the number of production stages and the velocity of transactions in the economy. In a highly intermediated economy like Poland's, where goods pass through manufacturing, wholesale, and retail channels before reaching consumers, aggregate turnover significantly exceeds GDP—potentially by a factor of 2 to 3 times.

However, a more technically sound approach recognizes that a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT would theoretically apply to all business revenues, including those currently captured in the VAT base plus certain VAT-exempt or reduced-rate sectors. Poland's standard VAT rate is 23%, with reduced rates of 8% and 5% applying to essential goods. The VAT base in 2024 generated revenues of PLN 297.9 billion (January-November 2025 data adjusted for annual patterns), implying a total VAT-taxable turnover substantially higher than GDP.

If we assume the turnover tax base broadly aligns with the VAT-taxable base plus additional currently exempt activities, the required rate can be estimated through the following relationship:

$$\text{Turnover Tax Rate} = \frac{\text{Target Revenue}}{\text{Taxable Turnover}} \times 100$$

Given Poland's total tax revenue of approximately PLN 570.8 billion in the state budget for 2025, with PIT and CIT comprising roughly 32% of total tax revenue, the turnover tax would need to capture this share from business activity. If the total taxable turnover is estimated at approximately PLN 2,800-3,000 billion (reflecting intermediate transactions and cross-sector economic activity), the requisite tax rate would fall in the range of **6-7%** on gross turnover.

Critically, this rate calculation assumes no behavioral changes—a heroic assumption contradicted by extensive public finance literature. High turnover taxes create powerful incentives for vertical integration (to eliminate taxable intercompany transactions) and off-book transactions. Historical precedents, including the Hungarian advertisement tax and Polish retail sector tax (both turnover-based), demonstrate that businesses restructure operations to minimize taxable turnover exposure, eroding the tax base and necessitating higher rates to maintain revenue neutrality. The effective rate required to fully compensate for PIT and CIT losses would likely exceed 8-10% when accounting for compliance responses and base erosion.

### Sectoral Impact Analysis: The Retail Trade Vulnerability

The retail sector in Poland presents a paradigmatic case study of vulnerability to turnover taxation. According to financial reports from leading market participants, net profit margins in Polish grocery retail are exceptionally thin—Biedronka operates at approximately 2% net margin, while Dino Polska achieves 5-6% and Żabka Group approximately 2.2%. EBITDA margins, which better reflect operating profitability before financing and tax effects, range from 7.7% (Biedronka, 2024) to 7.9% (Dino Polska, 2024) and 12.8% (Żabka, 2024).

The economic mechanics of turnover taxation create severe distortionary effects on low-margin, high-volume businesses. Consider a hypothetical supermarket with annual turnover of PLN 100 million and a 3% net profit margin (PLN 3 million profit). A 6% turnover tax would levy PLN 6 million in tax—double the entity's entire profit. The business faces three unpalatable alternatives: absorb losses and eventually fail, reduce costs through employment cuts or supplier pressure, or pass costs to consumers through price increases.

The compounding effect distinguishes turnover taxes from profit taxes in their impact on profitability. CIT applies only to net income; a company reporting zero profit pays zero CIT. A turnover tax applies indiscriminately to gross receipts regardless of profitability. For retailers operating in competitive markets with price-sensitive consumers—characteristics that define Polish grocery retail where discounters captured 37% market share in 2023 and price promotions drive purchasing decisions—the ability to pass tax costs forward is severely constrained. The sector already experienced margin compression in 2024 due to deflationary pressure and wage increases, with Dino's EBITDA margin declining 80 basis points year-over-year and Biedronka's falling 80 basis points.

The structural implications extend beyond immediate profitability. Polish retail trade accounts for approximately 13% of GDP and 10.2% of total employment (2024 data). A turnover tax that selectively burdens this sector—due to its high turnover-to-profit ratio—would generate significant deadweight losses through employment reduction, store closures, and reduced capital investment. The USDA Foreign Agricultural Service noted that multinational retailers in Poland already face "slim profit margins due to fierce competition," with Tesco closing 36 stores in 2019 following difficult trading conditions. Adding a substantial turnover tax burden on top of existing VAT (23% standard rate), excise duties where applicable, and social security contributions would render many retail operations economically unviable.

Furthermore, the tax would distort competitive dynamics among retail formats. Large-format hypermarkets and supermarkets with higher inventory turnover but lower per-unit margins would face disproportionate burdens compared to specialty retailers or vertically integrated producers. This could accelerate the ongoing shift toward convenience formats and discount channels, potentially reducing consumer welfare through diminished product variety and reduced geographical accessibility for rural populations.

### EU Legal Compatibility Assessment

The proposed universal turnover tax must satisfy multiple tests under European Union law: (1) compatibility with fundamental freedoms (free movement of goods, services, capital, and freedom of establishment), (2) non-violation of State aid prohibitions under Article 107 TFEU, and (3) compliance with the VAT Directive, specifically Article 401 concerning taxes that may interfere with the harmonized VAT system.

The Court of Justice of the European Union has adjudicated turnover tax cases in *Commission v. Poland* (C-562/19 P) and *Commission v. Hungary* (C-596/19 P), providing important jurisprudential guidance. In these cases, the CJEU ruled that progressive turnover taxes—the Polish retail sector tax and Hungarian advertisement tax—do not automatically constitute State aid merely because they impose higher effective rates on larger turnovers. The Court established that taxation is selective only if it constitutes a "derogation from" the reference system, and that progressive rate structures can be inherent features of the tax design rather than discriminatory deviations.

However, these rulings addressed sector-specific turnover taxes rather than universal turnover taxes replacing major direct taxes. A universal turnover tax covering all economic activity would face distinct legal challenges:

**Compatibility with Fundamental Freedoms:** Any turnover tax must apply without discrimination based on the origin of goods or services or the nationality of providers. A uniform rate applying to all turnover would likely satisfy non-discrimination requirements, provided no exemptions or reduced rates disproportionately benefit domestic entities.

**State Aid Assessment:** The critical vulnerability lies in potential selective advantage. If the tax system includes thresholds, exemptions for small enterprises, or rate progressivity, these features could be challenged as conferring selective advantages. The CJEU jurisprudence indicates that progressive taxation based on turnover is permissible as part of the tax's inherent design, but explicit exclusions for particular sectors or company sizes may trigger State aid scrutiny.

**Article 401 VAT Directive Compatibility:** Article 401 of Directive 2006/112/EC provides that Member States may not introduce "taxes, duties, or charges which meet the cumulative criteria of being imposed on the same taxable base as VAT, being collected at stages prior to the final consumer, and being charged at each stage of the production or distribution process." A universal turnover tax levied at each transaction stage would likely satisfy these criteria—applied to turnover (the VAT base), collected throughout the production chain, and charged at each stage—thereby constituting an impermissible interference with the harmonized VAT system unless structured to avoid such cumulation.

Scholarly analysis from the IBFD (2024) specifically examines this tension, concluding that turnover taxes compatible with Article 401 must either: (a) be structured as final taxes on specific transactions rather than cumulative multistage taxes, (b) apply exclusively to sectors outside VAT scope, or (c) constitute excise duties on specific products rather than general turnover taxes. A universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT that operates through the business production chain would likely violate Article 401 unless designed as a single-stage tax on final sales to consumers—an arrangement that would dramatically reduce the tax base and necessitate prohibitively high rates.

**WTO Considerations:** While not explicitly requested, the tax would also need to satisfy World Trade Organization obligations, specifically the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT) Article III national treatment principle and GATT Article II tariff commitments. Taxes on imported goods must not exceed those on domestically produced like products, and border-adjusted turnover taxes could be challenged as disguised tariffs.

### Macroeconomic and Structural Implications

Beyond sectoral impacts and legal compliance, the macroeconomic implications of replacing PIT and CIT with a turnover tax warrant consideration. The current Polish tax system generates automatic stabilizers through PIT—during economic downturns, profits and high incomes decline, reducing tax revenue and providing cushion to affected taxpayers. A turnover tax would eliminate this stabilizer; businesses would owe tax based on gross receipts even when operating at a loss or during recessionary periods when turnover may actually increase (as businesses discount inventory to generate cash).

The potential for tax cascading—pyramiding of tax on tax through multiple production stages—represents another structural concern. Each transaction in the supply chain would bear the full turnover tax rate. A product moving from raw material supplier (turnover tax applied) to manufacturer (turnover tax applied on value including embedded tax) to wholesaler (further tax application) to retailer (final tax application) could accumulate effective tax rates of 20-30% or higher on the final consumer price, even with a nominal 6-7% rate at each stage. This creates horizontal inequity—vertically integrated firms paying tax only once on their final turnover would face lower effective tax burdens than firms utilizing external suppliers.

## 4. Recommended Strategy or Key Conclusions

Considering the empirical data, legal constraints, and structural economic implications, the following conclusions emerge regarding the feasibility and desirability of a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT revenues in Poland:

**Key Findings:**

1. **Revenue-Neutral Rate Estimation:** To replace approximately PLN 184.2 billion in PIT and CIT revenues, a universal turnover tax would require a rate of **6-7%** on gross turnover assuming no behavioral responses. Accounting for anticipated compliance responses, base erosion through vertical integration, and economic contraction in affected sectors, the effective rate would likely need to exceed **10%** to maintain revenue neutrality—rendering the tax economically destructive for sectors with margins below this threshold.

2. **Retail Sector Crisis Risk:** Poland's retail trade sector, characterized by net margins of 2-6% and accounting for 13% of GDP and over 10% of employment, would face existential threat from turnover taxes above 5-6%. Major retailers including Biedronka and Dino Polska would see tax burdens exceeding total profitability, forcing restructuring, price increases, or market exits—with consequent impacts on consumer welfare, employment, and food security.

3. **EU Law Barriers:** While CJEU jurisprudence permits progressive turnover taxes, a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT would face substantial risk of incompatibility with Article 401 of the VAT Directive, which prohibits cumulative multi-stage taxes that interfere with the harmonized VAT system. The tax would likely require redesign as a final-stage consumption tax rather than a business turnover tax—or risk Commission challenge and suspension.

4. **Structural Economic Distortions:** The tax would incentivize vertical integration to eliminate taxable intercompany transactions, create tax pyramiding through supply chains, eliminate automatic fiscal stabilizers, and distort competition between integrated and non-integrated firms and between high-margin and low-margin sectors.

**Decision-Relevant Takeaways:**

Given these findings, the introduction of a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT in Poland appears **inadvisable** under current economic and legal conditions. If Polish policymakers seek tax system simplification and reduced compliance costs, alternative reforms—such as consolidating PIT and CIT into a unified business income tax, expanding the VAT base while reducing rates, or implementing a modified gross receipts tax with credits for taxed inputs—would achieve efficiency gains without the severe sectoral distortions and legal vulnerabilities inherent in a pure turnover tax.

Should the government proceed despite these concerns, the following mitigation strategies would be essential: (a) establishing very low rates (3-4% maximum) combined with comprehensive base expansion including currently exempt sectors, (b) providing tax credits or deductions for taxed inputs to prevent pyramiding and reduce effective rates on intermediated goods, (c) incorporating profit calculations for loss-making periods to prevent taxing unprofitable businesses, and (d) securing advance clearance from the European Commission regarding State aid and VAT Directive compatibility. Even with these modifications, the fundamental mismatch between turnover taxation and low-margin business models in Poland's retail-intensive economy would generate significant efficiency losses that warrant alternative reform pathways.
