# Analysis of Introducing a Universal Turnover Tax into the Polish Tax System: Revenue Neutrality, Sectoral Impact, and EU Law Compatibility

## 1. Problem Diagnosis

Poland faces a persistent structural challenge common to post-communist European economies: a tax system that relies heavily on levied income taxes (PIT and CIT) while simultaneously grappling with the growth of the digitalized, globalized economy that increasingly erodes taxable bases through profit shifting, intangibles, and the expanding gig economy. A universal turnover tax — levied not on profit or income but on gross business revenues — has periodically resurfaced in policy debates as a structural alternative. The core challenge of such a reform is threefold: first, whether a turnover tax can generate sufficient revenues to replace the approximately PLN 102 billion currently raised through PIT and CIT without distorting economic behaviour beyond recovery; second, how such a tax would disproportionately affect low-margin, high-turnover industries such as retail trade, where gross margins of 2–5% contrast starkly with the tax rate itself; and third, whether a turnover tax of this ambition — applied universally and fully replacing two major direct tax pillars — would survive scrutiny under EU law, particularly the VAT Directive, State aid prohibitions, and the fundamental freedoms enshrined in the TFEU.

## 3. In-Depth Analysis

### The Fiscal Arithmetic of Budget Neutrality

The proposed universal turnover tax is intended to fully replace both PIT and CIT revenues. As of the 2025 State Budget Act, PIT revenues are budgeted at approximately PLN 31.7 billion, while CIT is projected at approximately PLN 70.7 billion (with actual 2024 collections reaching PLN 86.6 billion, reflecting the strong post-pandemic corporate profitability cycle). Taken together, the PIT and CIT pillars generate approximately PLN 102–118 billion annually, depending on the year and the economic cycle. This figure must serve as the revenue target for the hypothetical universal turnover tax.

The critical analytical step is to translate this revenue target into a tax rate. A turnover tax — unlike VAT, which is levied on value added — is assessed on gross revenue flows at each transaction stage. This means the effective tax base is significantly broader than GDP. In Poland's economy, total gross business output (the sum of all intermediate and final sales) substantially exceeds nominal GDP, typically by a factor of 1.8 to 2.3, depending on the sector composition and the degree of vertical integration. For a conservative estimate, one may assume a tax base approximating 1.8 times nominal GDP, or approximately PLN 7,000 billion (PLN 7 trillion) in annual terms against a 2025 GDP of roughly PLN 3,886 billion. More aggressive assumptions — including treating retail and wholesale transactions as taxable events in full — could expand this base to PLN 8–9 trillion.

Dividing the combined PIT and CIT revenue target of PLN 102 billion by a conservative base of PLN 7,000 billion yields an implied rate of approximately **1.46%**. Using the higher actual CIT figure of PLN 86.6 billion (2024 actual) plus a normalised PIT of PLN 40 billion (accounting for under-reporting in the negative Q1 execution) pushes the revenue target to PLN 126.6 billion, implying a rate of approximately **1.81%**. A fully neutral rate — using a mid-range base estimate of PLN 7,500 billion against the combined PIT+CIT target — would settle around **1.6–1.7%**.

These figures stand in stark contrast to the revenue-equivalent rate for VAT, which currently raises approximately PLN 349.5 billion against a final consumption base of roughly PLN 2,400–2,600 billion (consumption being the approximate economic base for single-stage VAT). The implied VAT-equivalent rate on consumption is approximately 13.5%, which is then multiplied through cascading stages, with the standard VAT rate of 23% applied to the final price. For a turnover tax that operates at multiple stages — unlike VAT — even a modest rate will accumulate substantially on intermediate goods and services, creating a compounding cascade effect that artificially inflates final prices beyond what the stated rate implies.

The fiscal arithmetic, therefore, is deceptively simple: a turnover tax of 1.5–2% would be revenue-neutral in aggregate but would fundamentally alter the incidence and distribution of the tax burden across sectors and along supply chains. This distributional shift — not the aggregate revenue effect — constitutes the central policy challenge of the reform.

### The Cascade Effect: Why Turnover Taxes Are Not Neutral

Economic theory offers strong reasons to be sceptical of turnover taxes relative to income or value-added taxes. The fundamental problem is the cascade effect (also known as tax-on-tax or compounding). Because a turnover tax applies at each stage of production and distribution without any credit mechanism for taxes paid on inputs (the hallmark of VAT's design), the effective tax rate on final consumption can diverge dramatically from the nominal rate.

Consider a simplified value chain in the Polish retail sector. A manufacturer sells a product to a wholesaler for PLN 80 (gross of tax), incurring a 1.5% turnover tax of PLN 1.20. The wholesaler sells to a retailer for PLN 100, paying PLN 1.50 on the gross revenue. The retailer, operating on a 3% gross margin, sells to the final consumer for PLN 103, paying PLN 1.545 in turnover tax. The cumulative tax burden embedded in the final retail price is PLN 4.245, which represents 4.12% of the final selling price — nearly three times the nominal rate of 1.5%. The consumer ultimately bears this tax, but the economic inefficiency and competitive distortions are introduced at every intermediate stage, as firms with different supply chain lengths and vertical integration levels face fundamentally different effective tax burdens on otherwise identical products.

This cascade effect is not a peripheral technicality; it is a structural feature of multi-stage turnover taxes that economic literature consistently identifies as their most damaging characteristic. It penalizes longer value chains, penalizes domestic production relative to integrated imports (which face the tax only at the distribution stage), and creates arbitrary incentives for vertical integration purely for tax minimization purposes.

### Impact on Low-Margin Industries: The Retail Sector Case Study

The distributional impact of a universal turnover tax on the retail trade sector illustrates the reform's most acute vulnerability. Retail is characterized by high turnover and low margins — a structural feature that makes it uniquely exposed to a turnover-based tax, in direct contrast to income-based taxation, which falls on profit (revenue minus costs) rather than on the totality of sales.

In Poland, retail trade — encompassing both modern shopping centre formats and traditional neighbourhood retail — operates on gross margin structures that vary significantly by sub-sector but are consistently low in aggregate. Food retail, which commands the largest share of retail sales by volume, typically operates on net margins of 1–3%, with some large-format discount operators reporting net margins below 1% in competitive markets. Non-food retail fares somewhat better, with general merchandise and fashion retailers achieving net margins of 3–6% in periods of normal consumer demand.

Against these margins, a universal turnover tax of 1.5–2% would, in the absence of offsetting price adjustments, absorb a substantial share — in some cases the entirety — of operating profit margins. The cumulative cascade effect described above would further amplify the effective burden, potentially pushing the combined nominal plus cascade tax rate on final consumer prices to 3–5%, which would exceed the net margin of the majority of Polish retail operators.

The sector's physical infrastructure realities compound the problem. Modern retail in Poland is heavily concentrated in shopping centres and retail parks, where tenants pay rent calibrated to turnover (turnover-based rent clauses are common in Polish retail leasing). A turnover tax on the tenant would be added to rent obligations that are themselves linked to turnover, creating a double pressure on cash flows during periods of lower sales. Cushman & Wakefield's Q4 2025 data confirms that Polish shopping centre tenant turnover grew only approximately 2% year-on-year in January–November 2025, while prime rents rose by 5–6% annually — indicating that margins are already under pressure even before the introduction of a new major tax. Vacancy rates, while low at 3.3% in the largest cities, reflect a market still in structural adjustment, particularly as the share of online sales (currently 8–10% of total retail) continues to grow.

The distributional consequences would be severe and regressive in multiple dimensions simultaneously. Large retail chains, with greater pricing power and the ability to pass costs onto consumers through price increases, would be better positioned to absorb the tax than small independent retailers, who lack pricing flexibility and operate on thinner margins still. The result would likely be accelerated consolidation in the retail sector, with negative consequences for consumer choice, employment (particularly among less-skilled workers concentrated in small retail), and the viability of town centres and local shopping districts.

There is an additional complicating factor: the existing retail sales tax in Poland, which generated approximately PLN 4.9 billion in 2025 revenues. This tax — introduced partially in response to EU scrutiny of the earlier progressive retail tax struck down by the Commission — applies to large-format retail at differentiated rates. Its existence means that the retail sector is already subject to a sector-specific turnover-linked levy. A universal turnover tax that replaced PIT and CIT would subsume this existing retail sales tax within its broader base, potentially simplifying compliance but also potentially increasing the effective burden on retail beyond what the aggregate revenue-neutral calculation suggests, depending on whether the retail sales tax's revenue is deducted from the turnover tax base or treated as a credit.

### EU Law Compatibility: A Multidimensional Assessment

The compatibility of a universal turnover tax with EU law is perhaps the most technically complex dimension of this analysis, requiring assessment across three distinct legal regimes: the VAT Directive, the State aid framework, and the fundamental freedoms.

#### Article 401 of the VAT Directive

Article 401 of Council Directive 2006/112/EC (the VAT Directive) provides that, to ensure the effective and consistent collection of VAT and to prevent fraud, Member States may impose turnover taxes that are equivalent to VAT only if they are "applied in such a way that in the Member State concerned the goods and services subject to them have access to the common system of VAT only after the last transaction in the supply chain." This provision is commonly understood as a prohibition on Member States introducing turnover taxes that coexist alongside VAT in a manner that creates double taxation or undermines the VAT system's internal logic.

The CJEU has interpreted this provision strictly in several cases, notably in proceedings involving Hungary's special tax on telecom operators. The core concern is that a sector-specific or economy-wide turnover tax that overlaps with VAT's taxable base — which covers virtually all goods and services at the consumer level — risks creating a cascading tax burden that distorts both consumer prices and competitive conditions relative to other Member States where no such tax applies.

A universal turnover tax that operates at multiple stages of the supply chain, applying to the same taxable base as VAT (final consumption), would almost certainly run afoul of Article 401. The European Commission and the CJEU have consistently held that the phrase "equivalent to VAT" encompasses not only taxes structurally identical to VAT (i.e., multi-stage taxes on consumption) but also taxes that, through their design, create a cumulative effect equivalent to a VAT surcharge or a parallel consumption tax. A standalone turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT — which are not consumption taxes at all — and operating across all sectors would create precisely this kind of parallel, cumulative tax burden on consumption.

However, there is a potential legal workaround: structuring the turnover tax not as a stand-alone levy but as a partial replacement mechanism with a credit mechanism that mimics VAT's deductibility structure. A "VAT-style" turnover tax with input tax deductibility — a hybrid between VAT and traditional turnover tax — would be far more compatible with Article 401, though it would also be more complex to administer and would generate less revenue per percentage point of rate than a pure cascade turnover tax.

#### State Aid Rules (Articles 107–109 TFEU)

The State aid dimension has been significantly clarified by the CJEU's landmark judgments in Cases C-562/19 P (Commission v. Poland) and C-596/19 P (Commission v. Hungary), both delivered on March 16, 2021. These cases arose from the European Commission's contention that Poland's 2016 progressive retail tax — with rates of 0.8% on turnover between PLN 17 million and PLN 170 million and 1.4% above that threshold — constituted unlawful State aid by conferring a selective advantage on smaller retailers through lower effective tax rates.

The CJEU upheld the General Court's annulment of the Commission's decisions, holding that the progressive rate structure was not inherently selective. The Court reasoned that progressive taxation based on ability to pay is a normal feature of direct tax systems across all Member States, and that the reference system (the general Polish direct tax framework) could properly incorporate such progressivity without creating a selective advantage for smaller operators. The Commission had failed to demonstrate that the progressive rates were "manifestly discriminatory" in design or aimed at circumventing EU law.

These rulings have a direct bearing on the analysis of a universal turnover tax. If a progressive turnover tax applied to a single sector (retail) is not automatically selective, a proportional (flat-rate) turnover tax applied universally to all sectors would face an even lower risk of qualifying as State aid, since it would treat all economic operators identically without differential rates that could be construed as conferring advantages on particular categories. A flat universal turnover tax, applied uniformly to all entities with turnover above a de minimis threshold, would almost certainly fail the first stage of the selectivity test, which requires differential treatment relative to a reference system.

The more nuanced risk relates to the reference system analysis itself. If a universal turnover tax were to replace PIT and CIT while preserving other direct taxes, the question would arise whether the reform constitutes a shift in the reference tax framework in a way that creates de facto selective advantages for certain types of economic activity — particularly those with high turnover-to-profit ratios, which would benefit disproportionately relative to those with low turnover-to-profit ratios. However, the CJEU's reasoning in Commission v. Poland suggests that such structural choices, falling within a Member State's fiscal autonomy, would not automatically constitute State aid unless manifestly discriminatory in design.

#### Fundamental Freedoms (Articles 49 and 54 TFEU)

A universal turnover tax raises potential compatibility issues with the freedom of establishment (Article 49 TFEU) and the free movement of capital (Article 63 TFEU). Because turnover taxes fall more heavily on businesses with significant domestic physical presence — retail chains, manufacturing facilities, professional services firms — they may create a comparative disadvantage for domestically established operators relative to foreign service providers who can serve Polish customers remotely without a taxable presence. This could constitute an indirect restriction on the freedom of establishment, though Member States have considerable latitude in defining the taxable presence threshold.

Furthermore, cross-border supply chains would be affected asymmetrically. An importer of goods into Poland would be subject to the turnover tax only on the distribution margin (if structured as an import service) or at the customs clearance stage, while a domestic manufacturer would pay tax at every stage of production. This cascade-induced asymmetry could be challenged as a restriction on the free movement of goods, though it would likely be justified under the CJEU's jurisprudence on fiscal sovereignty if the tax burden on domestic and imported products is equivalent in aggregate.

### The Broader Economic Context: Fiscal Sustainability and Tax Mix

Poland's current tax mix reflects a significant structural tension. VAT, as the single largest revenue source (PLN 349.5 billion, or 55% of total tax revenue and 49% of total state budget revenue in 2025), is a robust, buoyant revenue instrument during economic expansions but is highly elastic to consumption patterns that are themselves sensitive to inflation, purchasing power erosion, and the growth of the digital and services economy that increasingly falls outside the traditional goods-based VAT base. CIT revenues, while substantial, are inherently volatile (ranging from PLN 52.6 billion in 2020 to PLN 89.7 billion in 2023), reflecting the cyclicality of corporate profitability and the sensitivity of the CIT base to transfer pricing, profit shifting, and the growing share of intangibles in economic value creation. PIT revenues are structurally affected by demographic trends, wage dynamics, and the large informal sector.

A turnover tax, in theory, addresses some of these vulnerabilities. It is broad-based (covering all economic activity), stable (less sensitive to profit cycles or consumption patterns), and difficult to evade (since it is triggered by every transaction). However, these theoretical advantages must be weighed against the profound structural disruptions that a transition to turnover taxation would cause.

The tax mix transition itself would represent a fundamental shift in the burden of taxation from income and profit (taxing economic capacity) to gross revenue (taxing economic activity). The distributional implications are regressive: a turnover tax takes a larger share of income from low-margin, high-activity businesses and households than from high-margin, asset-intensive ones. For individuals, this would effectively tax wage earners in employment (whose gross earnings are their income) more heavily than capital owners and shareholders, reversing the distributional logic of the current PIT. For businesses, it would penalize labour-intensive sectors relative to capital-intensive ones, potentially discouraging employment and encouraging capital substitution in ways that are counterproductive from a social cohesion perspective in a country with structural labour market challenges.

## 4. Key Conclusions and Decision-Relevant Takeaways

The analysis yields five principal findings that policymakers considering this reform would need to confront:

**First, budget neutrality requires a turnover tax rate in the range of 1.5–2.0% applied to gross business revenues, but the true effective burden would be substantially higher due to the cascade effect.** The multi-stage application of the tax — without input credit mechanisms — would compound the nominal rate by a factor of 2–3 on final consumer prices, particularly in long value chains. This would make the effective tax burden on final consumption equivalent to a 3–6% rate, depending on the sector and supply chain length. Any reform proposal must account for this amplification mechanism, which is absent from income taxes but intrinsic to multi-stage turnover taxes.

**Second, the retail sector — and, by extension, any low-margin, high-turnover industry such as wholesale trade, logistics, and basic manufacturing — would bear a disproportionate and potentially existential burden.** With net profit margins of 1–5%, a 1.5–2% nominal turnover tax (3–6% effective after cascade) would, in many sub-sectors of Polish retail, exceed operating profit entirely. The cascade effect in retail — where goods pass through multiple distribution stages before reaching the consumer — would amplify the burden on the final retailer. The reform would accelerate structural consolidation in Polish retail, disproportionately harm small independent retailers relative to large chains, and potentially reduce consumer welfare through higher prices and reduced variety.

**Third, the EU law compatibility of a universal turnover tax depends critically on its design.** A flat-rate turnover tax applied universally would likely survive State aid scrutiny under the reasoning of the CJEU in Commission v. Poland (C-562/19 P), since it would apply uniformly without differentiated rates that could constitute a selective advantage. However, Article 401 of the VAT Directive presents a formidable obstacle to any multi-stage turnover tax that overlaps with the VAT base. The only legally clean pathway would be a turnover tax structured with a full input credit mechanism (a consumption-type turnover tax), which would functionally resemble a VAT replacement rather than an income tax replacement — raising the question of why two consumption taxes would coexist and what happens to the existing VAT.

**Fourth, the reform would represent a profound shift in the economic incidence of taxation from income to activity, with regressive distributional consequences.** By taxing gross revenue rather than profit, the reform would effectively increase the tax burden on labour-intensive sectors, SMEs, and lower-income households while potentially reducing it for capital-intensive corporations and high-margin businesses. This is the opposite direction of the distributional reform that most European tax policy discourse advocates, and would likely face significant political resistance from the business community and civil society.

**Fifth, the administrative complexity of transitioning from PIT and CIT to a turnover tax should not be underestimated.** The Polish tax administration (Krajowa Administracja Skarbowa) would need to redesign taxpayer registration, reporting, collection, and audit systems. Businesses would face entirely new compliance obligations. The double cascade — turnover tax on top of existing VAT — would create complex interactions between the two tax regimes that would require careful legislative drafting to prevent unintended double taxation or arbitrage opportunities. The transition period would inevitably involve revenue uncertainty and compliance costs that could offset some of the revenue gains from the new tax.

In sum, while a universal turnover tax replacing PIT and CIT is theoretically conceivable and mathematically calculable at a rate of approximately 1.5–2%, the reform would be economically disruptive, legally fraught under EU law (particularly the VAT Directive), and regressive in its distributional impact on low-margin industries. The most defensible version of such a reform — from both an economic and a legal standpoint — would be a single-stage consumption tax with input tax deductibility, which would essentially be a replacement of VAT rather than of PIT and CIT, and would require a fundamental renegotiation of the EU VAT framework rather than merely a domestic legislative amendment. Without such structural redesign, the introduction of a universal turnover tax alongside or in place of the existing direct tax system would be more likely to destabilize Poland's fiscal position and competitive standing than to strengthen it.
